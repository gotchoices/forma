# Q78: How does a biological cell access the neutrino sheet?

**Status:** Open — two candidate mechanisms, one preferred
**Source:** Brainstorm session on neutrino-domain storage mobility
**Related:** [storage-in-t6](../papers/storage-in-t6.md) (parent
storage paper), [neutrino-domain-storage](../papers/neutrino-domain-storage.md)
(architecture paper), R26 F52–F67 (neutron as cross-plane mode),
R26 F64–F75 (T⁶ metric and cross-shear coupling)

## The problem

The [storage-in-t6](../papers/storage-in-t6.md) paper proposes
that compact-dimension modes on the neutrino T² (T²_ν) could
function as an information storage medium at the cellular scale.
Three numerical coincidences — spatial scale, energy scale,
frequency — connect T²_ν to the biological cell.

But the paper leaves a critical question open: **how does the
stored information move with the organism?**

If the compact dimensions are a fixed geometric feature of
spacetime, then a stored pattern at location A is a property of
spacetime at A.  Walk to location B and the pattern stays at A.
The organism arrives at B and finds whatever was already stored
there (presumably nothing).  This is fatal to the hypothesis
unless there is a mechanism for the stored state to travel with
the biology.

Two candidate mechanisms were identified.  Both start from the
same premise: in the T⁶ model, particles ARE compact-dimension
excitations, and particles move through space without leaving
their properties behind.  Whatever mechanism lets the electron
carry its mass, charge, and spin through R³ should also let a
compact-dimension storage excitation travel.  The two theories
differ on what provides the specific coupling gateway between
ordinary matter and T²_ν.

## Theory A: cells create their own neutrino ("funded neutrino")

### The idea

In the T⁶ model, particles are created by funding their
fundamental resonance mode.  Pair production (γ → e⁺e⁻) is a
photon exciting the (1,2) and (−1,2) modes on the electron T².
By analogy, a cell could create a neutrino by funding the
fundamental mode on T²_ν.

The three fundable neutrino modes (Assignment A):

| Mode | Mass | Frequency | Wavelength | Band |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ν₁ = (1,1) | 29.2 meV | 7.06 THz | 42.4 μm | Far-IR |
| ν₂ = (−1,1) | 30.5 meV | 7.37 THz | 40.7 μm | Far-IR |
| ν₃ = (1,2) | 58.2 meV | 14.07 THz | 21.3 μm | Mid-IR |

All are in the infrared, within the frequency range of molecular
vibrations in biological tissue.

Under the threshold model, energy accumulates continuously via
weak evanescent coupling.  A cell membrane firing thousands of
ion transits per second, each dripping a fraction of its energy
into T²_ν modes, could fund a neutrino–antineutrino pair over
some accumulation timescale.

### The co-particle concept

Once funded, the neutrino would be geometrically coupled to the
source that created it — the cell's electromagnetic field.  The
analogy is the neutron: the neutron (mode 1,2,0,0,1,2) binds
electron-T² and proton-T² excitations into a single cross-plane
mode via cross-shear coupling.  The binding is geometric, not
force-mediated.

Similarly, a funded neutrino localized at a cell would form a
"macro-particle" with the cell's atoms: the cell's electrons
(on the electron T²), protons and neutrons (on the proton T²),
plus the funded neutrino (on T²_ν), all linked by cross-shear
couplings into a single multi-sheet state in T⁶.  The whole
thing moves through R³ as a unit because it IS a unit.

The neutrino "wraps" the cell's matter, providing a direct,
always-present coupling channel to T²_ν for read/write
operations.

### Cell division as neutrino fission

A speculative extension: the cell's metabolic activity
continuously feeds energy into T²_ν modes through the funded
neutrino.  Energy accumulates in higher harmonics.  Nonlinear
mode coupling cascades energy toward the fundamental.  At some
critical total energy, the single-cavity state becomes unstable
— a single spatial wavepacket can no longer contain the energy
— and it bifurcates into two spatially separated wavepackets.
Each daughter inherits part of the mode distribution.  The cell's
R³ structure fractures sympathetically.

Predictions of the fission model:
- Cell division correlates with total energy stored in compact
  modes (testable via THz spectroscopy of pre-mitotic cells)
- The division threshold is an energy, not a clock — cells
  divide when they've accumulated enough, not after fixed time
  (consistent with observed cell-cycle variability)
- Rapidly dividing cells have more depolarized membranes, thus
  pump more energy into compact modes (consistent with Levin's
  observation that cancer cells and stem cells are depolarized)

### The pair-production problem

**This is the central difficulty with Theory A.**

Creating a neutrino from electromagnetic fields requires
creating a ν + ν̄ pair (conservation of lepton number).  The
minimum energy for the lightest pair is 2 × 29.2 meV ≈ 58 meV
— within the energy budget of a single ion transit across a
cell membrane (~70 meV for a potassium ion at −70 mV).

However, the creation mechanism is problematic:

1. **In the Standard Model**, ν-ν̄ pairs are created via Z boson
   exchange (a weak interaction).  The weak coupling constant at
   cellular energies (~meV) is extraordinarily small.  The rate
   of neutrino pair creation by thermal/electromagnetic processes
   at meV energies is negligible — unmeasurably rare.

2. **In the T⁶ model**, the coupling between ordinary EM fields
   and T²_ν is mediated by cross-shear parameters (σ_eν, σ_νp).
   R26 F72 showed that even σ_eν = 0.05 distorts the neutrino
   mass ratio by 4%.  The coupling is small by necessity
   (otherwise it ruins the neutrino mass predictions).  Whether
   this coupling is strong enough to fund a neutrino over
   biological timescales (~hours to days) has not been computed.

3. **The threshold model** might help: if energy accumulation is
   continuous, even very weak coupling could fund the pair given
   enough time.  But "enough time" could be geological, not
   biological.

4. **The antineutrino destination** is unclear.  If a ν-ν̄ pair
   is created, where does the antineutrino go?  If it remains
   localized with the neutrino, the pair is a boson (spin 0 or 1),
   not a fermion — potentially changing the storage
   characteristics.  If it escapes, conservation is satisfied
   but the mechanism requires a persistent asymmetry (one ν stays,
   one ν̄ leaves) that has no obvious driver.

### Status of Theory A

Creative and internally consistent as a narrative, but the
pair-production problem is severe.  The required coupling
strength, the creation rate at biological energies, and the
fate of the antineutrino are all unresolved.  The cell-division
prediction is testable and interesting regardless of the
mechanism.

## Theory B: neutrons as gateways ("built-in access")

### The idea

Nearly every atom heavier than hydrogen contains neutrons.  In
the T⁶ model, the neutron is a cross-plane mode (1,2,0,0,1,2)
that spans the electron T² and proton T².  The T⁶ metric also
contains cross-shear couplings to T²_ν (σ_eν and σ_νp).
Therefore, every neutron is a cross-plane coupling node — a
point in R³ where energy in ordinary EM fields can reach T²_ν
via the coupling chain:

    EM field → electron T² → σ_eν → T²_ν
    EM field → proton T²  → σ_νp → T²_ν

No new particle needs to be created.  The gateways already exist
in the cell's ordinary matter.

### Why this resolves mobility

Neutrons are bound to atomic nuclei.  Nuclei are bound into
molecules.  Molecules are bound into cells.  When a cell moves,
its neutrons move with it.  Each neutron couples to T²_ν at its
current position.  The stored state is continuously refreshed
at the cell's new location by the moving gateway population.

The stored information is an attribute of the matter (carried by
its neutron population), not a marking on fixed spacetime
coordinates.  This is the same sense in which an electron's
mass is an attribute of the electron (carried by its compact-
dimension wavefunction), not a property of the location where
the electron happens to be.

### Redundancy and robustness

A typical cell has ~10¹⁴ atoms, most with neutrons.  The
gateway population is massively redundant.  No single atom is
critical.  Atoms are replaced by metabolism, but replacement
atoms also contain neutrons.  The gateway population is
self-renewing.

### All gateways share one cavity

The spatial resolution of T²_ν is ~42 μm (the Compton
wavelength of the lightest neutrino mode).  All neutrons within
a cell are within one cavity.  They do not create separate
address spaces; they provide parallel coupling into the same
~180 modes.  More gateways = stronger coupling, not more
storage.

Between cells (separated by ≳42 μm), gateways couple to
different cavities — independent address spaces.

### Coupling strength scales with neutron density

Heavier elements have more neutrons per atom.  Neutron-rich
tissue (bone, mineral-dense organelles, metalloenzymes) would
couple more strongly to T²_ν than hydrogen-rich tissue (fat,
water).  This predicts measurable variation in compact-dimension
coupling across tissue types.

### Status of Theory B

Simpler than Theory A — it requires no new particle creation,
no pair-production, and no new physics beyond what the T⁶ model
already contains.  The neutron-gateway mechanism is a direct
consequence of the T⁶ cross-plane mode structure and the
existence of cross-shear couplings.  The main open question is
quantitative: is the per-neutron coupling strength, multiplied
by ~10¹⁴ gateways, sufficient for biologically relevant
read/write rates?

## Comparison

| Criterion | Theory A (funded ν) | Theory B (neutron gateway) |
|---|---|---|
| New particle required? | Yes (ν-ν̄ pair) | No |
| Pair-production problem? | Yes — severe | N/A |
| Mechanism within T⁶? | Requires threshold accumulation | Direct cross-shear coupling |
| Mobility explanation | ν co-particle moves with cell | Neutrons move with atoms |
| Gateway count per cell | 1 (or a few) | ~10¹⁴ |
| Redundancy | Low (single point of failure) | Massive |
| Cell-division prediction | Yes (ν fission) | Not directly |
| Coupling to T²_ν | Direct (funded mode on T²_ν) | Indirect (via cross-shear chain) |
| Simplicity | More moving parts | Fewer assumptions |

**Current preference: Theory B**, on grounds of parsimony and
avoidance of the pair-production problem.  However, Theory A's
cell-division prediction is worth preserving as a testable idea
independent of the access mechanism.

The two theories are not mutually exclusive.  Theory B provides
the baseline access mechanism (neutron gateways always present).
Theory A could operate on top of it — cells might, under some
conditions, fund a neutrino that enhances the coupling beyond
what the neutron gateways alone provide.  But Theory A is not
required for the storage hypothesis to work.

## Open questions

1. **Per-neutron coupling strength:** What is the energy transfer
   rate from a single neutron's cross-plane mode into T²_ν, via
   σ_eν and σ_νp?  This requires solving the T⁶ mode coupling
   equations at realistic cross-shear values.

2. **Aggregate coupling per cell:** Single-neutron rate × ~10¹⁴
   gateways.  Is this biologically relevant (write/read on
   millisecond timescales)?

3. **Pair creation rate at meV energies:** Can the threshold
   model's continuous accumulation fund a ν-ν̄ pair on
   biological timescales?  What accumulation time does the T⁶
   evanescent coupling imply?

4. **Antineutrino fate:** If a pair IS created, does the ν̄
   remain localized (forming a boson) or escape?  What drives
   the asymmetry?

5. **Cell division energetics:** Is there a measurable THz
   signature in pre-mitotic cells that could test the fission
   prediction?

6. **Neutron density mapping:** Does the observed variation in
   regenerative capacity across tissue types correlate with
   neutron density (heavier elements → more gateways)?

7. **Terminology:** Need a clean, consistent naming convention
   for T²_ν in papers aimed at non-physicists.  Current
   candidates: "neutrino sheet," "ν-sheet," "cellular torus."
