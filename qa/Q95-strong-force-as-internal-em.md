## 1. The energy partition inside the electron

The electron is a photon of energy m_e c² confined to a (1,2)
geodesic on Ma_e.  The energy distributes as:

| Component | Fraction | Energy |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Internal B field (co-rotating, magnetic dipole) | ~1/2 | ~0.256 MeV |
| Internal E field (counter-rotating) | ~1/2 | ~0.256 MeV |
| External Coulomb field (leaks through Compton window) | α ≈ 1/137 | ~0.00373 MeV |
| External magnetic dipole (also α-projected) | ~ α | ~0.00373 MeV |

The internal fields are ~1/(2α) ≈ 68× more energetic than the
external Coulomb field.  The g-factor ≈ 2 (WvM energy partition)
tells us that roughly half the photon's energy is in the co-rotating
component that produces the magnetic dipole moment.

**Both the external electric and magnetic fields are α-suppressed.**
The charge e and the Bohr magneton μ_B are both set by the same
α-coupling to S.  At distances r >> λ_C, the interaction between
two particles goes entirely through S, and the full apparatus of
QED applies.


## 2. What happens at r ~ λ_C

When two particles approach to within a Compton wavelength, their
Ma tori overlap spatially.  At this range, the interaction need not
go through S — the internal fields on Ma can couple directly,
torus-to-torus.

This changes everything:

- **Through S (r >> λ_C):** both E and B fields are α-projected.
  Coulomb ∝ e²/r² dominates over magnetic dipole ∝ μ²/r⁴.
  QED applies.  Force is repulsive for like charges.

- **Direct on Ma (r ~ λ_C):** the full internal fields interact.
  These are ~1/α ≈ 137× the projected fields.  The interaction
  has the character of the full photon field — predominantly
  magnetic (dipole-dipole), orientation-dependent, and potentially
  attractive.

Two bar magnets at contact: aligned N-to-S, they attract strongly.
Aligned N-to-N, they repel strongly.  The force depends on
orientation.  Two (1,2) photons circulating on adjacent tori would
see each other's full B field — not the α-attenuated sliver.


## 3. The strong force emerges

The measured coupling constants at nuclear distances:

| Interaction | Coupling | At nuclear scale |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| Electromagnetic | α ≈ 1/137 | The Compton-window projection |
| Strong | α_s ≈ 1 | The full internal field |
| Ratio | α_s / α ≈ 137 | = 1/α |

In MaSt, this ratio is not a coincidence.  It IS 1/α — the ratio
of the full internal field energy to the α-projected external field.
The "strong force" is the electromagnetic force seen from inside Ma,
without the Compton window's attenuation.

### Properties that match

| Strong force property | MaSt explanation |
|----------------------|------------------|
| Short range (~1 fm) | Only operates at torus-overlap distances (r ~ λ_C) |
| ~137× stronger than EM | Internal field / external field = 1/α |
| Attractive | Magnetic dipole N-to-S alignment |
| Spin-dependent | The (1,2) winding determines the dipole orientation |
| Repulsive hard core (~0.5 fm) | Anti-aligned magnetic orientation (S-to-S) |
| Charge-independent (pp ≈ pn ≈ nn) | Internal fields don't depend on the Coulomb projection; see §4 |


## 4. Charge independence

The strong force is approximately charge-independent: the p-p, p-n,
and n-n forces are nearly equal (after removing Coulomb).  In MaSt:

- The Coulomb charge is an α-projection artifact — it depends on n₁
  and n₅ (tube windings that produce the monopole through shear).
- The internal Ma field is the full photon, which doesn't "know"
  about charge — charge is a property of the S-projection, not of
  the mode itself.
- At torus-overlap distances, the interaction is between the full
  internal fields, which are charge-blind.

The proton and neutron have the same proton-sheet ring winding
(n₆ = 2) and similar total energy.  Their internal B fields are
nearly identical.  The neutron's charge neutrality (n₁ = 0, n₅ = 0)
only matters for the Coulomb projection, not for the direct Ma-Ma
coupling.

The small charge-dependence of the nuclear force (Coulomb correction
to pp vs nn) is then exactly what it appears to be: the α-projected
Coulomb field riding on top of the charge-blind internal interaction.


## 5. Connection to fusion (Q89)

Q89 established that fusion is a mode transition on Ma, not a
particle collision.  The missing piece was: what drives the
transition?

Answer: **direct Ma-Ma coupling at torus overlap.**

1. Two protons at large separation — Coulomb repulsion (through S,
   α-projected, repulsive).

2. Approaching ~1 fm (proton Compton wavelength) — the Ma tori begin
   to overlap.  The internal fields, 137× stronger than Coulomb,
   take over.

3. If aligned (N-to-S magnetic dipoles) — strongly attractive.
   The protons are pulled together.

4. At full overlap — the two modes occupy the same region of Ma.  If
   the combined configuration matches a nuclear mode (deuteron =
   (1,n₂,1,0,2,4) on Ma_p), the system snaps into that eigenstate.
   This is fusion.

5. If anti-aligned (S-to-S) — strongly repulsive.  The protons
   bounce off.  This is the "hard core" of the nuclear potential.

The "Coulomb barrier" is the α-projected repulsion that must be
overcome before torus overlap.  At r ~ 1 fm, the barrier is
~1 MeV.  Once past it, the 137× internal attraction takes over.

Most collisions have the wrong magnetic orientation → no fusion.
The fraction with correct alignment determines the cross-section.
At stellar temperatures, enough collisions have both sufficient
energy AND correct alignment → fusion at the observed rate.


## 6. Predictions

1. **α_s / α = 1/α at the nuclear scale.**  This is already
   approximately true: α_s(1 GeV) ≈ 0.5, α ≈ 1/137, ratio ≈ 68.
   The exact value of 1/(2α) ≈ 68 vs measured ~68 is intriguing
   (the factor of 2 comes from the 1/2 energy partition).

2. **Nuclear force range = proton Compton wavelength.**  λ_C(proton)
   = ℏ/(m_p c) = 1.32 fm.  The nuclear force range is ~1.4 fm
   (Yukawa: ℏ/(m_π c) = 1.41 fm).  These are close but not
   identical.  The MaSt prediction (1.32 fm) differs from the
   pion-exchange prediction (1.41 fm) by 6%.

3. **Running of α_s.**  At higher energies (shorter distances), the
   torus overlap increases and the Compton window becomes less
   relevant.  α_s should approach 1 as the full internal coupling
   is exposed.  This matches asymptotic freedom qualitatively —
   at very short distances, the coupling saturates at ~1 (the full
   internal field).  But asymptotic freedom in QCD has α_s → 0 at
   short distances, which is the opposite.  This is a potential
   problem (see §7).


## 7. What could be wrong

1. **Asymptotic freedom.**  QCD's α_s decreases at short distances
   (asymptotic freedom).  The MaSt picture naively predicts the
   opposite: at shorter distances (more torus overlap), the coupling
   should increase toward the full internal value.  Resolving this
   requires understanding what "short distance" means in the Ma
   picture — it may map to probing the internal structure of the
   mode, not to approaching a point particle.

2. **Gluon-like dynamics.**  QCD has SU(3) gauge symmetry, 8 gluons,
   and three color charges.  MaSt has none of this structure.
   The color degrees of freedom might correspond to the three
   material sheets (Ma_e, Ma_ν, Ma_p) — R14 Track 0 already noted
   that three linking planes map to three color charges.  But this
   is far from established.

3. **The magnetic picture is too simple.**  The internal field of a
   (1,2) photon on a torus is not a pure magnetic dipole.  It has a
   complex spatial structure that depends on r, the tube/ring ratio.
   The actual torus-torus interaction integral has not been computed.

4. **R39 found no attraction.**  But R39 computed interactions
   through S (projected fields), not through direct Ma-Ma coupling.
   The two calculations are different.  R39's result ("no EM
   attraction") is correct for the α-projected interaction.  It
   says nothing about direct Ma-Ma coupling at overlap.


## 8. What's computable

1. **The torus-torus interaction integral.**  Take two identical
   Ma_p tori at separation d, compute the direct field-field
   interaction energy as a function of d and relative orientation.
   This uses the full internal fields, not the α-projected monopole.
   Compare to the Yukawa nuclear potential.

2. **The coupling ratio.**  Compute the ratio of direct Ma-Ma
   coupling to α-projected Coulomb coupling at d = 1.3 fm.  If this
   equals ~137, the identification α_s = 1/α × α is confirmed.

3. **Orientation dependence.**  Compute the interaction for all
   relative orientations.  If the result is attractive for aligned
   dipoles and repulsive for anti-aligned, the hard core + attractive
   well structure of the nuclear potential is reproduced.

4. **Fusion cross-section.**  Using the torus-torus potential,
   compute the fusion cross-section and compare to measured values.

## 9. Summary

The standard model needs three separate forces (EM, weak, strong)
with three separate mechanisms.  MaSt proposes they are the SAME
electromagnetic field seen at different distances:

| Distance | What you see | Standard name |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| r >> λ_C | α-projected Coulomb field through Compton window | Electromagnetism |
| r ~ λ_C  | Full internal Ma field, direct torus-torus coupling | Strong force |
| Cross-sheet | σ_ep coupling between Ma sheets | Weak force |

The coupling constants (α ≈ 1/137, α_s ≈ 1, G_F ~ σ_ep²) are
not independent fundamental constants.  They are the same
electromagnetic interaction seen from different vantage points:
through the window (α), without the window (α_s ≈ 1), and across
sheets (G_F ∝ σ_ep²).
