## 1. The reinterpretation

### Standard picture

An atom is a nucleus (a composite particle at a point in
3D space) surrounded by electrons (separate particles
orbiting in 3D, held by the Coulomb force, prevented from
collapsing by the uncertainty principle).  The electron
shell structure comes from solving the Schrödinger equation
in a 1/r Coulomb potential.  Electrons are identical by
quantum statistical postulate (fermion indistinguishability).

### Proposed picture

An atom is a **single cross-sheet mode** on the Ma metric
— a standing wave spanning Ma_e (electron sheet) and Ma_p
(proton sheet), coupled through σ_ep.  There are no
separate particles.  There is one composite mode whose
projection into 3D produces what we observe as a nucleus
with orbiting electrons.

The electron component of an atom with Z electrons is the
**(Z, 2Z) harmonic** on Ma_e.  This mode decomposes into Z
identical (1,2) strands, each separated by 360°/Z in phase:

| Atom | Z | Ma_e mode | Strands | Phase separation |
|------|---|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| Hydrogen | 1 | (1,2) | 1 | — |
| Helium | 2 | (2,4) | 2 | 180° |
| Lithium | 3 | (3,6) | 3 | 120° |
| Carbon | 6 | (6,12) | 6 | 60° |
| Iron | 26 | (26,52) | 26 | ~14° |
| Gold | 79 | (79,158) | 79 | ~4.6° |

Each strand carries charge −e (one 2π winding, n₁ = 1 per
strand).  The total electron charge is −Ze.  The proton
component carries charge +Ze (from the nuclear mode on
Ma_p).  The atom is neutral: Q = −Z + Z = 0.

### Why electrons are identical

Not because of a quantum statistical postulate.  Because
they literally ARE the same standing wave, sampled at
different phase positions on the torus.  You cannot
distinguish two electrons because there are not two
electrons.  There is one mode with Z-fold symmetry.
Indistinguishability is geometric, not postulated.


## 2. Why electrons don't crash into the nucleus

In the standard picture, this requires explanation —
the uncertainty principle provides the kinetic energy
penalty that balances the Coulomb attraction.

In the cross-sheet mode picture, the question dissolves.
The electron and proton are not two objects at two
locations in 3D space, one trying to reach the other.
They are two components of one mode on the Ma sheets.
Inside the torus space, there is no Coulomb force.
Coulomb is the external 3D phenomenon — the 1/137 that
leaks through the torus surface.

The components interact through the Ma metric (σ_ep
coupling), not through 3D Coulomb.  "Crashing" is a 3D
spatial concept.  The electron-proton relationship is
modal, not spatial.  The electron can't crash into the
nucleus for the same reason the north pole of a magnet
can't crash into the south pole — they are features of
one object, not two objects approaching each other.

The quantized energy levels of the atom are the
eigenvalues of the cross-sheet mode — discrete by
topology (integer winding numbers), not by a boundary
condition on a spatial wavefunction.

The relationship between electrons in an atom is the
same KIND of thing as the relationship between quarks in

The difference is **scale and coupling strength**:

- Ma_p is ~1000× smaller than Ma_e → quarks are at the

  → electrons can be removed (ionization)

**Chemistry is weak-coupling nuclear physics on a bigger
torus.**


## 4. Valence shells from mode crowding

### The crowding hypothesis

As Z increases, the (Z, 2Z) mode has more strands packed
into the same torus circumference.  Each strand occupies
a phase interval of 360°/Z.  At low Z, the strands are
widely separated — minimal interaction between them.  At
higher Z, they crowd together — the phase intervals
shrink and the strands begin to interfere.

At some point, the crowding reaches a limit: the strands
cannot coexist in the same mode without the energy
becoming prohibitively high.  This is a **shell closure**
— the mode is full.

The next electron cannot join the existing mode.  It
must occupy a DIFFERENT mode — a higher harmonic, a
different winding pattern, or a mode on a different
region of the torus.  This higher mode is the next
valence shell.

### The proton sheet absorption hypothesis

Here is where the cross-sheet coupling becomes critical.

When the electron mode is "full" (phase-crowded to its
limit), the excess energy has to go somewhere.  The
cross-sheet coupling σ_ep connects Ma_e to Ma_p.  The
excess energy could transfer to the proton sheet,
exciting the nuclear mode to a higher harmonic.

In MaSt, the proton sheet supports multiple modes above
the fundamental.  The nuclear mode spectrum (R29) has
modes at A, 2A, 3A, ... on Ma_p.  The nucleus could
absorb the excess energy from the electron crowding by
transitioning to a higher proton-sheet mode.  This
"super proton" — a nucleus in an excited proton-sheet
harmonic — would then be able to support a higher-energy
electron mode on Ma_e.

The sequence for building heavier atoms:

1. Start with the fundamental electron mode (1,2) on Ma_e
   coupled to the nuclear mode on Ma_p
2. Add electrons: the Ma_e mode climbs through (2,4),
   (3,6), (4,8), ...
3. At some Z, the strands are too crowded — the mode
   energy exceeds a threshold
4. The excess energy transfers to Ma_p through σ_ep,
   exciting the nuclear mode to the next harmonic
5. The nuclear mode in its new harmonic can now support
   the next electron shell — a different, higher-energy
   Ma_e mode with wider phase intervals
6. The new shell fills, crowds, transfers energy to Ma_p
   again, and the process repeats

### Shell magic numbers

The noble gas shell closures are at Z = 2, 10, 18, 36,
54, 86.  The electrons per shell are:

| Shell | Electrons | Cumulative Z | Standard QM source |
|-------|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | 2 | 2 | 1s² |
| 2 | 8 | 10 | 2s² 2p⁶ |
| 3 | 8 | 18 | 3s² 3p⁶ |
| 4 | 18 | 36 | 4s² 3d¹⁰ 4p⁶ |
| 5 | 18 | 54 | 5s² 4d¹⁰ 5p⁶ |
| 6 | 32 | 86 | 6s² 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6p⁶ |

The numbers 2, 8, 18, 32 follow the pattern 2n² (for
n = 1, 2, 3, 4).  In standard QM, this comes from the
angular momentum quantum numbers (ℓ = 0, 1, 2, 3 giving
s, p, d, f orbitals with 2, 6, 10, 14 states each,
doubled by spin).

In the mode crowding picture, these numbers would need
to emerge from the torus geometry:
- How many (1,2) strands can coexist before the phase
  intervals are too small?
- Does the answer depend on the torus aspect ratio ε?
- Does the proton sheet's mode spectrum determine when
  the crossover happens?

The fact that shell sizes follow 2n² (a simple quadratic)
suggests a geometric origin — possibly related to the
number of independent winding patterns that fit on the
torus at each energy level.  This is computable in
principle from the Ma_e eigenvalue spectrum.

### How would we know the nucleus is absorbing energy?

The nuclear mode excitation at each shell closure would
manifest as:

- A slight increase in nuclear binding energy at shell
  closures (the nuclear mode accommodates the electron
  energy — this is testable)
- Correlations between electron shell structure and
  nuclear stability (magic numbers in nuclear physics
  ARE correlated with shell structure in atomic physics
  — this is observed but not explained in standard
  physics)
- Changes in the nuclear magnetic moment or charge
  radius at shell closures (the proton-sheet mode shape
  changes when it transitions to a higher harmonic)

Nuclear magic numbers (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) are
NOT the same as electron shell closures (2, 10, 18, 36,
54, 86), but there is overlap (2 appears in both, 8
appears close to 10).  Whether the cross-sheet coupling
produces a systematic relationship between the two sets
of magic numbers is a testable prediction.


## 5. Ionization in this picture

Removing an electron = the Ma_e mode dropping by one
harmonic: (Z, 2Z) → (Z−1, 2(Z−1)).  One strand is lost.
The remaining strands redistribute to fill the torus at
wider phase intervals.

The ionization energy is the difference in eigenvalues
between the Z-strand and (Z−1)-strand modes on Ma_e,
modified by the cross-sheet coupling to Ma_p.

**Ionization energies are periodic** (they follow the
periodic table, with peaks at noble gases and drops at
alkali metals).  In the mode picture, this periodicity
comes from the shell structure: removing one strand from
a full (phase-crowded) shell costs a lot of energy
(noble gas — tightly packed, every strand stabilizes
every other).  Removing one strand from a barely occupied
new shell costs little (alkali metal — the strand is
loosely coupled to the mode below).


## 6. Chemical bonding

If atoms are cross-sheet modes, then a molecule is a
**shared cross-sheet mode** — two nuclear modes on Ma_p
coupled to a common electron mode on Ma_e.

A covalent bond: two atoms share electron strands.  The
combined Ma_e mode has more strands than either atom
alone, but fewer than the sum of both (the shared
strands are counted once, not twice).  The bond energy
is the difference between the combined mode's eigenvalue
and the two separate modes' eigenvalues.

An ionic bond: one atom transfers strands to another.
The donor's Ma_e mode drops in harmonic; the acceptor's
rises.  The Coulomb attraction between the resulting ions
is the external 3D projection of the asymmetric mode.

This is a radical reinterpretation of chemistry.  But
the predictions are sharp: bond energies, bond angles,
and molecular geometries should follow from the Ma_e
mode spectrum coupled to the nuclear modes on Ma_p.


## 7. Binding energy as shear-boundary loss

### The mass defect is real

Atoms weigh LESS than the sum of their parts:

| System | Sum of parts | Actual mass | Deficit |
|--------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Hydrogen | 938.783 MeV | 938.783 − 0.0000136 MeV | 13.6 eV |
| Helium-4 | 3755.674 MeV | 3727.379 MeV | 28.3 MeV |
| Iron-56 | 55,618.6 MeV | 55,526.5 MeV | 492.3 MeV |

The "missing" mass is the binding energy — radiated away
as photons when the system assembled.  E = mc²: less
energy = less mass.  This is measured, not theoretical.

### The shear-boundary hypothesis

When two modes couple across sheets through σ_ep, energy
must cross the shear boundary.  The shear boundary is an
impedance mismatch — the same kind of junction physics
that gives us α at the 2D/3D torus-space boundary.

Energy crossing a mismatched boundary doesn't all
transmit.  The energy "lost" at the boundary radiates
into the 3D lattice as photons when the cross-sheet mode
forms.  This lost energy IS the binding energy.

The chain:

1. Electron mode and proton mode begin uncoupled
   (separate modes on separate sheets, total energy =
   sum of parts)
2. Cross-sheet coupling σ_ep activates (the modes enter
   the same spatial region)
3. Energy crosses the shear boundary to form the coupled
   cross-sheet mode
4. Transmission loss at the boundary — some energy
   doesn't make it across
5. The lost energy radiates through the torus surface
   into 3D space as photons
6. The coupled mode has less energy than the uncoupled
   sum — the atom is lighter than its parts

### What the eigenvalue solver can and cannot check

The model (`ma_model_d.py`) can compute
E(coupled mode) − E(sum of uncoupled parts).  If this
difference matches the measured binding energy, the
metric algebra reproduces the right number.

But the model cannot explain WHY the number is right.
It computes eigenvalues (kinematics — what states exist
at what energy), not transition dynamics (how the system
reaches that state, where the energy goes).  The
"shear-boundary loss" is a physical interpretation of the
math, not something the eigenvalue solver validates.

The computation tests the prediction.  The mechanism
remains a hypothesis until a dynamical model (time
evolution, transition amplitudes, boundary physics) is
built.

### Harmonic mass scaling

If 4 electrons = the (4,8) harmonic on Ma_e, how does
the electron-component mass scale with Z?

On a single torus: E(Z, 2Z) = Z × E(1,2).  Mass grows
**linearly** with Z.  Four electrons contribute exactly
4 × m_e = 2.044 MeV — and indeed, the electron-mass
contribution to an atom IS Z × m_e (minus binding energy).

But the binding energy does NOT grow linearly with Z.
Total ionization energies scale roughly as Z^(7/3) for
heavy atoms (Thomas-Fermi scaling).  This nonlinearity
must come from the cross-sheet coupling — either from
the mode geometry (how Z strands couple to the nuclear
mode at the shear boundary) or from the details of the
boundary impedance at each shell.

If the eigenvalue solver reproduces both the linear
electron-mass scaling AND the nonlinear binding energy
scaling, that would be strong evidence that the
cross-sheet mode picture captures the right physics.

## 9. Electron shielding as evidence

A striking feature of atomic physics supports the modal
picture: **filled electron shells screen the nuclear
charge almost perfectly.**

The effective nuclear charge seen by an outer electron:

| Atom | Z | Inner electrons | Z_eff at outer shell |
|------|---|----------------|---------------------|
| Li | 3 | 2 (1s²) | ~1.3 |
| Na | 11 | 10 ([Ne]) | ~1.8 |
| K | 19 | 18 ([Ar]) | ~2.2 |
| Noble gas | Z | Z (all filled) | ~0 |

Lithium's outer electron sees Z_eff ≈ 1.3, not 3.  The
two inner electrons have nearly cancelled two of the
three proton charges.  From outside the first shell, the
atom looks like hydrogen.

Noble gases take this to the limit: all shells filled,
all nuclear charge screened, Z_eff ≈ 0 from outside.
This is why noble gases are chemically inert — there is
no net charge for another atom to interact with.

**In the cross-sheet mode picture:** a filled shell with
Z electrons matching Z protons is a neutral composite
mode — n₁ = Z on Ma_e matching n₅ = Z on Ma_p, giving
Q = −Z + Z = 0.  Only the outermost incomplete shell
carries unmatched charge.  The screening isn't an
approximate cancellation of separate charges — it IS
the neutral mode, with only the excess (unfilled)
strands visible externally.

### The role of dark modes

In standard QM, each shell has subshells (s, p, d, f)
with electron counts 2, 6, 10, 14.  Not all contribute
equally to chemistry — the outermost subshell dominates.

In Q105's picture, these subshells might correspond to
different mode types on the torus: some with odd n₁
(charged strands), some with even n₁ (dark modes that
occupy phase space without contributing charge).  The
dark modes would be structural — they fill out the
shell and contribute to its energy and stability, but
they don't produce external Coulomb field.

If so, the 2n² shell capacity is not all charged modes.
It is charged + dark modes combined, with the dark modes
providing the internal scaffolding that makes each shell
stable.  The torus has plenty of high-energy dark modes
available (R42: ~860 modes below 2 GeV).  These would
not be "ghost particles" in this context — they would be
structural components of atomic shells.

  > "Ma determines particle identity and nuclear mass
  > (MeV scale); S determines atomic binding and nuclear

  > they live below the Ma resolution floor."

  Paper 3 derived hydrogen's ground-state energy
  (−13.6 eV) and Bohr radius (0.53 Å) successfully
  using Ma-defined particles bound through the Coulomb

  **The tension:** Q105 proposes atoms ARE cross-sheet
  modes.  Paper 3 says they are NOT.  Both positions
  produce the hydrogen spectrum (Paper 3 proved it for
  the spatial-binding picture; Q105 has not yet computed
  it for the modal picture).

  **The resolution depends on a computation:** does the
  cross-sheet Ma eigenvalue spectrum have states at the
  eV scale?  The Ma mode spacing is set by the torus
  circumferences (~MeV for Ma_p, ~keV for Ma_e).
  There are no obvious Ma modes at 13.6 eV.  However:

  - For high harmonics (large Z), the spacing between
    adjacent (Z, 2Z) and (Z+1, 2(Z+1)) modes decreases.
    Whether it reaches eV at physically relevant Z
    values is unknown.
  - The cross-sheet coupling σ_ep could shift eigenvalues
    to produce eV-scale splittings even when the
    single-sheet spacing is larger.
  - The two pictures may not be mutually exclusive —
    the spatial Coulomb binding (Paper 3) might be the
    3D projection of the cross-sheet modal coupling

  **Neither position is conceded.**  Both need the
  cross-sheet eigenvalue computation to be resolved.

  **Critical gap in R31's argument:** R31 searched for a
  single Ma mode AT the hydrogen mass (938.783 MeV).  It
  found none within 661 keV.  But Q105 does not propose a
  mode at that mass.  Q105 proposes a cross-sheet mode
  where the coupling σ_ep produces a perturbative splitting
  — the binding energy (13.6 eV) would be the DIFFERENCE
  between the coupled and uncoupled eigenvalues, not a new

  The analogy: the neutron mass is not "proton + electron."
  It is a cross-sheet eigenvalue modified by σ_ep.  The
  neutron-proton mass difference (1.293 MeV) comes from the
  cross-coupling.  R31 should have computed the eigenvalue
  of the coupled mode (1,2,0,0,1,2) at nonzero σ_ep and
  checked whether the splitting from the uncoupled sum is

  thing (a mode at a specific mass) instead of the right
  thing (a perturbative splitting from cross-sheet coupling).

  This does not prove Q105 is right.  It means the question
  is genuinely open — not settled by R31 as claimed.


## 11. Caution

This is a **foundational reinterpretation**, not a
computed result.  Nothing in this Q file has been
calculated.  The claims are structural and qualitative.

The strongest point is the dissolution of the
electron-crash problem: if electrons and nuclei are
components of one mode, the crash question is a category
error.

The most supportive observation is electron shielding:
filled shells screen nuclear charge almost perfectly,
consistent with matched-harmonic neutral modes (§8).

The most testable point is the shell structure: the
2, 8, 18, 32 pattern must emerge from the Ma_e torus
geometry.  If it does, the reinterpretation gains
substantial weight.  If it doesn't — if the mode
crowding threshold gives the wrong numbers — the
picture is wrong.

The most speculative point is the proton-sheet
absorption hypothesis (§4): nuclear mode excitation at
electron shell closures is a novel prediction with no
current evidence either way.

**The critical tension** is with Paper 3 (atoms-from-
geometry), which successfully derived the hydrogen
spectrum using the two-tier (spatial binding) picture.
Q105 must either reproduce this success from the modal
picture or explain why the two-tier picture works even
if Q105 is correct (projection argument).

**Priority:** determine whether the mode crowding
threshold on Ma_e, at the electron sheet's aspect ratio
ε_e, produces shell closures at 2, 8, 18, 32.  This is
a single computation (eigenvalues of the (Z, 2Z) modes
vs Z, looking for energy discontinuities) that would
validate or falsify the picture.  Simultaneously, check
whether the cross-sheet eigenvalue spectrum has states
at the eV scale — this resolves the tension with
Paper 3.

**The right context for this test is model-D.**  Paper 3

The "atoms are not modes" conclusion from Paper 3 was
computed with model-C parameters.  Model-D may produce

This question is deferred until after the model-D particle

But it should be among the first follow-up computations
