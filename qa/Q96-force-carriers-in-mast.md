## 1. The Standard Model's force carrier framework

In the Standard Model, every force is mediated by exchanging
a boson — a particle with integer spin:

| Force | Carrier | Mass | Spin | Discovery |
|-------|---------|------|------|-----------|
| Electromagnetic | Photon (γ) | 0 | 1 | Maxwell (1865), Einstein (1905) |
| Strong | 8 gluons (g) | 0 | 1 | DESY (1979) |
| Weak | W⁺, W⁻ | 80.4 GeV | 1 | CERN (1983) |
| Weak | Z⁰ | 91.2 GeV | 1 | CERN (1983) |
| Mass mechanism | Higgs (H) | 125 GeV | 0 | CERN (2012) |
| Gravity | Graviton | 0? | 2 | Not observed |

This structure comes from quantum field theory (QFT): matter
fields (fermions) interact by exchanging quanta of gauge
fields (bosons).  Two electrons repel because they exchange
virtual photons.  A proton holds together because its quarks
exchange gluons.  Beta decay occurs because a quark emits a
W boson.

The bosons are as "real" as the fermions — they have definite
mass, spin, and coupling strengths, and the W, Z, and Higgs
have all been directly produced and detected at colliders.

**The question:** Does MaSt need force-carrying bosons?  If
forces arise from field coupling rather than particle
exchange, what are the W, Z, Higgs, and gluons?


## 2. MaSt's force framework: field coupling, not particle exchange

MaSt starts from a different foundation.  All particles are
electromagnetic energy confined to compact material sheets.
Forces are not mediated by a separate class of objects.  They
arise from how the EM fields of different modes interact,
with the mechanism depending on distance scale.

Q95 §9 established the mapping:

| Distance | What you see | SM name |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| r >> λ_C | α-projected Coulomb field through Compton window | EM force |
| r ~ λ_C  | Full internal Ma field, direct torus-torus coupling | Strong force |
| Cross-sheet | σ coupling between Ma sheets | Weak force |

The coupling constants (α ≈ 1/137, α_s ≈ 1, G_F ∝ σ²)
are not independent fundamental constants.  They are the
same electromagnetic interaction seen from different vantage
points: through the window (α), without the window (α_s),
and across sheets (G_F).

This eliminates the need for force-carrying bosons **as a
category**.  But each SM boson must still be accounted for
individually — either as unnecessary, as a reinterpretation
of something real, or as a Ma mode in its own right.


## 3. The photon — same physics, different framing

The photon is the least problematic case.  In MaSt, a photon
is electromagnetic energy propagating through spacetime S.
It is not a "force carrier" in some abstract sense — it IS
the electromagnetic field.

When two electrons interact at long range, their Coulomb
fields overlap in S.  QED describes this as "virtual photon
exchange," but this is a perturbative bookkeeping device for
calculating the field interaction.  There is no literal
particle bouncing between the electrons.  The field does
what fields do.

MaSt changes nothing about how EM propagates — Maxwell's
equations are axiomatic.  The only new element is the
Compton window: the factor α relating the full internal Ma
field to the fraction projected into S.  But MaSt does give
the EM force a mechanical picture that the SM lacks.


## 4. Attraction and repulsion as vacuum elasticity

The SM can compute the Coulomb force to extraordinary
precision, but it does not say what the vacuum IS — only
that it has a permittivity ε₀.  MaSt identifies ε₀ with
the **elastic stiffness of the compact geometry** against
field leakage through the Compton window.

### The vacuum as an EM spring

A charged particle's internal field (confined on Ma) projects
a fraction α of itself into S.  This creates the radial E
field we measure as the Coulomb field.  Creating that field
in S costs energy — it displaces the Ma-S boundary, pushing
field energy from the compact dimensions into the spatial
dimensions.  The energy cost is proportional to E², and the
proportionality constant is ε₀.

In this picture, ε₀ is the **spring constant of the vacuum**:
the resistance of the compact geometry to having its internal
field pulled into S.  Q77 developed this as the "impedance
mismatch" between Ma and S — the same idea in wave-mechanics
language.  Spring constant and impedance are the same concept
in different notation.

### Why like charges repel

A positive charge has an outward-pointing radial E field —
it stretches the vacuum spring outward.  A negative charge
has an inward-pointing field — it compresses the spring
inward.

Between two positive charges, both fields push outward.  The
spring between them is compressed from both sides.  Stored
energy increases as they approach → the system resists →
repulsion.

Between a positive and negative charge, one pushes out and
the other pulls in.  The fields between them partially
cancel.  Stored energy decreases as they approach → the
system relaxes → attraction.

This is the Maxwell stress tensor given a physical identity.
In the SM, ε₀E²/2 is the field energy density, and the
force is its gradient — but the "stress" is stress on
nothing.  In MaSt, the stress is on the compact geometry.
The spring is the Ma-S coupling interface.

### The connection to the strong force

At r >> λ_C, the interaction goes through the spring — the
E field leaks through the Compton window, stores energy in
the S vacuum, and the gradient of that stored energy is
the Coulomb force.  The spring constant ε₀ attenuates the
force by α.

At r ~ λ_C (Q95), the tori overlap and the fields couple
directly through Ma, bypassing the spring entirely.  The
internal fields are ~1/α ≈ 137× stronger.  Same field, same
geometry — one path goes through the spring, the other
doesn't.  That is the EM/strong force ratio.

### What this does NOT explain

MaSt takes Maxwell's equations as axiomatic.  How EM fields
propagate, how they store energy, and why the wave speed is
c are not derived — they are the starting point.  The spring
metaphor does not change the dynamics (a spring also
propagates disturbances at finite speed).  What MaSt adds is
the identity of the medium: ε₀ is not a bare constant.  It
is the stiffness of the compact geometry against field
displacement into spacetime.


## 5. Gluons — not needed

This is where MaSt diverges most sharply from the SM.

Q95 showed that when two hadrons approach within a Compton
wavelength, their Ma tori overlap.  The internal fields
couple directly — torus to torus, through the material
dimensions — without going through S.  These internal fields
are ~1/α ≈ 137× stronger than the external Coulomb field,
because the Compton window only projects a fraction α of the
internal energy into S.

In the SM, this enormous short-range force requires 8 gluons,
three "color charges," and the full SU(3) gauge symmetry of
QCD.  In MaSt, it is the same electromagnetic field that
produces the Coulomb force — just coupling through Ma instead
of through S.

### What replaces color charge?

QCD's three colors (red, green, blue) are a bookkeeping
system ensuring that baryon wave functions are antisymmetric.
Three colors map naturally to MaSt's three material sheets
(Ma_e, Ma_ν, Ma_p) — R14 Track 0 already noted this
correspondence.  The requirement that hadrons be "color
singlets" (one of each color) maps to the requirement that
stable cross-sheet modes involve all three sheets
symmetrically (as the neutron does — R27 F15–F18).

### What replaces the 8 gluons?

QCD has 3² − 1 = 8 gluon types, corresponding to the
generators of SU(3).  In MaSt, the torus-torus interaction
has geometric degrees of freedom: relative orientations of
the two tori, phase alignments of the circulating modes,
and coupling amplitudes between the two sheets' fields.

Three sheets with pairwise couplings give 3 × 2 / 2 = 3
coupling channels (Ma_e ↔ Ma_p, Ma_e ↔ Ma_ν, Ma_ν ↔ Ma_p),
plus each channel has orientation and phase degrees of
freedom.  Whether this combinatorics reproduces exactly 8
independent coupling modes is an open question — but the
numbers are in the right range.

### What replaces confinement?

In QCD, confinement means the potential between quarks grows
linearly with distance, making isolation impossible.  Q90 §6
offers an alternative: confinement is destructive
interference.  Sub-modes whose winding numbers don't permit
a closed standing wave on the torus surface cannot propagate
independently — their wave function never closes.  They must
be bound with partners whose phase offsets sum to an integer.

No gluon field is needed to enforce this.  The compact
geometry does it automatically.


## 6. W and Z bosons — transient mode-transition states

The W⁺, W⁻, and Z⁰ mediate the weak force in the SM.  Their
defining role is to change particle identity: a W⁻ converts
an up quark to a down quark (beta decay), or annihilates an
electron-neutrino pair.  The Z⁰ mediates neutral-current
interactions where identity is preserved but momentum is
transferred.

In MaSt, these are **mode transitions** — energy
redistributing from one eigenmode to another, possibly across
material sheets.  Consider neutron beta decay:

    neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino

The neutron is a three-sheet mode (R27 F15–F18) spanning
Ma_e, Ma_ν, and Ma_p.  In the decay, the mode reconfigures:
the Ma_p component settles into the proton eigenmode, the
Ma_e component becomes an electron, and the Ma_ν component
becomes an antineutrino.  The cross-sheet coupling parameters
σ_ep and σ_eν govern the transition rate.

### What is the W boson in this picture?

The W is the **intermediate state** during the transition.
The standing wave is momentarily in a superposition that is
not any single eigenmode — energy is redistributing between
sheets, fields are reconfiguring.  This transient
configuration has a definite energy (80.4 GeV) and spin (1),
and it can be produced and detected at colliders by
supplying enough energy to force the transition.

The W's mass reflects the energy scale at which cross-sheet
coupling becomes significant — the "height of the barrier"
between sheets.  Its short lifetime (3 × 10⁻²⁵ s) reflects
how quickly the transient state resolves into stable
eigenmodes.

### What is the Z boson?

The Z mediates transitions where the particle identity is
preserved but energy and momentum are exchanged.  In MaSt,
this would be a **same-sheet resonance** of the cross-sheet
coupling mechanism — the sheets vibrate against each other
without a permanent mode transition occurring.  The Z's mass
(91.2 GeV) and the W's mass (80.4 GeV) are related by the
Weinberg angle (sin²θ_W ≈ 0.23), which in MaSt might emerge
from the geometric relationship between inter-sheet coupling
and the Compton window.

### The electroweak scale

R34 found a midpoint coupling energy of 310 GeV ≈ 1.26×
the Higgs vacuum expectation value (v = 246 GeV), placing it
at the electroweak scale.  This is suggestive: the Ma
geometry may contain a natural energy scale that corresponds
to the SM's electroweak symmetry breaking — not from a Higgs
field condensate, but from the cross-sheet coupling geometry.


## 7. The Higgs boson — a mode, not a mechanism

In the SM, the Higgs field is unique.  It permeates all of
space, and particles acquire mass by interacting with it.
Without the Higgs mechanism, all SM particles would be
massless (gauge symmetry would forbid mass terms in the
Lagrangian).

### MaSt does not need the Higgs mechanism for mass

In MaSt, mass comes directly from geometry.  A particle's
mass is set by its winding numbers and the sheet's
circumferences:

    m = ℏ / (λ_C · c)

where λ_C is determined by the geodesic path length on the
compact surface.  Mass is intrinsic to the mode's topology,
not "given" by an external field.  No Higgs mechanism is
required.

### But the Higgs boson is real

The Higgs boson at 125 GeV was observed at the LHC in 2012.
It is a real resonance with definite properties: spin 0,
charge 0, mass 125.1 GeV.  MaSt must account for it.

The most natural interpretation: the Higgs is a **scalar
(spin-0) mode** on one of the material sheets.  Spin-0
modes have zero tube winding (n₁ = 0), which produces no
charge — consistent with the Higgs being electrically
neutral.  A (0, n₂) mode on the proton sheet with the
appropriate ring winding would have a mass near 125 GeV.

However, R28 F5 established that above ~2 GeV, the Ma mode
spectrum becomes so dense (band spacing < 5 MeV) that any
mass can be matched to within a few MeV.  The W, Z, and
Higgs all find nearby modes, but trivially so — this is not
a discriminating test.

### What the Higgs "does" in MaSt

The Higgs field's role in the SM — breaking electroweak
symmetry and giving mass to W, Z, and fermions — maps to
the properties of the compact geometry itself in MaSt:

| SM Higgs role | MaSt equivalent |
|---------------|----------------|
| Gives mass to fermions | Mode path length on Ma (geometric) |
| Gives mass to W, Z | Cross-sheet coupling barrier height |
| Breaks electroweak symmetry | Ma geometry selects which modes exist |
| Higgs vev v = 246 GeV | A geometric scale (R34: midpoint at 310 GeV) |
| Higgs self-coupling λ | Moduli potential of the Ma sheets (R37, R40) |

The Higgs boson itself would be the quantized excitation of
the sheet geometry — a ripple in the moduli potential.  R40
is computing this potential; if it has a minimum with
curvature corresponding to 125 GeV, the identification would
be concrete.

R40 also found that n₁ = 0 modes (spin 0, no charge) are
**penalized** by the dynamic torus deformation — the (1,2)
mode's tube winding disfavors modes that don't wind around
the tube.  This would make scalar modes intrinsically
unstable, which matches the Higgs boson's extremely short
lifetime (1.6 × 10⁻²² s).


## 8. The graviton — open territory

The graviton (spin 2, massless, mediates gravity) has never
been observed.  MaSt has not derived gravity from compact
geometry, though the extra-dimensional framework is
reminiscent of Kaluza-Klein theory, which originally unified
electromagnetism and gravity through a fifth dimension.

In standard KK theory, the metric components g_μ5 produce
electromagnetism and g_55 produces a scalar (the dilaton).
MaSt has six compact dimensions, giving potentially many
more metric components.  Whether the graviton emerges as a
massless spin-2 excitation of the ten-dimensional metric is
a major open question — one that would connect MaSt to
string theory's graviton.


## 9. Summary: one field, many appearances

The SM needs two categories of particles: fermions (matter)
and bosons (force carriers), plus the Higgs to give mass.
MaSt has **one category** — electromagnetic eigenmodes on
compact geometry — and forces arise from how those modes'
fields interact at different distance scales.

| SM boson | SM role | MaSt reinterpretation |
|----------|---------|----------------------|
| Photon (γ) | EM force carrier | EM energy in S — same physics, no change |
| 8 gluons (g) | Strong force carriers | Not needed — direct Ma field coupling (Q95) |
| W⁺, W⁻ | Weak force / transmutation | Transient state during cross-sheet mode transition |
| Z⁰ | Weak neutral current | Cross-sheet coupling resonance without mode change |
| Higgs (H) | Gives mass to particles | A scalar (0, n₂) mode; mass is geometric, not from H |
| Graviton | Gravity carrier | Open — not yet derived |

The "force-carrying boson" is not a fundamental concept in
MaSt.  Forces are field coupling.  The SM's bosons are
either unnecessary (gluons), transient intermediate states
(W, Z), specific eigenmodes misidentified as mechanisms
(Higgs), or the field itself (photon).

This does not mean the SM's predictions are wrong — QED,
QCD, and electroweak theory are extraordinarily precise
calculational frameworks.  But the ontology is different.
MaSt says the universe has one field (electromagnetic),
one geometry (ten-dimensional, with six compact), and
everything else — particles, forces, masses, charges — is
how that field behaves in that geometry.
