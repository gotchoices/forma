# Q05: Can a compact dimension be orthogonal to xyz if it fits inside the uncertainty radius?

**Status:** Answered  
**Source:** User question  
**Related:** [Q07](Q07-flat-compact-dimensions.md), [Q13](Q13-three-compact-dimensions.md)

**Short answer:** Yes. Orthogonality is a property of the metric, not of size.
A compact dimension of radius R ≪ Δx is still metrically orthogonal to the
extended dimensions; no physical interaction forces it to align.

---

## Question

If a compactified dimension has circumference L smaller than the position
uncertainty of the particle it hosts, can it still be considered toroidal and
genuinely orthogonal to the three extended spatial dimensions?

The concern: if the compact space is "inside" the particle in some sense,
does it get dragged along with the particle's extended-space position and
lose its independent character?

## Answer

**Orthogonality is metric, not positional.** In Kaluza-Klein geometry the
full spacetime is a product (or fibre bundle) M₄ × K, where K is the
compact space. The metric separates:

    ds² = g_μν dx^μ dx^ν + g_ab dy^a dy^b

The cross-terms g_μa vanish (or are small — they are the KK gauge field).
This separation is exact regardless of the size of K relative to the
uncertainty in x^μ. "Orthogonal" means ∂/∂y^a is everywhere perpendicular
to ∂/∂x^μ in the metric sense; it has nothing to do with whether L < Δx.

**Size and orthogonality are independent.** The uncertainty principle bounds
how precisely we can *locate* a particle in x^μ, but it says nothing about
the internal coordinates y^a. The compact dimension is not "inside" the
particle in a spatial nesting sense — it is an independent direction that
exists at every point of M₄, including the point where the particle is
localized.

**Standard KK result.** This is the standard picture in Kaluza-Klein theory
and string compactification. Extra dimensions can have circumference as small
as the Planck length (~10⁻³⁵ m), orders of magnitude below any experimental
resolution, and remain fully orthogonal. The Compton-scale compact space
(R ~ 10⁻¹³ m) in our model is actually *large* by that standard, but the
same logic applies.

**No confinement mechanism required.** A separate concern is whether some
mechanism is needed to keep the particle "on" the compact space. It is not:
the particle (photon on a geodesic) simply follows the intrinsic geometry of
M₄ × K. There is no confinement force any more than there is a force keeping
light on a straight line in flat space.

## Summary

| Concern | Resolution |
|---------|-----------|
| L < Δx suggests compact space is "inside" the particle | Size and orthogonality are independent concepts |
| Compact space might be dragged by the particle's position | Compact coordinates y^a are independent of x^μ by construction |
| Orthogonality might require a separation mechanism | Metric orthogonality needs no mechanism; it is a property of the geometry |
