# Why the neutrino is necessary

The neutrino is not an optional feature of the particle model.  It is
forced by angular momentum conservation.  An intelligent designer
building the periodic table from T² compact dimensions cannot substitute
photons or any other boson — the neutrino (or something with its
quantum numbers) must exist.

## The spin constraint

Beta decay converts a neutron into a proton plus an electron:

    n → p + e⁻ + ?

Count the spins:

| Particle | Spin | Type |
|----------|------|------|
| Neutron (before) | ½ | fermion |
| Proton (after) | ½ | fermion |
| Electron (after) | ½ | fermion |

The proton and electron combine to total spin 0 or 1 — always
**integer**.  The neutron had spin **½** — half-integer.  To recover
half-integer total angular momentum in the final state, the missing
particle must carry half-integer spin.

A photon has spin 1 (integer).  Adding any number of photons to an
integer-spin state gives integer spin.  No combination of photons can
close the angular momentum budget.

**The decay must emit a fermion.**

The neutrino is the cheapest fermion available: no charge, minimal
mass, spin ½.  It is the minimum addition required by the topology of
angular momentum.

## In T² language

In the compact-dimension model:

- The electron is a (1,2) winding mode — spin ½ from the 1:2
  winding ratio (topological, exact).
- The proton is a (1,2) fundamental plus uncharged harmonics —
  spin ½ overall.
- The neutron is two opposite-charge fundamentals (+1,2) and (−1,2)
  plus harmonics — spin ½ overall.

In beta decay, one fundamental escapes as the electron.  The winding
number bookkeeping closes: 2 windings before = 1 (proton) + 1
(electron) after.  Charge closes: 0 = +e + (−e).

But spin does not close with just these two products.  Both are
spin ½, so their combined spin is integer.  The initial neutron
spin ½ is half-integer.  **A third fermion is required regardless
of the model's details** — it follows from the fact that all three
named participants are (1,2) winding modes with spin ½.

## Why not just radiate the energy?

The neutron-proton mass difference is 1.293 MeV.  After producing the
electron (0.511 MeV), there is 0.782 MeV of kinetic energy to
distribute.  One might ask: why not emit this as a photon?

The answer is not about energy — it is about angular momentum.  A
photon carries integer spin.  The decay products (p + e⁻) already
have integer total spin.  Adding a photon keeps the total at integer
spin.  The neutron's spin ½ cannot be recovered.  The energy
*could* be carried by a photon in principle, but the angular momentum
*cannot*.

This is why Pauli originally postulated the neutrino in 1930 — not
because energy was missing (that could have been radiation), but
because spin was missing.  The continuous beta spectrum confirmed a
three-body decay, but the *necessity* of the third body is spin.

## Implications for the T³ model

R24 Track 1 proposes neutrinos as (0,0,n₃) modes on T³ — pure
oscillations in the third compact dimension.  The spin constraint
places a hard requirement on this proposal:

**The (0,0,n₃) mode must carry spin ½.**

In the WvM picture, spin ½ arises from the (1,2) winding topology.
A pure θ₃ mode has no such winding.  If T³ is a simple direct
product T² × S¹, these modes are scalar (spin 0) — and the model
fails.

For the T³ neutrino to work, the compact space must have non-trivial
fibration: twisted boundary conditions or topological structure that
endows θ₃ modes with spinor character.  This is R24 open question Q1
and is a gate for the entire neutrino program.

The neutrino's existence is not negotiable — spin conservation demands
it.  The question is whether T³ can provide it.
