# R47 Review: The proton mode question — (1,2), (1,3), or (3,6)?

Independent review of the proton mode hypothesis, conducted
after R50 Tracks 1-4 exposed the composite charge formula
problem.

---

## The charge formula problem

Model-D introduced the composite charge formula
Q = −n₁ + n₅/gcd(|n₅|, |n₆|) to make the (3,6) proton
give Q = +1 instead of the +3 that the fundamental formula
Q = −n₁ + n₅ produces.

This formula works for the proton.  It breaks for nuclei:

| Nucleus | n₅ | n₆ | gcd | Q_fundamental | Q_composite | Correct Q |
|---------|----|----|-----|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| p | 3 | 6 | 3 | +3 | +1 | +1 |
| d | 2 | 4 | 2 | +1 | 0 | +1 |
| ⁴He | 4 | 8 | 4 | +2 | −1 | +2 |
| ¹²C | 12 | 24 | 12 | +6 | −5 | +6 |

The composite formula treats every mode as a quark
composite (dividing by gcd), which gives the wrong charge
for collective nuclear modes.  The fundamental formula
works for nuclei but gives Q = +3 for the (3,6) proton.

**This is a structural inconsistency:** the model needs
different charge formulas for different kinds of modes.
Any system that requires knowing what a mode "is" before
computing its charge has a classification problem that
undermines the model's ambition of deriving everything
from quantum numbers alone.


## The (1,3) proton — reassessment

### Why (1,3) was rejected

R47 Track 1 nullified (1,3) because spin = n₁/n₂ = 1/3,
not ½.  The proton has spin ½.  Track 1 was declared null
and the (1,3) hypothesis was abandoned.

### Why the rejection is no longer valid

Model-D (R50 Track 1 F5) established that physical spin
is **topological**: odd tube winding → spin ½.  The WvM
ratio formula (spin = n₁/n₂) has been superseded.

For (1,3) on Ma_p: n₅ = 1 (odd) → spin ½ ✓.

The topological spin rule was not available when R47
Track 1 was conducted.  Under current rules, the
nullification is invalid.  (1,3) should be re-evaluated.

### The spin subtlety

The two spin rules give different answers for (1,3):

| Rule | Formula | (1,2) result | (1,3) result |
|------|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Topological (model-D) | odd tube → ½ | ½ ✓ | ½ ✓ |
| WvM ratio (model-C) | n₅/n₆ | 1/2 ✓ | 1/3 ✗ |
| Classical L_z (path integral) | ∫ ... dθ | ~0.53ℏ | ~0.33ℏ |

For the (1,2) electron and the (1,2) proton (model-C),
the topological rule and the WvM ratio agree: both give ½.
For (1,3), they **disagree**.  This disagreement is unique
to (1,3) among the candidate proton modes.

The topological rule is the one that determines quantum
spin — it always gives half-integers, as spin must be.
The WvM ratio and the classical L_z are continuous
geometric quantities that reflect the geodesic's orbital
angular momentum projection, not the quantum spin.  R50
F5 established this distinction clearly.

**However:** the fact that (1,2) modes have both rules
agreeing — while (1,3) has them disagreeing — is worth
noting.  If the WvM ratio captures something physically
real about the mode's angular structure (precession rates,
coupling to magnetic fields, scattering geometry), the
disagreement might show up in some observable.

This is not a disqualification.  The quantum spin IS ½
by the topological rule.  But it is a difference between
(1,3) and (1,2)/(3,6) that should be watched for
experimental consequences.  If the proton's magnetic
moment, for instance, depends on the classical L_z rather
than purely on the topological spin, the (1,3) value of
~0.33ℏ vs the (1,2)/(3,6) value of ~0.53ℏ would produce
different predictions.

### (1,2) — the original model-C proton

**Strengths:** simplest mode. Same structure as electron.
Fundamental charge formula works everywhere.

**Weaknesses:** bare moment = 2 μ_N, needs +40% correction
to reach 2.793.  No quark structure.  The anomaly is in the
wrong direction AND large (non-perturbative).  Model-C
pinned r_p = 8.906 and σ_ep = −0.091 from the neutron and
muon, which hid problems now exposed by model-D.

### (1,3) — the resurrected candidate

**Strengths:**
- Charge formula works without any kludge (Q = +1 from
  fundamental formula, gcd = 1)
- Nuclear scaling works cleanly: n₅ = A, n₆ = 3A, charge
  Q = −n₁ + A = Z.  One formula for all modes.
- Spin ½ from topological rule (n₅ = 1, odd)
- Bare moment ~3 μ_N, needs only −7% correction to reach
  2.793.  This is a perturbative correction (compare
  electron: bare = 2, correction = +0.12% = α/2π).  A −7%
  correction is much larger than α/2π but much smaller than
  the +40% (1,2) requires.
- The (1,2) mode on Ma_p becomes a ghost that the waveguide
  kills — the proton is the FIRST SURVIVING charged mode,
  just as the electron (1,2) on Ma_e is the first surviving
  mode after the (1,1) ghost is killed
- Simplicity: fundamental mode, same charge formula as
  everything else, no composite machinery

**Weaknesses:**
- No quarks.  gcd(1,3) = 1 — irreducible.  No three-strand
  decomposition, no constituent quark mass, no confinement
  from filtering.
- Deep inelastic scattering shows three point-like
  constituents.  The (1,3) mode has no substructure to
  scatter off.  This is the strongest experimental argument
  against (1,3).
- Requires the (1,2) ghost to be killed by the waveguide
  at ε_p ~ 0.33.  This is a tighter constraint than
  (1,2)-as-proton (where no proton-sheet ghost exists).

### (3,6) — the quark composite

**Strengths:**
- Quarks emerge naturally: gcd = 3 → three (1,2) strands
  at 120°
- Constituent quark mass m_p/3 = 313 MeV falls out of the
  sheet geometry
- Confinement from ring cutoff: (1,2) quarks are below the
  filter threshold
- DIS structure explained: three strands scatter
  independently at high momentum
- Magnetic moment ~3 μ_N (same as (1,3))

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires the composite charge formula (a kludge)
- Composite charge formula breaks for nuclei
- Creates the standalone (1,2) ghost at ~313 MeV that
  passes the waveguide — the confinement problem (R50 F3)
- Requires mode-type-dependent charge calculation (must
  know whether a mode is a "composite" before computing
  its charge)
- More complex than necessary if quarks can be explained
  differently


## The quark question

The initial concern: (1,3) is cleaner but has no quarks.
(3,6) has quarks but needs a charge formula kludge.

On closer analysis, **(1,3) DOES have quarks** — but
through wave mechanics rather than algebraic decomposition.

### Quarks as antinodes

The (1,3) standing wave has phase ∝ cos(θ₅ + 3θ₆).  At
any fixed tube position, the ring direction has **three
energy peaks** (antinodes) at 0°, 120°, and 240°.  These
are three localized concentrations of energy on the torus
surface.

A high-energy probe (DIS) with wavelength much shorter
than the torus circumference resolves these three peaks
as three separate scattering centers.  From outside, they
look like three point-like constituents — quarks — even
though they are peaks of one irreducible mode.

**Each antinode carries:**
- **~1/3 of the mass** — the energy is distributed among
  three peaks, each at ~313 MeV ≈ m_p/3 (the constituent
  quark mass)
- **~1/3 of the charge** — the charge density on the torus
  has three-fold symmetry, so each peak carries 1/3 of the
  net Gauss flux

The total charge is +1 (from n₅ = 1, fundamental formula).
The three-fold distribution of that charge emerges from the
standing wave pattern, not from the charge formula.  No
composite formula needed.

### How this differs from (3,6) quarks

| Property | (3,6) quarks | (1,3) quarks |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Mechanism | Algebraic: gcd = 3 → 3 strands | Wave: 3 ring windings → 3 antinodes |
| Independence | Strands are independent sub-modes | Antinodes are features of one mode |
| Fractional charge | From composite formula (n₅/gcd) | From charge density distribution |
| Quark mass ~313 MeV | From strand energy E(1,2)/3 | From antinode energy E(1,3)/3 |
| Confinement | Strands killed by waveguide | Antinodes can't separate (one mode) |
| Charge formula | Composite (kludge) | Fundamental (no kludge) |

The key advantage of (1,3) quarks: **confinement is
automatic**.  The antinodes cannot separate because they
are not independent modes — they are features of one
standing wave.  Destroying one antinode destroys the
entire (1,3) mode.  There is no "free quark" problem
because there is no free antinode.

With (3,6), confinement required an additional mechanism
(the waveguide cutoff killing standalone (1,2) quarks).
But this created a tension: the (1,2) strand that makes
the (3,6) composite propagate also propagates alone
(R50 F3).  The (1,3) mode has no such tension — the
antinodes are not modes and cannot propagate at all.

### Supporting observations

1. **Constituent quark mass from the geometry.**  The
   (1,2) ghost mode on Ma_p has energy ~m_p/3 ≈ 313 MeV.
   This is a lattice property (the energy of the lowest
   charged ghost on the sheet), not a substructure property.
   It exists regardless of whether the proton is (1,2),
   (1,3), or (3,6).  The "quark mass" is what you get
   when you probe the sheet at one step below the proton.

2. **Three ring passes per Compton wavelength.**  The (1,3)
   geodesic traverses the ring three times for every tube
   circuit.  Each pass deposits energy in one 120° sector.
   The three passes ARE the three quarks — three visits to
   three locations, creating three energy concentrations.

3. **The neutron's standing wave would show the same
   pattern** — three antinodes on the proton ring, shifted
   in position and amplitude by the cross-sheet coupling.
   The neutron's quarks (udd vs uud) would correspond to
   a different charge distribution across the three peaks.

### What remains to be checked

These observations are qualitative.  To validate (1,3)
quarks quantitatively:

- Compute the charge density ρ(θ₅, θ₆) on the (1,3)
  torus and verify three-fold symmetry
- Determine whether the three peaks carry charges +2/3,
  +2/3, −1/3 (uud for the proton) or some other
  distribution
- Compute the form factor F(Q²) from the antinode
  structure and compare to measured proton form factors
- Check whether the Bjorken sum rule and DIS scaling
  violations are reproduced by a three-antinode model


## The parallel to the electron sheet

There is a suggestive structural parallel:

| Property | Ma_e | Ma_p (if (1,3)) |
|----------|------|-----------------|
| Ghost killed | (1,1) | (1,2) |
| First surviving charged mode | (1,2) = electron | (1,3) = proton |
| Waveguide cutoff selects | n₂ ≥ 2 | n₆ ≥ 3 |
| Ring windings of first mode | 2 | 3 |
| Charge | −1 (from n₁ = 1) | +1 (from n₅ = 1) |
| Spin | ½ (from odd n₁) | ½ (from odd n₅) |

The electron is the first surviving mode after the (1,1)
ghost is killed.  The proton would be the first surviving
mode after the (1,2) ghost is killed.  Same mechanism,
different sheet, different cutoff.  The different cutoff
(n₂ ≥ 2 vs n₆ ≥ 3) reflects different aspect ratios
(ε_e ≈ 0.65 vs ε_p ≈ 0.33).

This parallel is absent for (3,6): the proton as a
composite doesn't follow the same pattern as the electron
as a fundamental.


## Recommendation

**(1,3) should be tested alongside (3,6) in R50 Track 5
and R51.**  The charge formula problem is serious enough
to warrant a parallel investigation.

The test is straightforward:
- Run Track 5 nuclear scaling with (1,3) proton
  (n₅ = A, n₆ = 3A) and fundamental charge formula
- Run R51 hydrogen mode search with (1,3) proton
- Compare mass matches, charge correctness, and
  consistency against the (3,6) results

If (1,3) matches nuclear masses AND avoids the charge
formula problem, it may be the preferred proton mode
despite the loss of quark substructure.  The quark
question would then become: "why does the proton LOOK
like it has three constituents in DIS?" rather than "why
does the proton's mode decompose into three strands?"

**The decision criterion:** whichever mode gives correct
charge, spin, mass, and nuclear scaling with ONE charge
formula (no mode-type switching) is preferred.  If both
work, prefer the simpler one (Occam's razor → (1,3)).
If neither works without a kludge, we have a deeper
problem.


## Impact on other work

If (1,3) is adopted:

- **R50 Track 3 results** — most baryon matches would
  need recomputation (different L_ring_p from different
  proton eigenvalue).  The Ω⁻ mechanism (composite
  electron winding, F22) is independent of the proton
  mode and would survive.

- **R47 Tracks 4-7** — Track 1 nullification is reversed.
  Tracks 4 (spindle) and 6 ((3,6) confinement) become
  less relevant.  Track 7 comparison table needs updating.

- **The white paper** — already has a model-era note.
  Would need an additional note that the proton mode is
  under reassessment.

- **Q89 (fusion)** — the neutron mode candidates would
  change (different proton ring spacing).  The σ values
  and the bootstrap pathway are independent of the proton
  mode.

- **Q105 (atoms as modes)** — the cross-sheet eigenvalue
  calculation depends on the proton mode.  Different
  proton modes give different coupled eigenvalues.

- **charge-from-energy primer** — §9 currently presents
  (3,6) as "believed to be" the proton mode.  Would need
  softening to present both candidates.
