# The Emergent Neutron

## What this article is about

The T⁶ model was built around three stable particles: the
electron, three neutrino mass eigenstates, and the proton.  Each
gets its own two-dimensional sheet (a T²) in a six-dimensional
compact space.  The neutron was not put in.  It appeared on its
own — a natural vibration of the combined geometry with the right
mass, zero charge, and a built-in explanation for why it decays
into exactly the particles we started with.

This article explains what that means and why it matters.
Technical details are in R26 findings F64–F69 and F74.


## Background: what the model is

The starting premise is simple: **particles are confined light.**
A photon trapped on a closed surface — a flat torus with periodic
boundaries — behaves like a massive particle.  Its energy becomes
mass.  Its winding pattern determines charge and spin.  No
fundamental charges or point particles are assumed.  The only
inputs are energy (photons), geometry (the shape of compact
dimensions), and the rules of propagation (Maxwell's equations).

An electron, in this picture, is a photon winding once around the
tube and twice around the ring of a small torus (T²).  That (1,2)
winding gives spin ½ exactly (topological, not approximate) and
produces an electric charge −e from the field's asymmetric
projection into ordinary 3D space.  The torus's size is set by
the electron's Compton wavelength (~386 pm).

A proton is the same idea on a much smaller torus (~1.3 fm), with
the same (1,2) winding giving spin ½ and charge +e.  Its greater
mass comes from its smaller geometry: more energy is needed to
fit a photon into a tighter space.

The three neutrino mass eigenstates live on a third, much larger
torus (~mm scale), with different winding patterns that produce
zero charge and the observed mass-squared splittings.

All three tori are embedded as subplanes of a single
six-dimensional compact space, T⁶.  Think of it as three sheets
of paper that share a common 6D room.


## How the neutron appears

When the three sheets are perfectly perpendicular to each other
(mathematically: zero cross-shear), they are completely
independent.  A wave on the electron sheet stays on the electron
sheet.  A wave on the proton sheet stays on the proton sheet.
The allowed vibrations are exactly the particles we put in:
electron, neutrinos, proton.

But the sheets don't have to be perfectly perpendicular.  If the
electron sheet and the proton sheet are tilted by even a tiny
amount — say 2.2° from perpendicular — new vibrations become
possible.  A wave can ring across both sheets simultaneously.

The neutron is exactly this: **a single standing wave whose
vibration pattern is (1,2) on the electron sheet AND (1,2) on
the proton sheet at the same time.**  In T⁶ coordinates, its
quantum numbers are (1,2,0,0,1,2) — the electron's signature
in the first pair, nothing in the neutrino pair, the proton's
signature in the last pair.

It is not two waves glued together.  It is one wave that spans
both sheets, the way a vibration can run across two drumheads
connected by a thin bridge.


## Why it has the right properties

**Zero charge.**  The electron's (1,2) pattern on its sheet
generates charge −e.  The proton's (1,2) pattern on its sheet
generates charge +e.  The neutron's single wave carries both
patterns: −e + e = 0.  This cancellation is not imposed — it is
automatic, a consequence of the same mechanism that creates
charge in the first place.

**The right mass.**  At zero tilt, the neutron's energy is
√(m_e² + m_p²) ≈ 938.272 MeV — essentially the proton mass
(the electron's contribution is negligible).  Any nonzero tilt
increases the energy quadratically: the wave has to "stretch"
across the angle between sheets, and stretching costs energy.  At
a tilt parameter of |σ_ep| ≈ 0.038 (2.2° from perpendicular),
the energy reaches 939.565 MeV — the measured neutron mass.

This tilt is geometrically modest: 7.1% of the maximum allowed
before the metric becomes inconsistent.  The geometry is barely
disturbed.

**Natural instability.**  The neutron decays (in about 15 minutes)
into a proton + electron + antineutrino.  In T⁶ language, the
cross-sheet wave (1,2,0,0,1,2) can relax: it splits into the
proton wave (0,0,0,0,1,2) on its own sheet, the electron wave
(1,2,0,0,0,0) on its sheet, and the leftover energy goes into
neutrino modes.  The coupling between sheets is weak (only 2.2°
of tilt), so the process is slow — hence the long lifetime.

**Naturally heavier than the proton.**  In the standard model,
m_n > m_p is a fine-tuned fact with enormous consequences (it
allows free neutron decay, prevents free proton decay, and makes
nuclear physics possible).  Here it is geometric: any nonzero
coupling between the sheets makes the cross-sheet wave heavier.
The neutron is heavier than the proton for the same reason that
a vibration spanning two drumheads has higher frequency than one
on a single drumhead.  It couldn't be otherwise.


## What is significant about predicting a neutron

There are only four particles that make up essentially all
ordinary matter: electron, proton, neutron, and (for nuclear
reactions and stellar processes) neutrinos.  The model was
designed around three of them.  The fourth showed up uninvited.

More specifically:

1. **The mode was not sought.**  Nobody went looking for a neutron
   in the T⁶ spectrum.  The mode (1,2,0,0,1,2) is just the most
   natural cross-sheet vibration — the simplest pattern that
   involves both the electron and proton sheets.

2. **Its properties are not adjustable.**  Given the electron and
   proton masses (which set the sheet sizes), the neutron's charge
   (zero) and approximate mass (~m_p) are fixed.  Only the precise
   mass difference m_n − m_p depends on the tilt parameter.

3. **Its decay products are exactly the input particles.**  The
   neutron decays to proton + electron + neutrino.  These are the
   three particles the T⁶ was built around.  The decay is the
   cross-sheet wave relaxing back into single-sheet waves.

4. **It explains why m_n > m_p.**  This fact is not a coincidence
   in the model; it is a geometric necessity.

The neutron is the first particle the T⁶ model produces that was
not put in by hand.  Even if the quantitative mass prediction
needs refinement (a self-consistency correction is required;
see R26 F74), the qualitative result — a charge-neutral,
slightly-heavier-than-proton particle that decays into exactly
e + p + ν — is striking.


## Open questions about the neutron

**Self-consistent mass.**  The initial calculation (F67) assumed
that the cross-shear doesn't shift the electron and proton masses.
It does, slightly (F74).  A self-consistent treatment that
re-derives the torus sizes at each cross-shear value is needed
before m_n − m_p can be quantitatively claimed.  The qualitative
result (m_n > m_p, charge = 0) is robust.

**Spin.**  The mode (1,2,0,0,1,2) has two odd tube windings
(n₁ = 1 and n₅ = 1), each contributing spin-½.  Naively two
spin-½ contributions combine to spin-0 or spin-1, but the
neutron is spin-½.  The resolution likely lies in the SO(6)
spinor structure on the full T⁶ — the spin composition rules
in six dimensions differ from naive three-dimensional addition.
This is analyzed in R26 F68.

**The sign of σ_ep.**  Both positive and negative tilt give the
same neutron mass (the energy correction is quadratic).  This
discrete ambiguity — two geometrically distinct T⁶ configurations
producing the same neutron — may relate to charge conjugation
symmetry.


## What the neutron tells us about the model

The neutron is a proof of concept.  It demonstrates that the T⁶
is not just a bookkeeping device for three separate tori — the
cross-sheet coupling produces genuinely new physics.  The
geometry does more than host the particles we designed it for.

This raises an immediate question: what else does the T⁶
spectrum contain?  If the simplest cross-sheet mode is the
neutron, what are the next-simplest modes?  Could they correspond
to other known particles?  Every such match would constrain the
free parameters of the model, and enough matches would determine
the geometry completely.  These questions are the subject of
studies R27 and R28.
