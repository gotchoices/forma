# Threshold Theory and the Unquantum Effect

Eric Reiter's threshold model proposes that the quantization we
observe in experiments is not a fundamental property of nature
but an illusion created by threshold detectors acting on
classical waves.  This primer explains the theory, catalogs the
experimental claims, and examines where the ideas touch — and
diverge from — our T⁶ model of particles as confined light.

The final section explores a speculative but physically grounded
possibility: that the T⁶ model may *contain* a threshold-like
mechanism, arising from the natural tendency of energy in
coupled oscillating systems to cascade into the fundamental mode.

---

## 1. What is threshold theory?

### The standard quantum picture (what Reiter challenges)

In orthodox quantum mechanics, energy is emitted and absorbed in
discrete packets.  A photon of energy E = hν is indivisible: at
a beam splitter, it goes one way or the other — never both.  A
detector clicks because a single quantum delivered all of its
energy at once.  The fundamental constants h (Planck's constant),
e (electron charge), and m_e (electron mass) represent
indivisible quanta: you cannot have half a photon, half a charge,
or half an electron mass.

### The threshold reinterpretation

Reiter proposes replacing quantization with thresholding.  The
distinction is subtle but consequential:

- **Quantization** says: the smallest unit of action is h, the
  smallest unit of charge is e, the smallest unit of electron
  mass is m_e.  Nothing smaller exists.

- **Thresholding** says: h, e, and m_e are *maxima* — threshold
  values that matter accumulates toward.  Below threshold,
  sub-h action, sub-e charge, and sub-m_e mass can exist in free
  space.  But our instruments never see them, because our
  instruments *also* operate by thresholds: a detector soaks up
  energy gradually, then fires when it crosses a threshold, giving
  the appearance of receiving an indivisible quantum.

In Reiter's framing, quantization is a *subset* of thresholding.
Most experiments that appear to confirm quantization are equally
consistent with thresholding.  The two frameworks make different
predictions only in specific situations — which is where Reiter's
experiments come in.


### The three pillars

Reiter's threshold model rests on three principles:

**Pillar 1 — The loading theory (continuous absorption,
explosive emission).**  This is not a new idea.  Planck
himself proposed it in 1911 in what historians call "Planck's
second theory."  The idea: energy is emitted in quantized bursts
(E = hν at the instant of emission), but after emission the wave
spreads classically — no photons, no particles in transit.  When
the spreading wave encounters a detector, the detector absorbs
energy continuously, gradually filling an internal reservoir,
until a threshold is reached.  Then the detector fires
explosively — producing a "click" that looks exactly like a
single quantum arriving all at once.

The critical concept is the **pre-loaded state**: a detector that
has already absorbed some sub-threshold energy from previous
waves.  When the next pulse arrives, it only needs to supply the
*remainder* to reach threshold.  This explains why detectors
respond faster than a "start from zero" loading calculation would
predict, and why (Reiter claims) two detectors can fire in
coincidence from a single emitted pulse.

Planck wrote (1911): emission is quantized, but absorption is
continuous.  Lenard (1902) proposed a "trigger hypothesis" for
the photoelectric effect.  Sommerfeld and Debye (1913) explored
an electron spiraling inward during resonant absorption.
Millikan (1947) described the loading theory but called it
"terribly difficult to conceive."  Reiter argues that physics
abandoned the loading theory prematurely, partly because
textbooks compared the *shortest* observed response time against
the *longest* calculated loading time — an unfair test.

**Pillar 2 — Threshold constants.**  The constants h, e, and m_e
are reinterpreted as maxima rather than indivisible quanta:

| Constant | Orthodox QM | Threshold model |
|----------|------------|-----------------|
| h (Planck) | Minimum quantum of action | Maximum threshold of action |
| e (charge) | Indivisible charge unit | Threshold charge value |
| m_e (mass) | Point-particle mass | Threshold mass of charge-wave |

Below threshold, sub-h action and sub-e charge can exist in free
space, carried by classical waves.  The constants become upper
bounds rather than building blocks.

**Pillar 3 — The ratio principle.**  This is what Reiter
considers his most important insight.  The key experiments that
established quantum mechanics — the photoelectric effect, the
Compton effect, Thomson's e/m measurement — do not measure h, e,
or m_e individually.  They measure *ratios*:

- **e/m** — Thomson's electron deflection experiment
- **h/m** — Compton effect, de Broglie wavelength
- **e/h** — photoelectric effect, Aharonov-Bohm effect

Reiter's argument: if a charge-wave spreads out in space and
thins, with charge, mass, and action all decreasing
proportionally, the ratios are conserved.  Our experiments see
only these ratios and therefore cannot distinguish between
quantized and sub-quantized amounts.

Quoting Reiter: "Visualize a small sample volume of that initial
emission and realize that its e/m ratio is conserved.  In that
sample volume, let us imagine a hundredth of the original e and a
hundredth of the original m.  Now realize those hundreds will
cancel to deliver the ratio e/m, the way our experiments read."

The one class of experiments that *does* measure e independently
(not as a ratio) is the Millikan oil-drop experiment and its
relatives.  But these experiments use macroscopic masses (oil
drops, atoms in bulk), where an ensemble threshold effect can
rally charges toward multiples of e regardless of whether free-
space charge is truly quantized.

## 2. The unquantum effect (experimental claims)

### The beam-split coincidence test

The experimental centerpiece of Reiter's program is a beam-split
coincidence test.  The logic:

1. A radioactive source emits one gamma ray at a time.
2. The gamma ray encounters a beam splitter (or passes through
   one detector toward a second).
3. Two detectors monitor both paths.
4. Count coincidences — events where both detectors fire within
   a narrow time window.

**QM prediction:** A single photon goes one way or the other.
The only coincidences are random (chance), occurring at rate:

    R_c = R₁ × R₂ × τ

where R₁ and R₂ are the singles rates and τ is the coincidence
window.

**Loading theory prediction:** The classical wave splits at the
beam splitter.  Each half can load a pre-loaded detector to
threshold, producing coincidences above the chance rate.

Reiter reports coincidence rates *far* exceeding chance:

| Configuration | Source | R_e/R_c | Notes |
|--------------|--------|---------|-------|
| Tandem NaI detectors | ¹⁰⁹Cd (88 keV γ) | 33.5 | "July 5, 2004 test" |
| Triple coincidence | ²²Na (511 keV pair) | 963 | Annihilation γ pair |
| Alpha split through gold leaf | ²⁴¹Am (5.5 MeV α) | 105 | Matter wave splitting |
| HPGe scatter off Ge | ¹⁰⁹Cd (88 keV γ) | 206 | Beam-split geometry |
| Tandem, tight SCA | ⁵⁷Co | ~190 | Tightened discriminator |

(R_e = measured coincidence rate, R_c = calculated chance rate.)

### Conditions where the effect appears (and doesn't)

The unquantum effect is not universal.  Reiter reports:

- **Works** with ¹⁰⁹Cd (88 keV), ⁵⁷Co (122 keV), ²²Na (511
  keV annihilation) — all cases where the detector's
  photoelectric efficiency exceeds its Compton efficiency.
- **Does not work** with ¹³⁷Cs (662 keV), where Compton
  scattering dominates.  This null result is consistent with
  Reiter's model: Compton scattering recoils the electron,
  disrupting the pre-loaded state.
- **Does not work** with ²⁴¹Am gamma rays (59 keV from alpha
  decay) — despite high photoelectric efficiency.  Reiter
  attributes this to the temporal properties of alpha-decay
  gamma emission.
- **Sensitive to temperature**: liquid-nitrogen-cooled aluminum
  scatterer produces ~50% more coincidences (coherent
  Rayleigh scattering enhanced by reduced thermal motion).
- **Sensitive to chemical state**: ¹⁰⁹Cd in salt form produces
  5× more coincidences than in metallic form (attributed to
  greater coherence from crystalline order).
- **Sensitive to distance**: falls off as the classical wave
  spreads.

### The alpha-ray split

The ²⁴¹Am alpha-split experiment is particularly striking.  An
alpha particle (helium nucleus, 5.5 MeV kinetic energy) passes
through gold leaf.  Two silicon surface-barrier detectors record
coincidences at 105× chance.  The pulse-height pairs show that
most splits are near half-height (energy roughly divided
between the two detectors), but six events clearly exceed
particle-energy conservation — both detectors registering more
than half the original energy.  Even counting only those six,
the rate exceeds chance by 4×.

This matters because:
- At 5.5 MeV, there is not enough energy to split a helium
  nucleus into components (~7 MeV/nucleon needed).
- The events exceeding energy conservation cannot be explained
  by particle splitting.
- Reiter interprets this as a helium matter-wave splitting
  classically and loading pre-loaded detectors.


### Status and caveats

The experimental claims are published in:
- *Progress in Physics* (2014) — peer-reviewed, but not a
  mainstream high-impact journal
- *Physics Essays* (2022) — peer-reviewed
- viXra (2012) — open preprint, no peer review
- SPIE conference proceedings (2015)
- Several self-published detailed reports

**No independent replication** by another research group has been
reported as of the material surveyed.  The claims, if confirmed,
would overturn a century of quantum mechanics.  The absence of
replication is the single most important caveat.  Reiter
attributes this to the narrow experimental window (specific
isotopes, specific detector types, specific geometries) and to
institutional resistance.

The work should be treated as an interesting open claim deserving
of independent testing, not as established physics.

## 3. The modified de Broglie wavelength

Reiter proposes a theoretical framework to accompany the
experiments.  Starting from the photoelectric effect equation
(ignoring work function):

    hν_L = ½ m_e v²

where ν_L is light frequency, he introduces a charge-wave beat
model.  Rather than treating the electron as a probability wave
(de Broglie's interpretation), he treats it as a physical
charge-wave with an internal beat structure.

The Balmer equation of hydrogen (ν_L = ν_{Ψ2} − ν_{Ψ1}) tells
us that light frequency equals a *difference frequency* of charge
oscillations.  Reiter makes this fundamental: light always
couples to charge through beats.  Since there are two beats per
modulator wave:

    2ν_L = ν_g

where ν_g is the group (beat) frequency of the charge-wave.
Substituting into the photoelectric equation and using ν_g = v_g/λ_g
yields:

    λ_g = h / (m_e v_g)

This looks identical to de Broglie's equation but has a different
physical interpretation:

| | de Broglie | Reiter |
|---|----------|--------|
| λ | Phase wavelength of probability wave | Wavelength of charge-wave beat envelope |
| m | Point-particle mass | Threshold mass of spreading charge-wave |
| v | Particle velocity | Group velocity of charge-wave |
| ψ | Probability amplitude | Physical charge distribution |

In Reiter's version, the constants become Q-ratios (Q for
quotient): Q_{h/m} = h/m, where both numerator and denominator
can be sub-threshold in free space but the ratio is conserved.
The photoelectric effect equation becomes ν_L = ½ Q_{m/h} v_g²,
and the "pre-loaded state" is energy already stored in the
Q_{m/h} ratio of the detector atoms.

## 4. Comparison with our T⁶ model

The T⁶ model and Reiter's threshold model start from a
strikingly similar philosophical premise and then diverge sharply
in their mathematical implementation.

### Points of contact

**4.1 — Particles are wave phenomena, not point objects.**

Our T⁶ model's founding axiom: "particles are confined light."
An electron is a photon trapped on a T², a proton on another T²,
neutrinos on a third.  Reiter's founding axiom: particles are
charge-waves, not point objects.  Light is classical.  Both
frameworks reject the standard model's treatment of particles as
elementary point-like entities and instead derive particle
properties from wave behavior.

**4.2 — E = hν as a property of geometry/matter, not free
light.**

In the T⁶ model, quantization E = ℏc/L arises from the compact
geometry — it is a property of the torus (the "matter"), not of
photons propagating freely in 3+1 dimensions.  A free-space
photon in our model is an ordinary classical EM wave; the
quantization that produces particle masses occurs at the compact
boundary.

Reiter makes the same point, following Planck's 1911 position:
E = hν is a property of matter (of the absorber/emitter), not of
light.  Light is classical and continuous.

This is the deepest resonance between the two frameworks.  Both
locate quantization in the structure of matter (compact geometry
in T⁶; absorber thresholds in Reiter), not in the nature of free
electromagnetic radiation.

**4.3 — The ratio principle echoes geometric ratios.**

In the T⁶ model, observable quantities emerge as ratios of
geometric parameters.  The fine-structure constant α is:

    α = r² sin²(2πs) / (4π(2−s)² √(r²(1+2s)²+4))

a ratio of geometric factors (aspect ratio r, shear s).  The
neutrino mass-squared ratio depends only on the shear s₃₄:

    Δm²₃₁/Δm²₂₁ = (3 − 2s) / (4s)

with the aspect ratio r canceling completely.

The model cannot independently predict e and m_e — it predicts
their relationship through geometry, including the charge-to-mass
coupling encoded in α.  This echoes Reiter's observation that
experiments measure ratios (e/m, h/m, e/h), not individual
constants.

**4.4 — Threshold-like behavior in mode selection.**

The T⁶ mode spectrum has a structural property that resembles
thresholding.  Not every vibration pattern on the T⁶ manifests as
a stable particle.  Only modes satisfying specific topological
constraints — correct winding numbers, coprimality, spin
selection — are stable.  Modes that fail these constraints either
do not couple to known forces or decay rapidly.

In the neutrino sector: only modes with tube winding |n₃| = 1
carry weak charge and can be produced in beta decay (R26 F35).
The 14 other light modes on the neutrino T² are "dark" — they
exist in the spectrum but cannot thermalize.  This acts as a
selection threshold: a mode must satisfy discrete topological
criteria to participate in physics.


### Points of divergence

**4.5 — Quantization is topological in T⁶; Reiter rejects
intrinsic quantization.**

This is the core disagreement.  In the T⁶ model, quantization is
exact and topological.  Mode quantum numbers (n₁, ..., n₆) are
integers because the wave must satisfy periodic boundary
conditions on the torus.  Mass eigenvalues are discrete because
the spectrum of the Laplacian on T⁶ is discrete.  Charge is
exactly ±e because the (1,2) winding produces exactly one unit of
net E-flux projection through the shear mechanism.

This quantization is not an assumption — it is a mathematical
consequence of the topology.  A wave on a closed manifold *must*
have integer winding numbers.  There is no "sub-quantum" regime.

Reiter's model explicitly opposes this kind of intrinsic
quantization in free space.  For Reiter, sub-h, sub-e, sub-m
amounts exist but are invisible to threshold detectors.

**4.6 — "No photons" versus "everything is photons."**

Reiter's position: "There are no photons.  Light is classical."

Our position: "Everything IS photons" — particles are photons
trapped on compact geometry.

This disagreement is partly semantic.  When Reiter says "no
photons," he means no indivisible energy packets traveling through
space.  When we say "everything is photons," we mean that the
electromagnetic field is the only fundamental ingredient, and
particle properties emerge from its confinement.  Our free-space
photon is also effectively a classical EM wave — the
quantization occurs at the compact geometry level, not during
propagation.

So the disagreement about the nature of free-space light is
actually smaller than the slogans suggest.

**4.7 — No geometric mechanism for spin or charge.**

Reiter's framework lacks the mathematical machinery to explain
spin or charge from first principles.  In the T⁶ model:

- Spin-½ arises from the (1,2) winding topology (topological,
  exact, geometry-independent).
- Charge arises from the shear-induced breaking of φ-symmetry
  on the embedded T² (R19).
- The g-factor g ≈ 2 follows from the spin-1 photon producing
  a spin-½ fermion.

The threshold model explains *why quantization appears* (threshold
detectors on classical waves) but does not explain *what
determines the specific quantum numbers* that nature selects.

**4.8 — The T⁶ model's quantization is robust against
Reiter's challenge.**

Even if Reiter is correct that free-space absorption is
continuous rather than quantized, the T⁶ model's predictions
about mass spectra, charges, and spins are unchanged.  These
properties depend on the compact geometry (mode spectrum of the
Laplacian on T⁶), not on the nature of free-space propagation.
The integer winding numbers, the discrete mass eigenvalues, the
charge mechanism — all are topological and survive any
reinterpretation of how energy is absorbed by macroscopic
detectors.

## 5. Could T⁶ explain the threshold effect?

If Reiter's experimental claims are eventually confirmed by
independent replication, our T⁶ model may contain a natural
mechanism to explain them — without abandoning quantization.
The mechanism involves energy cascade between oscillation modes,
a well-established phenomenon in wave physics.


### 5.1 — Energy cascades to the fundamental

In any real oscillating system — a vibrating string, a microwave
cavity, a laser — energy has a natural tendency to cascade toward
the lowest-frequency mode (the fundamental).  This is one of the
most robust phenomena in wave physics:

**Dissipative cascade.** Higher harmonics dissipate faster than
the fundamental (damping rate increases with frequency).  Over
time, a complex mix of overtones dies away and the fundamental
emerges as the surviving oscillation.

**Nonlinear mode coupling.** In systems with any nonlinearity,
modes exchange energy.  The Fermi-Pasta-Ulam-Tsingou (FPUT)
problem (1955) demonstrated this dramatically: energy placed in
the fundamental of a nonlinear chain spread to higher modes and
then *returned almost entirely to the fundamental*.  Energy
transfer between modes is structured, not random, and the
fundamental is privileged.

**Bose-Einstein condensation.** At low temperatures, bosonic
atoms condense into the ground state — the fundamental mode of
the trapping potential.  This is a threshold phenomenon: above a
critical temperature, atoms occupy many modes; below it, a
macroscopic fraction falls into one mode.

**Parametric down-conversion.** In nonlinear optics, a photon at
frequency 2ω spontaneously splits into two photons at frequency
ω — energy cascading from the second harmonic to the fundamental.


### 5.2 — The laser analogy

The clearest parallel to Reiter's threshold concept is the laser.
Consider a laser cavity's behavior as pump power increases:

**Below threshold:** Energy pumped into the gain medium
distributes across many cavity modes incoherently.  No single
mode dominates.  The output is spontaneous emission —
unfocused, broadband, dim.  Energy is stored in excited atomic
states (the "pre-loaded" states, in Reiter's language).

**At threshold:** Once the accumulated energy (population
inversion) exceeds the loss for one mode — typically the
fundamental or lowest-loss mode — that mode begins to dominate.
A positive feedback loop activates: stimulated emission into the
dominant mode depletes the gain faster than competing modes can
grow.

**Above threshold:** The dominant mode captures essentially ALL
the available energy.  Competing modes are suppressed.  The
output is coherent, directional, monochromatic.  Energy has
"collapsed" from a diffuse distribution across many modes into
a single fundamental.

The threshold is sharp.  Below it: incoherent light from many
modes.  Above it: coherent light from one mode.  This is a
genuine phase transition, experimentally demonstrated in
thousands of laser systems since 1960.


### 5.3 — How this maps onto T⁶

In the T⁶ model, each T² subplane supports a spectrum of
standing-wave modes.  The fundamental (1,2) mode on the
electron's T² IS the electron.  Higher modes are higher-energy
excitations — the muon (if it maps to an excited electron-T²
mode), other unstable particles, or modes that don't couple to
known forces.

Consider what happens when energy is deposited on a T²:

**Step 1 — Energy at the fundamental.**  If the incoming energy
matches the (1,2) mode energy (m_e c² for the electron T²), it
excites the fundamental directly.  A stable electron exists.

**Step 2 — Energy between fundamental and first harmonic.**  If
additional energy arrives, but not enough to excite the next
stable mode, it must distribute across higher modes temporarily.
These modes are the T²'s overtones — higher winding numbers,
higher masses.  On an isolated T², these higher modes are
quasi-stable at best.  The excess energy sits in a superposition
of non-fundamental modes, unable to form a stable particle.

This IS a pre-loaded state: energy stored in the system,
distributed across non-fundamental modes, not yet organized
into a recognizable particle.

**Step 3 — Cascade to the fundamental.**  The higher modes are
unstable.  Through the same mechanisms that operate in any
oscillating system — dissipation (coupling to the non-compact
dimensions), nonlinear mode coupling (cross-shear interactions
between T² planes), or parametric processes — energy cascades
from the higher modes back to the fundamental.  When enough
energy has accumulated in the fundamental to satisfy the
topological boundary conditions (integer winding, correct
periodicity), a stable particle "clicks" into existence.

The click is sharp — the transition from "energy distributed
across many modes" to "energy organized into a fundamental
standing wave" is a threshold transition, analogous to laser
threshold or Bose-Einstein condensation.

This provides a geometric explanation for Reiter's observations
(if confirmed):

- **"Pre-loaded" detectors** are detector atoms whose internal
  compact geometry already has sub-threshold energy distributed
  across non-fundamental modes from previous interactions.

- **"Continuous absorption"** corresponds to incoming EM energy
  gradually populating the mode spectrum of the compact space,
  without yet triggering a cascade to the fundamental.

- **"Explosive emission"** (the detector click) corresponds to
  the mode cascade: once enough energy occupies the system, it
  reorganizes into the fundamental, and the resulting stable mode
  interacts with the macroscopic detector as a particle event.

- **The beam-split coincidence** would occur because a classical
  EM wave splits and each half delivers energy to a detector
  whose compact modes are already pre-loaded.  Neither half alone
  would create a new particle from nothing — each half merely
  supplies the final increment that triggers a cascade in an
  already-loaded system.


### 5.4 — Threshold from topology

There is a deeper reason why the cascade produces sharp
thresholds rather than gradual transitions.  On a compact
manifold, the mode spectrum is discrete.  The (1,2) fundamental
has a specific energy E₀ determined by the torus geometry.
There is no mode at 0.99 × E₀ or 1.01 × E₀ — the spectrum has
gaps.

This means the fundamental mode is either fully occupied (energy
≥ E₀) or not occupied at all.  There is no gradual turning-on.
The transition between "energy in non-fundamental modes" and
"energy in the fundamental" is necessarily sharp, because the
fundamental mode's energy is topologically fixed.

Compare this to a laser cavity, where the mode spacing is
determined by the cavity length (L):

    Δν = c / (2L)

The laser threshold is sharp because the mode spacing creates a
discrete set of allowed frequencies.  In the T⁶ model, the "mode
spacing" is set by the compact geometry, and the threshold
sharpness is likewise set by the topology.


### 5.5 — Decay as the reverse process

The T⁶ model's treatment of unstable particles is the reverse
of the loading process.  The muon (if it is an excited electron-
T² mode) is energy that has been loaded into a higher mode and
has not yet cascaded to the fundamental.  It is metastable —
held up temporarily by some coupling barrier — but eventually
the energy reorganizes:

    muon → electron + neutrinos

The muon's 2.2 μs lifetime measures the timescale for energy to
cascade from the excited mode to the fundamental.  The neutron's
15-minute lifetime reflects the weakness of cross-sheet coupling
(only 2.2° of tilt between the electron and proton T² planes) —
a very slow cascade.

In the language of mode coupling:

| Particle | Description | Cascade time |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Electron | Fundamental of electron T² | Stable (no lower mode) |
| Muon | Excited electron-T² mode (?) | ~2.2 μs |
| Neutron | Cross-sheet mode (1,2,0,0,1,2) | ~15 min |
| Proton | Fundamental of proton T² | Stable (no lower mode) |
| Neutrinos | Fundamental modes of neutrino T² | Stable |

Stable particles are the fundamentals.  Unstable particles are
excited modes.  Decay is energy cascading to the fundamental.
The lifetime measures the coupling strength between the excited
mode and the fundamental.


### 5.6 — What this does and does not explain

**Does explain (in principle):**
- Why detectors exhibit threshold behavior: the compact mode
  spectrum is discrete, so mode occupation is all-or-nothing.
- Why particle creation has sharp energy thresholds: you need at
  least E₀ to excite the fundamental.
- Why unstable particles decay to stable ones: energy cascades
  to the fundamental modes.
- Why the cascade is sharp: topological quantization of the mode
  spectrum forbids "partial" occupation.

**Does not explain:**
- Reiter's specific experimental results (beam-split
  coincidences exceeding chance).  These would require a detailed
  model of how sub-threshold energy distributes across non-
  fundamental compact modes in a macroscopic detector crystal, and
  how incoming EM radiation loads those modes.  This is far beyond
  the current state of the T⁶ framework.
- Whether pre-loaded states actually exist in detector materials.
  This is an experimental question, not a theoretical one.
- The ratio principle specifically.  In the T⁶ model, the ratios
  e/m, h/m, e/h emerge from geometry, but the individual
  constants are also well-defined (charge is exactly e, mass is
  exactly the mode energy, h is exactly the periodicity scale of
  the compact space).  Sub-quantum ratios do not exist in the T⁶
  framework.

The honest summary: the T⁶ model's mode-cascade mechanism
provides a natural language for threshold-like behavior in
particle physics (creation thresholds, decay cascades, the
stability of fundamental modes).  Whether it can explain the
specific anomalies Reiter reports in detector physics is an open
question that would require substantial theoretical development.

The two frameworks are complementary more than contradictory.
The threshold model asks *how detectors work* (a question the T⁶
model has not addressed).  The T⁶ model asks *what particles are*
(a question the threshold model leaves open).  If both are
partially correct, the synthesis would be: particles are
topologically quantized modes on compact geometry (T⁶), and
their creation/destruction in detectors involves a mode-cascade
process that looks like threshold loading from the outside.
