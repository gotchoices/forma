# The Nine-Dimensional Atom

**Status:** draft

**Paper 3 of 3.**  Paper 1: [Matter from Light](matter-from-light.md).
Paper 2: [Sub-Quantum Memory](sub-quantum-memory.md).

This paper extends the Ma model from single particles to
multi-body systems: nuclei, atoms, and (potentially) the
periodic table.  Particles are defined in Ma (the six-dimensional
material space), but the atom requires all nine: Ma × S.  The
central result is a two-tier structure — nuclei are single
Ma modes, while atoms are nuclei + electrons bound in S —
and a surprise: the discovery that nuclei are modes
retroactively dissolves a tension in the boson-mediation
framework that once threatened the model.

---


### Abstract

Paper 1 described how single particles emerge from the
material geometry Ma: mass, charge, and spin are
determined by mode quantum numbers on six compact
dimensions.  This paper asks whether the same geometry
predicts interactions between particles.

The answer comes in two acts.  First, the wave equation on
the full Ma × S geometry produces gauge fields as emergent
mathematical objects (R36 F8).  The zero-mode gauge field
gives the Coulomb potential V(r) = −αℏc/r with α = 1/137
from the shear mechanism — no new parameters.  Hydrogen's
ground-state energy (−13.6 eV) and Bohr radius (0.53 Å)
follow from the geometry with < 0.1% error.

But the massive gauge bosons predicted by the same
decomposition produce Yukawa corrections 10⁴× larger than
the measured Lamb shift — a crisis.  The resolution comes
from the neutron lesson of Paper 1: nuclei, like the
neutron, are not composites bound by a force.  They are
single Ma modes.  Every stable nucleus from the deuteron
to iron-56 matches a Ma mode following a universal scaling
law (n₅ = A, n₆ = 2A) at < 1% error.  The deuteron is
predicted at 0.02% with 86% of its binding energy
captured.  Nuclear spins follow from mode parity for 9 of
11 tested nuclei.

The boson-mediation picture was wrong, not the geometry.
Nuclei need no mediator because there is nothing to
mediate between — each nucleus is one standing wave.
A two-tier structure emerges: Ma determines particle
identity and nuclear mass (MeV scale); S determines atomic
binding and nuclear stability (eV scale).  Atoms are not
Ma modes — they live below the Ma resolution floor.

## 1. Introduction: from particles to systems

Paper 1 described how single particles emerge from the Ma
material geometry: mass, charge, and spin are determined by
mode quantum numbers on six material dimensions.  But nature
is not isolated particles.  Atoms exist.  Nuclei are stable.
Electrons occupy discrete energy levels.

This paper asks: does the geometry that predicts particle
masses also predict their interactions?  The answer is yes —
and the way it does so contains a surprise.


## 2. Nine dimensions: Ma × S

### 2.1 Adding spatial dimensions
The Ma model describes particles but not their positions.
To describe two particles at different locations, we need
S (the three spatial dimensions of ordinary space).  The
full static framework is Ma × S: nine dimensions.
Time (R¹) is deferred — we treat static configurations.

### 2.2 The dimensional division of labor
A clean separation emerges:

| Space | Determines | Scale |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Ma (material) | Particle identity: mass, charge, spin | MeV |
| S (spatial) | Position, separation, binding | eV |

Particles are *what they are* because of Ma.  Particles
are *where they are* because of S.


## 3. Coulomb from geometry

### 3.1 How forces emerge from material dimensions

In MaSt, particles are standing electromagnetic waves on
material sheets — confined photons, not point particles
moving through compact dimensions.  This is different from
the original Kaluza-Klein picture, where a pre-existing
particle carries momentum along a compact direction and
that momentum appears as electric charge.

But the mathematical structure is shared.  R36 (geometric
tilt) showed that when you solve the wave equation on the
full Ma × S geometry, off-diagonal metric components
g_{μi} — mixing spatial (S) and material (Ma) dimensions —
emerge as gauge potentials (R36 F8–F9).  They are not
imposed; they fall out of the wave equation.  With 6
material dimensions, 6 independent U(1) gauge fields
appear.

The physical origin of charge is different from the
original KK picture: charge arises from shear-induced
E-field asymmetry on the material sheet (R19), not from
compact-direction momentum.  MaSt has no pre-existing
particles carrying momentum — it has confined photons
whose standing-wave pattern breaks symmetry.  But the
long-range Coulomb force between two charged modes is
correctly described by the emergent gauge field.  The
mathematical tool works even though the physical mechanism
underneath is geometry, not particle motion.

The gauge fields are massless in the zero-mode sector —
they propagate as 1/r in S — giving the Coulomb potential.
Each material dimension also contributes a tower of massive
gauge bosons with mass m ≈ 2πℏc/L, where L is the
circumference.  These produce short-range Yukawa
corrections that become important in section 6.

### 3.2 Electromagnetism identified
The electromagnetic field is the specific combination
A^EM = −A¹ + A⁵ (mixing the electron-tube and proton-tube
gauge fields).  This gives charge Q = −n₁ + n₅, matching
the charge formula used throughout Paper 1.

### 3.3 The coupling constant
α = 1/137.036 is determined by the shear mechanism (R19)
on both the electron and proton material sheets.  No new
parameters are introduced.  The same α that gives the
electron its charge governs the interaction between
particles.

### 3.4 The Coulomb potential
A charged mode at position x₀ in S produces a 1/r
potential through the zero-mode of the emergent gauge field.
The result: V(r) = −α ℏc / r = −1.440/r MeV·fm.


## 4. Hydrogen from first principles

### 4.1 The complete chain

The derivation has no gaps.  Ma geometry fixes the mode
quantum numbers; the shear mechanism (R19) gives α = 1/137
from the lattice tilt; the zero-mode gauge field gives
V(r) = −αℏc/r; solving the Schrödinger equation with the
reduced mass μ = m_e m_p / (m_e + m_p) gives the hydrogen
spectrum.

| Quantity | Ma prediction | Measured | Error |
|----------|--------------|---------|-------|
| E₁ | −13.5983 eV | −13.5984 eV | +0.001% |
| a₀ | 0.52947 Å | 0.52918 Å | +0.054% |

The tiny residuals come from rounding in the input
physical constants, not from the model.

### 4.2 What this means

Hydrogen's ground-state energy is derived from the shape
of six material dimensions.  No force is assumed — the
electromagnetic interaction is a consequence of the
geometry that defines the particles.  No coupling constant
is input — α comes from the same shear that gives the
electron its charge.  The chain from confined photon to
atomic spectrum is unbroken:

    confined photon → standing wave on Ma_e → charge (shear)
    → Coulomb potential (gauge field on Ma × S) → E₁ = −13.6 eV


## 5. Six gauge fields: beyond electromagnetism

### 5.1 The gauge census

Each gauge field has a mass set by the circumference of its
material dimension.  The proton and electron dimensions are
compact enough to produce massive gauge bosons; the
neutrino dimensions are macroscopic (~mm) and decouple
from atomic physics because the electron and proton modes
have n₃ = n₄ = 0 (no neutrino-sheet winding).

| Gauge field | Material dim | Mass | Range | Couples to |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A¹ (e-tube) | L₁ = r_e × L₂ | r_e-dependent | r_e-dependent | n₁ ≠ 0 |
| A² (e-ring) | L₂ = 4880 fm | 0.254 MeV | 775 fm | n₂ ≠ 0 |
| A³ (ν-tube) | L₃ ~ 42 μm | ~5 × 10⁻⁹ MeV | macroscopic | n₃ ≠ 0 |
| A⁴ (ν-ring) | L₄ ~ 200 μm | ~10⁻⁹ MeV | macroscopic | n₄ ≠ 0 |
| A⁵ (p-tube) | L₅ = 23.7 fm | 52 MeV | 3.8 fm | n₅ ≠ 0 |
| A⁶ (p-ring) | L₆ = 2.66 fm | 467 MeV | 0.42 fm | n₆ ≠ 0 |

### 5.3 Nuclear force candidates

A⁵ and A⁶ sit in the right mass and range window for
nuclear binding — comparable to the pion (140 MeV, ~1.4 fm)
that mediates the nuclear force in the Standard Model.
A⁵ couples to all modes with proton-tube winding (n₅ ≠ 0);
A⁶ couples to ring winding (n₆ ≠ 0).

### 5.4 The neutron-specific force

The neutrino-tube gauge field A³ couples to n₃ ≠ 0.  The
neutron has n₃ = 1; the proton has n₃ = 0.  This creates a
force that the neutron feels but the proton does not —
a potential explanation for neutron-proton asymmetry in
nuclear physics.


## 6. The Yukawa crisis

### 6.1 The boson approach to nuclear binding

The conventional strategy is: derive forces from geometry,
then compute bound states.  Section 3 extracted the Coulomb
force successfully.  The natural next step is to push
further — the massive gauge bosons (section 5) should
mediate short-range forces between nucleons.

### 6.2 It fails spectacularly

The dominant correction comes from the electron-tube gauge
boson, whose circumference L₁ = r_e × L₂ grows with the
free parameter r_e.  The proton-sheet gauge bosons are
exponentially suppressed (their dimensions are ~fm, far
smaller than the Bohr radius ~53,000 fm).  But the
electron tube can be large.

The 1S wave function peaks at the origin, where the
Yukawa correction approaches full Coulomb strength.  The
correction is not just the Yukawa factor at the Bohr
radius (which would be exponentially small); the wave
function integral samples all distances, and the short-
range overlap is substantial.

At r_e = 6.6 (a moderate value), the results are
catastrophic (R29 F11–F12):

| Quantity | Ma prediction | Measured | Ratio |
|----------|--------------|---------|-------|
| 1S energy shift | −0.74 eV | < 10⁻¹² eV | ~10¹² × |
| 2S−2P splitting | −0.089 eV | +4.37 × 10⁻⁶ eV | ~10⁴ × |

Even at r_e = 1.5, the Lamb-like splitting is still
1400× the measured Lamb shift.  For the correction to
fall below measurement precision (~10 kHz ≈ 2.4 × 10⁻⁹
eV), the electron tube would need L₁ < ~2 fm, requiring
r_e < 0.0004 — far below the shear mechanism threshold
(r_e > 0.26, R19 F40).

No value of r_e simultaneously satisfies both constraints.
Five candidate resolutions exist (R29 F13) — the most
promising being that the massive gauge modes do not couple
with the same strength as the zero mode, because charge
comes from the shear integral (R19), not from uniform
compact-direction momentum.

### 6.3 The failure is in the intermediate theory

The boson-mediation picture assumed that the gauge fields
emergent from the geometry mediate forces between
particles, which bind nuclei.  The leading-order Coulomb
term works perfectly.  But the massive boson tower produces
unphysical corrections.  This looks like it could be fatal
to the entire framework — unless the intermediate theory
is wrong.


## 7. The nuclear surprise

### 7.1 The neutron lesson, revisited

The Yukawa crisis of section 6 looks terminal.  But the
resolution was already foreshadowed in Paper 1.

Recall: the neutron was not derived from a proton-electron
bound state.  We initially hypothesized it as two particles
held together by some force — that failed.  Instead, the
neutron turned out to be its own Ma mode: a single standing
wave (1,2,0,0,1,2) spanning two material sheets.  No
binding force was needed because there was nothing being
bound.

The key insight: rather than asking "what force binds a
proton and neutron?", ask "is there a Ma mode with the
mass, charge, and spin of a deuteron?"  This is directly
testable with the existing Ma solver.

### 7.2 Yes, there is

The deuteron mode (1,n₂,1,0,2,4) has mass 1875.9 MeV
(observed: 1875.6 MeV, error 0.02%).  No nuclear force
assumed.  No free parameters beyond those already pinned
by the neutron and muon.  The deuteron is a single standing
wave in the material space — not two particles bound
together.

Its charge is automatic: Q = −n₁ + n₅ = −1 + 2 = +1 ✓.
Its binding energy (2.2 MeV) is 86% captured: the Ma mode
sits 1.9 MeV below the free proton + neutron sum.

If the deuteron is a mode, what about heavier nuclei?


## 8. The nuclear scaling law

### 8.1 A universal pattern

A systematic search reveals that every stable nucleus from
the deuteron to iron-56 is matched by a Ma mode following
a simple rule:

    n₅ = A  (mass number)
    n₆ = 2A
    n₁ = N  (neutron number = A − Z)

A nucleus is literally the proton mode (0,0,0,0,1,2) wound
A times around the material torus.  It is not A particles
bound together — it is one oscillation at the A-th
harmonic.  Charge is automatic: Q = −n₁ + n₅ = −N + A = Z.

### 8.3 Binding energy

The Ma mode energy falls between the free-nucleon sum and
the observed mass for every nucleus — the correct direction
for binding.  The deuteron's binding energy is 86% captured.
But the fraction captured decreases with A:

| Nucleus | A | Gap/A (MeV) | BE/A observed (MeV) | Captured |
|---------|---|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| d | 2 | 0.15 | 1.11 | 86% |
| ⁴He | 4 | 6.27 | 7.07 | 11% |
| ¹²C | 12 | 6.98 | 7.68 | 9% |
| ⁵⁶Fe | 56 | 8.09 | 8.79 | 8% |

The residual gap per nucleon asymptotes near ~8 MeV/A.
This uncaptured binding energy likely represents S spatial
effects — Coulomb repulsion, surface-to-volume terms, and
other interactions — that the Ma-only calculation misses.
The Ma mode captures the gross nuclear mass.  The fine
binding energy requires the full Ma × S geometry.


## 9. Nuclear spins from mode parity

### 9.1 The rule

Nuclear spin follows the mode_spin convention from
Paper 1: spin = count of odd tube quantum numbers among
n₁, n₃, n₅.  Each odd winding contributes ½ unit of
angular momentum.

### 9.3 Failures

Two nuclei fail with the exact A×proton scaling pattern:

- **³He** (spin ½): n₁ = 1, n₅ = 3 — both odd gives
  spin 1, not ½.  An unconstrained search finds an
  alternative mode (0,−12,1,0,2,6) that does match.
- **⁷Li** (spin 3/2): n₁ = 4 (even), n₅ = 7 (odd) —
  base spin = ½, cannot reach 3/2 with available n₃.

These are the same charge-spin structural constraint found
for the Ω⁻ baryon in R27 (F36): certain (A, Z, spin)
combinations require modes that depart from the exact
scaling pattern.  The departures are well-defined — the
modes exist but have slightly different quantum numbers.


## 10. Free neutron ≠ nuclear neutron

### 10.1 Two different modes

The free neutron (from R27) has quantum numbers
(0,−2,1,0,0,2) — critically, n₅ = 0.  It has no
proton-tube winding.

Inside a nucleus, each nucleon contributes n₅ = 1 to the
collective mode (and n₆ = 2 to the ring winding).  The
"neutron" inside a deuteron has n₅ = 1.  This is a
different Ma mode from the free neutron.  The nuclear
neutron is part of the proton-sheet harmonic; the free
neutron is not.

### 10.2 Why neutrons don't decay in nuclei

The free neutron (n₅ = 0) decays with a mean life of
~880 s.  But neutrons inside stable nuclei do not decay.
In MaSt, the explanation is geometric: there is no neutron
inside the nucleus to decay.  The nuclear mode
(n₅ = A, n₆ = 2A) is a single standing wave that cannot
shed one nucleon without disrupting the entire oscillation.

A nucleus is not A separate particles.  It is one mode at
the A-th harmonic.  There is no neutron in a nucleus.
There is only the nuclear mode.


## 11. The Yukawa crisis dissolved

### 11.1 The boson picture was wrong, not the geometry
The Yukawa tension (section 6) arose from an intermediate
theory: we assumed that the emergent gauge bosons mediate
forces between particles, then computed corrections from
those bosons.  The corrections were unphysically large.

But sections 7–10 show that the intermediate theory was
unnecessary.  Nuclei are not particles bound by exchanged
bosons — they are single Ma modes.  The deuteron is not a
proton and neutron exchanging a pion (or a massive gauge
boson).  It is one standing wave.  No mediator is needed
because there is nothing to mediate between.

### 11.2 What this means for atomic physics
The zero-mode gauge field — the Coulomb potential —
correctly reproduces hydrogen (section 4).  The massive
gauge tower produces large corrections if you assume it
couples with the same strength.  But the nuclear result
shows that the boson-mediation picture is not how Ma
physics works at short range.  The geometry speaks
directly through modes, not through exchanged quanta.

The Yukawa tension is not a problem with the geometry.
It is a problem with applying an intermediate theory
(boson exchange) where the geometry has already provided
the answer (direct modes).

### 11.3 The residual question
What remains open is the detailed coupling structure of
the massive gauge tower.  If the shear-based charge
mechanism (R19) does not couple to the massive modes in
the same way as the zero mode, the Yukawa corrections
would be naturally suppressed — consistent with both the
hydrogen spectrum and the nuclear mode picture.  This is
an active theoretical question, but it is no longer a
crisis.


## 12. Two-tier structure

### 12.1 Ma determines mass, S determines stability

For a given mass number A, the Ma mode energy is
essentially independent of the charge Z.  For A = 12:

    E(Z = 0)  = 11260.145 MeV  (pure neutrons)
    E(Z = 6)  = 11260.140 MeV  (carbon-12)
    E(Z = 12) = 11259.264 MeV  (pure protons)
    Total spread: < 1 MeV on 11260 MeV (< 0.01%)

Ma determines *which mass numbers exist* (from the proton-
sheet energy ladder).  S determines *which isotope is
stable* for each A (through Coulomb repulsion between
protons, the neutron-proton mass difference, and other
spatial effects).  This is a clean separation of
responsibilities:

    Ma  →  nuclear mass (from A)
    S   →  nuclear stability (which Z)

### 12.2 Atoms are NOT Ma modes

The smallest Ma energy step near the proton mass is
~22 keV (from changing n₂ by ±1).  Atomic binding is
~13.6 eV — 1600× smaller.  Atomic binding is invisible
to the Ma mode solver; it lives below the Ma resolution
floor.

    Ma energy resolution:  ~22 keV
    Atomic binding:        ~13.6 eV
    Ratio:                 ~1600

Nuclei are Ma modes.  Atoms are nuclei (Ma modes) plus
electrons (Ma modes) interacting through the Coulomb
potential (derived from Ma × S in §3) in ordinary space.

### 12.3 The architecture

| System | Lives in | Binding scale | Nature |
|--------|----------|--------------|--------|
| Particle | Ma | rest mass (MeV) | single mode |
| Nucleus | Ma | nuclear (MeV) | single mode (A-th harmonic) |
| Atom | Ma × S | Coulomb (eV) | modes interacting in space |
| Molecule | Ma × S | chemical (eV) | multi-atom spatial structure |


## 13. Open: the periodic table

### 13.1 What's needed
Extending from hydrogen to helium requires treating two
electrons in the Coulomb field of a Z = 2 nucleus.  The
Ma model provides the nuclear charge (Z) and the Coulomb
coupling (α) — the rest is the standard multi-electron
Schrödinger equation.  No new material-dimension physics
enters.

### 13.2 What would be new
The Ma model predicts 6 gauge fields, not just
electromagnetism.  If the additional forces contribute at
atomic scales (unlikely, given their short range, but not
excluded without calculation), they could produce
detectable corrections to atomic energy levels — spectral
features not predicted by standard QED.

### 13.3 Whether to go there
Reproducing the periodic table from the Ma Coulomb
potential would be a strong validation but would not test
new physics — it would use the same α and V(r) = −Zα/r
that standard QED uses.  The real test is whether the
Ma-specific predictions (additional gauge fields, Yukawa
corrections) produce measurable effects.


## 14. Ma versus the Standard Model

### 14.1 No quarks

The Standard Model explains hadrons as composites of quarks:
mesons are quark-antiquark pairs (qq̄), baryons are quark
triplets (qqq), held together by gluons carrying a three-
valued "color" charge.  The quark model was introduced to
explain the proliferation of hadrons in the 1960s, and deep
inelastic scattering experiments in the 1970s provided
evidence of point-like constituents inside protons.

The Ma model has no quarks.  Every hadron is a single
standing wave on the material geometry:

| SM picture | Ma picture |
|------------|-----------|
| Proton = uud | Proton = mode (0,0,0,0,1,2) |
| Neutron = udd | Neutron = mode (0,−2,1,0,0,2) |
| Kaon = us̄ | Kaon = mode (1,−5,1,0,2,2) |
| Deuteron = p + n bound by pion exchange | Deuteron = mode (1,n₂,1,0,2,4) |

No fractional charges exist — all charges are integers
(Q = −n₁ + n₅).  No gluons exist — there is no color
charge and no strong force in the usual sense.  No quark
confinement puzzle arises because there are no quarks to
confine.

The "strong force" is replaced by two different mechanisms:

1. **For nucleons** (proton, neutron): these are simply
   modes on the material geometry.  No binding force is
   needed because there is nothing being bound — the
   particle is a single standing wave, not a composite.

2. **For nuclei**: the nuclear scaling law (n₅ = A,
   n₆ = 2A) makes each nucleus a single Ma mode at the
   A-th harmonic of the proton.  Again, no inter-nucleon
   force is needed.  The deuteron is not a proton and
   neutron exchanging pions — it is one oscillation.

What quarks explain that Ma does not yet address:

- **Deep inelastic scattering**: point-like substructure
  inside protons, seen in high-energy electron-proton
  collisions.  This is above the Ma predictive horizon
  (~2 GeV) and may require understanding the internal
  structure of the standing wave — how the energy is
  distributed within the material dimensions.

- **Jets**: collimated sprays of hadrons in high-energy
  collisions, explained in QCD by quark/gluon production
  and fragmentation.  Again above the predictive horizon.

- **Asymptotic freedom**: the weakening of the strong
  coupling at short distances.  Ma has no analog of a
  running coupling constant.

The most dramatic difference between the two frameworks
is the number of free parameters.

| Category | Count | Status |
|----------|------:|--------|
| Ring scales (L_θe, L_θp, L_θν) | 3 | Set by m_e, m_p, Δm²₂₁ (inputs) |
| Within-plane shears (s_e, s_ν, s_p) | 3 | Constrained: s_e by α (given r_e), s_p by α (given r_p), s_ν by Δm² ratio |
| Aspect ratios (r_e, r_ν, r_p) | 3 | r_p pinned by neutron + muon; **r_e free**; **r_ν ≥ 3.2** |
| Aggregate cross-shear σ_ep | 1 | Pinned jointly with r_p (R27 F18) |
| Other cross-shear components | 11 | Set to zero; no measurable effect on any tested observable |
| **Constrained** | **8** | 6 inputs + 2 particle fits |
| **Formally free** | **13** | r_e, r_ν, 11 cross-shear components |
| **Effectively free** | **2** | r_e (unconstrained), r_ν (≥ 3.2) |

The four experimental inputs are m_e, m_p, Δm²₂₁, and α.
The two fit particles are the muon (pins r_p) and neutron
(pins σ_ep).  After these 6 constraints, the model derives
s₃₄ = 0.022 from the neutrino mass-squared ratio.  Every
remaining prediction — kaon, eta, eta-prime, phi, nuclear
masses from deuteron to iron-56 — uses zero additional
parameters.

The MeV-scale predictions are called "parameter-free"
because they are **insensitive** to the 13 remaining
degrees of freedom.  Nuclear masses depend almost entirely
on r_p and σ_ep (R29 F22, F27).  But the model as a whole
is under-determined: r_e has no known observable to pin it,
and r_ν is only lower-bounded by cosmological Σm_ν.

**Net comparison:**

| | Standard Model | Ma |
|---|---|---|
| Inputs to set scales | ~9 masses | 3 masses + α |
| Coupling constants | 3 (α, α_s, sin²θ_W) | 0 (α is an input; sin²θ_W matches 3/13 to −0.19% but origin unexplained, R43) |
| Mixing parameters | 8 (CKM + PMNS) | 0 |
| Other | ~6 (Higgs, θ_QCD, ...) | 0 |
| Free (unconstrained) | ~25 | 2 effective (r_e, r_ν) |

The Ma model covers a narrower domain (particles below
~2 GeV, nuclei, atoms) but does so with dramatically
fewer inputs.  The Standard Model covers a wider domain
(all known particles, all forces, precision QED/QCD) but
requires ~25 empirical parameters.  The honest comparison
is not "0 vs 25" — it is "6 constrained + 2 free" versus
"~25 free."


### 14.3 What Ma explains that the Standard Model does not

Several features that are unexplained inputs or
"fine-tuned" coincidences in the Standard Model have
geometric explanations in Ma:

- **Why m_n > m_p**: In the SM, this depends on the
  up-down quark mass difference (m_d − m_u ≈ 2.5 MeV)
  partially offset by electromagnetic self-energy — both
  free parameters.  In Ma, the neutron is a cross-sheet
  mode that necessarily has more energy than the single-
  sheet proton mode.

- **Why exactly 3 stable particles**: The SM has no
  explanation for why only e, p, ν are stable.  In Ma,
  each is the lightest mode on its own material sheet — there
  is nothing lighter to decay into.

- **Why the neutrino is so light**: In the SM, neutrino
  masses require either a seesaw mechanism or ad hoc
  Yukawa couplings 10⁶× smaller than the electron's.
  In Ma, the neutrino lives on a separate, larger material
  sheet (Ma_ν) — light because big.

- **Neutron decay products**: The SM requires the weak
  force (W boson exchange) to explain n → p + e⁻ + ν̄.
  In Ma, the cross-sheet neutron mode unravels into the
  three constituent sheets — p, e, ν — which ARE the
  decay products.

- **Nuclear binding without a separate force**: The SM
  requires QCD (quarks + gluons) to explain why nuclei
  hold together.  In Ma, nuclei are single modes — no
  binding force is needed.


### 14.4 What the Standard Model explains that Ma does not

- **Electroweak unification**: W/Z/Higgs absolute masses.
  The Weinberg angle now has a geometric match:
  sin²θ_W = 3/13 = N_sheets/(2D+1) to −0.19% (R43), and
  the weak force is cross-sheet coupling (Q96), but the
  W mass (80.4 GeV) is not yet derived from Ma geometry.

- **QCD**: asymptotic freedom, confinement, jet production,
  deep inelastic scattering, lattice QCD calculations.
  Ma has no strong coupling or color charge.

- **Precision QED**: anomalous magnetic moment (g − 2) to
  12 digits, Lamb shift, hyperfine splitting.  Ma
  reproduces g = 2 at leading order only.

- **CP violation**: matter-antimatter asymmetry, kaon
  oscillations, B-meson physics.  Ma has no CP-violating
  mechanism.

- **High-energy collider physics**: top quark, Higgs
  production, diboson processes.  Above the Ma predictive
  horizon.

The two frameworks are not competitors across their full
domains.  They overlap in the 0.1–2 GeV range where both
make predictions.  Below ~0.1 GeV (atomic physics), both
use the same Coulomb potential.  Above ~2 GeV, only the
Standard Model currently applies.  In the overlap region,
Ma makes parameter-free predictions that the SM
requires ~20 inputs to match.


## 15. Open: what remains

### 15.1 The r_e problem
The electron aspect ratio remains unconstrained.  Nuclear
masses are insensitive to it (electron material dimensions
contribute energy at the keV scale, 10⁴–10⁵× smaller than
the proton-sheet contributions).  It must be pinned by
electron-scale observables — possibly through the gauge
coupling structure or through the R19 shear-charge formula.

### 15.2 The 8 MeV/A residual
Heavy nuclei show a systematic residual of ~8 MeV per
nucleon between the Ma mode energy and the observed mass.
The deuteron is captured to 86%, but by iron-56 only ~8%
of the binding energy is accounted for.  This growing gap
likely represents S spatial effects (Coulomb repulsion
between protons, surface/volume energy terms) that the
Ma-only calculation misses.

### 15.3 Ghost nuclear modes
Does the Ma spectrum contain nuclear modes with no observed
counterpart?  An island of Ma stability that contradicts
the experimental valley of stability would be a serious
problem for the model.
