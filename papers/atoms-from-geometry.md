# The Nine-Dimensional Atom

**Status:** rough outline

**Paper 3 of 3.**  Paper 1: [Matter from Light](matter-from-light.md).

This paper extends the T⁶ model from single particles to
multi-body systems: nuclei, atoms, and (potentially) the
periodic table.  Particles are defined in T⁶ (6 compact
dimensions), but the atom requires all nine: T⁶ × R³.  The
central result is a two-tier structure — nuclei are single
T⁶ modes, while atoms are nuclei + electrons bound in R³ —
and a surprise: the discovery that nuclei are modes
retroactively dissolves a tension in the boson-mediation
framework that once threatened the model.

## 1. Introduction: from particles to systems

Paper 1 described how single particles emerge from the T⁶
compact geometry: mass, charge, and spin are determined by
mode quantum numbers on six compact dimensions.  But nature
is not isolated particles.  Atoms exist.  Nuclei are stable.
Electrons occupy discrete energy levels.

This paper asks: does the geometry that predicts particle
masses also predict their interactions?  The answer is yes —
and the way it does so contains a surprise.


## 2. Nine dimensions: T⁶ × R³

### 2.1 Adding spatial dimensions
The T⁶ model describes particles but not their positions.
To describe two particles at different locations, we need
the three spatial dimensions of ordinary space (R³).  The
full static framework is T⁶ × R³: nine dimensions.
Time (R¹) is deferred — we treat static configurations.

### 2.2 The dimensional division of labor
A clean separation emerges:

| Space | Determines | Scale |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| T⁶ (compact) | Particle identity: mass, charge, spin | MeV |
| R³ (spatial) | Position, separation, binding | eV |

Particles are *what they are* because of T⁶.  Particles
are *where they are* because of R³.


## 3. Coulomb from geometry

### 3.1 The Kaluza-Klein gauge mechanism
In Kaluza-Klein theory, gauge fields are not added to the
geometry — they ARE the geometry.  The off-diagonal metric
components g_{μi} (mixing spatial and compact dimensions)
are the gauge potentials.  T⁶ has 6 compact dimensions, so
the KK decomposition yields 6 independent U(1) gauge fields.

### 3.2 Electromagnetism identified
The electromagnetic field is the specific combination
A^EM = −A¹ + A⁵ (mixing the electron tube and proton tube
gauge fields).  This gives charge Q = −n₁ + n₅, matching
the charge formula used throughout Paper 1.

### 3.3 The coupling constant
α = 1/137.036 is determined by the shear mechanism (R19)
on both the electron and proton T² sheets.  No new
parameters are introduced.  The same α that gives the
electron its charge governs the interaction between
particles.

### 3.4 The Coulomb potential
A charged mode at position x₀ in R³ produces a 1/r
potential through the zero-mode KK gauge field.  The
result: V(r) = −α ℏc / r = −1.440/r MeV·fm.


## 4. Hydrogen from first principles

### 4.1 The complete chain
T⁶ geometry → mode quantum numbers → charges (R19) →
Coulomb potential (KK mechanism) → hydrogen binding energy.
The result: E₁ = −13.598 eV, a₀ = 0.5295 Å.  Matches
experiment to better than 0.05%.  Zero free parameters.

### 4.2 What this means
Hydrogen's ground state energy is derived from the shape of
six compact dimensions.  No force is assumed.  No coupling
constant is input.  The electromagnetic interaction is a
consequence of the geometry that defines the particles.


## 5. Six gauge fields: beyond electromagnetism

### 5.1 The gauge sector census
T⁶ predicts 6 U(1) gauge fields, one per compact dimension.
Only one combination (A^EM) is the electromagnetic force.
The other five are additional forces predicted by the
geometry.

### 5.2 Nuclear force candidates
Two gauge bosons have nuclear-scale properties:
- A⁵ (proton tube): mass 52 MeV, range 3.8 fm
- A⁶ (proton ring): mass 467 MeV, range 0.42 fm

These sit in the right mass and range window for nuclear
binding.  A⁵ couples to modes with proton-tube winding
(n₅ ≠ 0); A⁶ couples to ring winding (n₆ ≠ 0).

### 5.3 The neutron-specific force
The neutrino-tube gauge field A³ couples to n₃ ≠ 0.  The
neutron has n₃ = 1; the proton has n₃ = 0.  This creates
a force that the neutron feels but the proton does not —
a potential explanation for neutron-proton asymmetry in
nuclear physics.


## 6. The Yukawa crisis

### 6.1 The boson approach to nuclear binding
The conventional strategy is: derive forces from geometry,
then compute bound states.  Section 3 extracted the Coulomb
force successfully.  The natural next step is to push the
KK framework further — the massive gauge bosons (section 5)
should mediate short-range forces between nucleons.

### 6.2 It fails spectacularly
Each compact dimension contributes a tower of massive gauge
bosons.  The lightest (from the electron tube) has mass
~0.04 MeV and range ~5000 fm.  Its Yukawa correction to
the hydrogen 1S energy is not exponentially suppressed at
the Bohr radius — the wave function integral samples all
distances, and the short-range overlap is substantial.

The result: the predicted Lamb-like splitting exceeds the
measured Lamb shift by a factor of 10⁴.  No value of the
electron aspect ratio r_e simultaneously satisfies the
shear mechanism threshold (r_e > 0.26) and the
spectroscopic constraint (r_e < 0.001).

### 6.3 The failure is in the intermediate theory
The boson-mediation picture assumed that the T⁶ compact
space generates gauge bosons (KK mechanism), which mediate
forces between particles, which bind nuclei.  The leading-
order Coulomb term works perfectly.  But the massive boson
tower produces unphysical corrections.  This looks like it
could be fatal to the entire framework.


## 7. The nuclear surprise

### 7.1 The neutron lesson, revisited
Recall from Paper 1: the neutron was not derived from a
proton-electron force.  It appeared as its own T⁶ mode — a
single standing wave, not a composite.  No binding force
was needed.

Apply the same logic to nuclei.  Instead of asking "what
force binds a proton and neutron?", ask: "is there a T⁶
mode with the mass, charge, and spin of a deuteron?"

### 7.2 Yes, there is
The deuteron mode (1,n₂,1,0,2,4) has mass 1875.9 MeV
(observed: 1875.6 MeV, error 0.02%).  No nuclear force
assumed.  No free parameters.  The deuteron is a single
standing wave in the compact space — not two particles
bound together.


## 8. The nuclear scaling law

### 8.1 A universal pattern
Every stable nucleus from deuteron to iron-56 matches a
T⁶ mode following:

    n₅ = A (mass number)
    n₆ = 2A
    n₁ = N (neutron number)

A nucleus is the proton mode wound A times.  It is not
A particles bound together — it is one oscillation at
the A-th harmonic.

### 8.2 Mass accuracy
All 12 tested nuclei match to < 1%.  The deuteron is the
best (0.02%).  Heavy nuclei show a systematic residual
growing to ~0.87% at iron-56.

### 8.3 Binding energy
The T⁶ mode captures the gross nuclear mass (> 99%) and
a fraction of the binding energy.  The deuteron's binding
energy is 86% captured.  The uncaptured residual (~8 MeV/A
for heavy nuclei) may represent R³ spatial effects the
T⁶-only calculation misses.


## 9. Nuclear spins from mode parity

Nuclear spin is determined by the parity (odd/even) of the
tube winding numbers.  9 of 11 tested nuclei have spins
correctly predicted.  The two failures (³He, ⁷Li) have the
same structural constraint seen in the Ω⁻ baryon — certain
(A, Z, spin) combinations require modes outside the simple
scaling pattern.


## 10. Free neutron ≠ nuclear neutron

### 10.1 Two different modes
The free neutron (from R27) has quantum numbers
(0,−2,1,0,0,2) — no proton-tube winding (n₅ = 0).

Inside a nucleus, each nucleon contributes n₅ = 1 to the
collective mode.  The "neutron" in a nucleus has n₅ = 1.
These are different T⁶ modes.

### 10.2 Why neutrons don't decay in nuclei
The free neutron mode is energetically accessible to decay.
The nuclear mode is a higher harmonic that cannot shed a
single nucleon without disrupting the entire standing wave.
A nucleus is not A separate particles — it is one
oscillation that cannot be decomposed.

There is no neutron in a nucleus.  There is only the
nuclear mode.


## 11. The Yukawa crisis dissolved

### 11.1 The boson picture was wrong, not the geometry
The Yukawa tension (section 6) arose from an intermediate
theory: we assumed that KK gauge bosons mediate forces
between particles, then computed corrections from those
bosons.  The corrections were unphysically large.

But sections 7–10 show that the intermediate theory was
unnecessary.  Nuclei are not particles bound by exchanged
bosons — they are single T⁶ modes.  The deuteron is not a
proton and neutron exchanging a pion (or a KK gauge boson).
It is one standing wave.  No mediator is needed because
there is nothing to mediate between.

### 11.2 What this means for atomic physics
The zero-mode KK gauge field — the Coulomb potential —
correctly reproduces hydrogen (section 4).  The massive
KK tower produces large corrections if you assume it
couples with the same strength.  But the nuclear result
shows that the boson-mediation picture is not how T⁶
physics works at short range.  The geometry speaks
directly through modes, not through exchanged quanta.

The Yukawa tension is not a problem with the geometry.
It is a problem with applying an intermediate theory
(boson exchange) where the geometry has already provided
the answer (direct modes).

### 11.3 The residual question
What remains open is the detailed coupling structure of
the massive KK tower.  If the shear-based charge mechanism
(R19) does not couple to the massive modes in the same way
as the zero mode, the Yukawa corrections would be naturally
suppressed — consistent with both the hydrogen spectrum and
the nuclear mode picture.  This is an active theoretical
question, but it is no longer a crisis.


## 12. Two-tier structure

### 12.1 T⁶ determines mass, R³ determines stability
For a given mass number A, the T⁶ mode energy is
essentially independent of the charge Z.  All Z
compositions for the same A have nearly the same T⁶
energy (spread < 0.01%).

T⁶ determines *which mass numbers exist*.  R³ determines
*which isotope is stable* (through Coulomb repulsion, the
neutron-proton mass difference, and other spatial effects).

### 12.2 Atoms are NOT T⁶ modes
The smallest T⁶ energy step near the proton mass is ~22
keV.  Atomic binding is ~13.6 eV — 1600× smaller.  Atoms
live below the T⁶ resolution floor.

Nuclei are T⁶ modes.  Atoms are nuclei (T⁶ modes) plus
electrons (T⁶ modes) interacting through Coulomb in R³.

### 12.3 The architecture

| System | Lives in | Binding scale | Nature |
|--------|----------|--------------|--------|
| Particle | T⁶ | rest mass (MeV) | single mode |
| Nucleus | T⁶ | nuclear (MeV) | single mode (A-th harmonic) |
| Atom | T⁶ × R³ | Coulomb (eV) | modes interacting in space |
| Molecule | T⁶ × R³ | chemical (eV) | multi-atom spatial structure |


## 13. Open: the periodic table

### 13.1 What's needed
Extending from hydrogen to helium requires treating two
electrons in the Coulomb field of a Z = 2 nucleus.  The
T⁶ model provides the nuclear charge (Z) and the Coulomb
coupling (α) — the rest is the standard multi-electron
Schrödinger equation.  No new compact-dimension physics
enters.

### 13.2 What would be new
The T⁶ model predicts 6 gauge fields, not just
electromagnetism.  If the additional forces contribute at
atomic scales (unlikely, given their short range, but not
excluded without calculation), they could produce
detectable corrections to atomic energy levels — spectral
features not predicted by standard QED.

### 13.3 Whether to go there
Reproducing the periodic table from the T⁶ Coulomb
potential would be a strong validation but would not test
new physics — it would use the same α and V(r) = −Zα/r
that standard QED uses.  The real test is whether the
T⁶-specific predictions (additional gauge fields, Yukawa
corrections) produce measurable effects.


## 14. T⁶ versus the Standard Model

### 14.1 No quarks

The Standard Model explains hadrons as composites of quarks:
mesons are quark-antiquark pairs (qq̄), baryons are quark
triplets (qqq), held together by gluons carrying a three-
valued "color" charge.  The quark model was introduced to
explain the proliferation of hadrons in the 1960s, and deep
inelastic scattering experiments in the 1970s provided
evidence of point-like constituents inside protons.

The T⁶ model has no quarks.  Every hadron is a single
standing wave on the compact geometry:

| SM picture | T⁶ picture |
|------------|-----------|
| Proton = uud | Proton = mode (0,0,0,0,1,2) |
| Neutron = udd | Neutron = mode (0,−2,1,0,0,2) |
| Kaon = us̄ | Kaon = mode (1,−5,1,0,2,2) |
| Deuteron = p + n bound by pion exchange | Deuteron = mode (1,n₂,1,0,2,4) |

No fractional charges exist — all charges are integers
(Q = −n₁ + n₅).  No gluons exist — there is no color
charge and no strong force in the usual sense.  No quark
confinement puzzle arises because there are no quarks to
confine.

The "strong force" is replaced by two different mechanisms:

1. **For nucleons** (proton, neutron): these are simply
   modes on the compact geometry.  No binding force is
   needed because there is nothing being bound — the
   particle is a single standing wave, not a composite.

2. **For nuclei**: the nuclear scaling law (n₅ = A,
   n₆ = 2A) makes each nucleus a single T⁶ mode at the
   A-th harmonic of the proton.  Again, no inter-nucleon
   force is needed.  The deuteron is not a proton and
   neutron exchanging pions — it is one oscillation.

What quarks explain that T⁶ does not yet address:

- **Deep inelastic scattering**: point-like substructure
  inside protons, seen in high-energy electron-proton
  collisions.  This is above the T⁶ predictive horizon
  (~2 GeV) and may require understanding the internal
  structure of the standing wave — how the energy is
  distributed within the compact dimensions.

- **Jets**: collimated sprays of hadrons in high-energy
  collisions, explained in QCD by quark/gluon production
  and fragmentation.  Again above the predictive horizon.

- **Asymptotic freedom**: the weakening of the strong
  coupling at short distances.  T⁶ has no analog of a
  running coupling constant.


### 14.2 Free parameters

The most dramatic difference between the two frameworks
is the number of free parameters.

**Standard Model: ~25 free parameters**

| Category | Parameters | Count |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Quark masses | m_u, m_d, m_s, m_c, m_b, m_t | 6 |
| Lepton masses | m_e, m_μ, m_τ | 3 |
| Neutrino masses | Δm²₂₁, Δm²₃₁, m_lightest | 3 |
| CKM matrix | 3 angles + 1 CP phase | 4 |
| PMNS matrix | 3 angles + 1 CP phase | 4 |
| Gauge couplings | α, α_s, sin²θ_W | 3 |
| Higgs sector | v (or m_H), λ | 2 |
| QCD vacuum | θ_QCD | 1 |
| **Total** | | **~26** |

Some of these are known to high precision (α to 12 digits),
others poorly (θ_QCD consistent with zero, absolute neutrino
mass unknown).  All are empirical inputs — the Standard
Model provides no mechanism to derive any of them.

**T⁶ model: 3 inputs + 3 unconstrained**

| Parameter | Status | How determined |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| m_e | Input | Sets electron T² scale |
| m_ν (Δm²₂₁) | Input | Sets neutrino T² scale |
| m_p | Input | Sets proton T² scale |
| α = 1/137 | Input | Determines shear s₁₂ for any r_e |
| r_p = 8.906 | Derived | Pinned by neutron + muon masses |
| σ_ep = −0.091 | Derived | Pinned by m_n − m_p |
| s₃₄ = 0.022 | Derived | Pinned by Δm²₃₁/Δm²₂₁ |
| r_e | Free | Invisible to MeV-scale observables |
| r_ν | Weakly constrained | r_ν ≳ 5 from mass convergence |
| σ_eν, σ_νp | Irrelevant | No measurable effect |

The three input masses (m_e, m_ν, m_p) are analogous to
the SM lepton masses — they set scales.  α is the one
genuinely unexplained input.  After these 4 inputs, the
model derives r_p, σ_ep, and s₃₄ from other measured
particles (neutron, muon, neutrino mass ratio).  Every
remaining prediction — kaon, eta, eta-prime, phi, nuclear
masses — uses zero additional parameters.

The two free parameters (r_e, r_ν) are invisible: r_e
does not affect any observable we can currently compute,
and r_ν enters only through neutrino absolute masses
(not yet precisely measured).

**Net comparison:**

| | Standard Model | T⁶ |
|---|---|---|
| Inputs to set scales | ~9 masses | 3 masses |
| Coupling constants | 3 (α, α_s, sin²θ_W) | 1 (α) |
| Mixing parameters | 8 (CKM + PMNS) | 0 |
| Other | ~6 (Higgs, θ_QCD, ...) | 0 |
| Truly free | ~25 | 2 (r_e, r_ν) |

The T⁶ model covers a narrower domain (particles below
~2 GeV, nuclei, atoms) but does so with dramatically fewer
inputs.  The Standard Model covers a wider domain (all
known particles, all forces, precision QED/QCD) but
requires ~25 empirical parameters to do so.


### 14.3 What T⁶ explains that the Standard Model does not

Several features that are unexplained inputs or
"fine-tuned" coincidences in the Standard Model have
geometric explanations in T⁶:

- **Why m_n > m_p**: In the SM, this depends on the
  up-down quark mass difference (m_d − m_u ≈ 2.5 MeV)
  partially offset by electromagnetic self-energy — both
  free parameters.  In T⁶, the neutron is a cross-sheet
  mode that necessarily has more energy than the single-
  sheet proton mode.

- **Why exactly 3 stable particles**: The SM has no
  explanation for why only e, p, ν are stable.  In T⁶,
  each is the lightest mode on its own T² sheet — there
  is nothing lighter to decay into.

- **Why the neutrino is so light**: In the SM, neutrino
  masses require either a seesaw mechanism or ad hoc
  Yukawa couplings 10⁶× smaller than the electron's.
  In T⁶, the neutrino lives on a separate, larger T²
  — light because big.

- **Neutron decay products**: The SM requires the weak
  force (W boson exchange) to explain n → p + e⁻ + ν̄.
  In T⁶, the cross-sheet neutron mode unravels into the
  three constituent sheets — p, e, ν — which ARE the
  decay products.

- **Nuclear binding without a separate force**: The SM
  requires QCD (quarks + gluons) to explain why nuclei
  hold together.  In T⁶, nuclei are single modes — no
  binding force is needed.


### 14.4 What the Standard Model explains that T⁶ does not

- **Electroweak unification**: the relationship between
  electromagnetism and the weak force, W/Z/Higgs masses,
  and the Weinberg angle.  T⁶ has no analog above ~2 GeV.

- **QCD**: asymptotic freedom, confinement, jet production,
  deep inelastic scattering, lattice QCD calculations.
  T⁶ has no strong coupling or color charge.

- **Precision QED**: anomalous magnetic moment (g − 2) to
  12 digits, Lamb shift, hyperfine splitting.  T⁶
  reproduces g = 2 at leading order only.

- **CP violation**: matter-antimatter asymmetry, kaon
  oscillations, B-meson physics.  T⁶ has no CP-violating
  mechanism.

- **High-energy collider physics**: top quark, Higgs
  production, diboson processes.  Above the T⁶ predictive
  horizon.

The two frameworks are not competitors across their full
domains.  They overlap in the 0.1–2 GeV range where both
make predictions.  Below ~0.1 GeV (atomic physics), both
use the same Coulomb potential.  Above ~2 GeV, only the
Standard Model currently applies.  In the overlap region,
the T⁶ makes parameter-free predictions that the SM
requires ~20 inputs to match.


## 15. Open: what remains

### 15.1 The r_e problem
The electron aspect ratio remains unconstrained.  Nuclear
masses are insensitive to it (electron dimensions contribute
energy at the keV scale, 10⁴–10⁵× smaller than the proton-
sheet contributions).  It must be pinned by electron-scale
observables — possibly through the KK coupling structure
or through the shear-charge formula.

### 15.2 The 8 MeV/A residual
Heavy nuclei show a systematic residual of ~8 MeV per
nucleon between the T⁶ mode energy and the observed mass.
The deuteron is captured to 86%, but by iron-56 only ~8%
of the binding energy is accounted for.  This growing gap
likely represents R³ spatial effects (Coulomb repulsion
between protons, surface/volume energy terms) that the
T⁶-only calculation misses.

### 15.3 Ghost nuclear modes
Does the T⁶ spectrum contain nuclear modes with no observed
counterpart?  An island of T⁶ stability that contradicts
the experimental valley of stability would be a serious
problem for the model.
