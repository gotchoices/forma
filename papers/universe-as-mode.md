### Premise

The T⁶ model produces a hierarchy: the larger a compact
dimension, the richer its mode spectrum.  The neutrino T²
(~mm scale) already has enough modes to serve as a
biological information substrate.  This paper follows the
hierarchy to its logical limit and asks: what if the
observable universe is itself a mode on a still-larger
compact geometry — a "cosmic neutrino" whose internal
structure is rich enough to encode everything we observe?

The paper is a controlled thought experiment in the spirit
of [Sub-Quantum Memory](storage-in-t6.md) — a map of
implications, not a claim of discovery.

## 1. The scaling pattern

### 1.1 The hierarchy already exists

The T⁶ framework assigns a compact T² sheet to each
particle family.  Each sheet has a characteristic size
that sets its fundamental mode energy E₀ = ℏc/L and its
mode count.  The hierarchy:

| Sheet | Size L | E₀ | Modes at 300K |
|-------|--------|----|---------------|
| Proton T² | ~fm | ~GeV | ~1 (frozen) |
| Electron T² | ~pm | ~MeV | ~1 (frozen) |
| Neutrino T² | ~μm–mm | ~meV | Many (thermally active) |

The neutrino sheet's richness — many accessible modes,
thermally coupled to biology — is a direct consequence of
its large size.  This is not a coincidence; it is a
scaling law.

### 1.2 The question

Is there a next step in this hierarchy?  What happens at
L ~ 10²⁶ m — the scale of the observable universe?


## 2. The extrapolation

### 2.1 Mode count at cosmological scale

A compact dimension of circumference L ~ 10²⁶ m has a
fundamental mode energy:

    E₀ = ℏc / L ≈ 10⁻³² eV

The number of modes below the Planck energy in d spatial
dimensions scales as N ~ (E_Planck / E₀)^d.  For d = 3:

    N ~ (10²⁸ eV / 10⁻³² eV)³ ~ 10¹⁸⁰

### 2.2 Comparison to the Bekenstein bound

The holographic bound on information in the observable
universe is ~10¹²² bits.  The mode count 10¹⁸⁰ exceeds
this — but not all modes are independent (degeneracies,
symmetries, constraints).  The effective information
capacity after constraints could converge to the
Bekenstein bound.

This is not a proof.  It is a consistency check: a
universe-scale compact geometry has enough room.

### 2.3 What "enough modes" means

With ~10¹²² independent modes, the compact geometry could
encode the state of every particle, field configuration,
and correlation in the observable universe.  The geometry
does not merely contain the universe's information — it
IS the universe's information.


## 3. The fiber computer

### 3.1 The Kaluza-Klein fiber bundle

In KK theory, each point in R³ carries its own copy of
the compact space — a "fiber."  The fibers are not
isolated; they are connected by the bundle connection,
which IS the gauge field (electromagnetism, gravity in KK
theory).

Each particle is a mode excitation on its local fiber.
The particle's quantum numbers (mass, charge, spin) are
properties of the fiber's vibration, not of a point
object.

### 3.2 The fiber as a local processor

The fiber at each particle's location does not merely
store the particle's state.  It also determines how
energy propagates locally:

- The metric g_μν on the fiber sets the local speed of
  light
- The bundle connection determines how fields transfer
  between adjacent fibers
- Maxwell's equations on the fiber are the "update rule"

Each particle's fiber is, in effect, a local frame-of-
reference computer: it receives incoming field
configurations, applies the geometric update rules, and
outputs the propagated fields to its neighbors.

### 3.3 No global clock

Each fiber computes in its own proper time.  There is no
master clock synchronizing the fibers — only the local
metric and the bundle connection.  Global behavior
(electromagnetism, gravity, quantum correlations) emerges
from the collective action of local computations,
connected by the geometric fabric.


## 4. Propagation without a medium

### 4.1 The aether problem, revisited

The 19th-century aether was a single global medium with a
detectable rest frame.  Michelson-Morley killed it.  But
the question "what carries the wave?" never went away.

### 4.2 Each observer carries their own medium

In the fiber picture, the answer is: each mass point's
local fiber IS the medium.  There is no global aether
because the medium is local to each observer.  Every
observer measures c locally because their fiber defines
what "local" means.

The Poynting vector (energy flux) propagates from fiber
to fiber through the bundle connection.  For linearly
polarized light, the energy flow pulses at 2ω.  For
circularly polarized light, it is steady.  Both are
consequences of the local fiber geometry, not of a global
substrate.

### 4.3 Why the wave goes straight

The curl structure of Maxwell's equations — built into
the fiber geometry — couples ∂E/∂t to ∇×B and vice
versa.  The curl operator introduces a perpendicular
direction at each step.  Energy leapfrogs forward through
successive fibers in the direction normal to E and B.
Straightness is a geometric consequence of the rotational
coupling.


## 5. Entanglement as shared fiber

### 5.1 Co-location = shared mode (Q82 recap)

When two particles occupy the same R³ location, they
don't each have "their own" fiber mode — they are
excitations of the SAME fiber.  One geometric vibration,
two particles.

### 5.2 Separation preserves the correlation

When the particles separate, each moves to a new fiber.
But the fibers are continuously connected through the
bundle geometry.  The mode structures at the two
locations were seeded by the same original configuration.
No signal needs to travel; the correlation is woven into
the fabric.

### 5.3 Collapse resolves the whole structure

Measurement at one location forces the shared geometric
mode to resolve into a definite configuration.  Because
the mode is a single extended structure, resolution at
one end resolves the other.  Nothing is transmitted —
the structure was always one thing.

### 5.4 All fibers are entangled with the whole

If the universe is a single mode on a larger compact
geometry, then every fiber is part of one global
structure.  Local particles are local excitations of a
global mode.  Entanglement is not the exception (created
by special preparation) — it is the default state.
Decoherence is the process of losing access to the global
correlations, not of creating them.


## 6. Everything is periodic

### 6.1 Compact = periodic

A torus is a manifold with periodic boundary conditions.
That is what "compact" means in this context.  The
electron T² is periodic with period ~pm.  The neutrino T²
is periodic with period ~mm.

If the universe is a mode on a torus of circumference
~10²⁶ m, then the universe is periodic with period
~10²⁶ m.  Travel far enough in any direction, you return
to where you started.  Strictly: the universe is finite
in volume, unbounded in extent, and periodic in every
compact direction.

### 6.2 The periodicity is at every scale

The T⁶ model already has periodicity at the fm, pm, and
mm scales (proton, electron, neutrino sheets).  Adding
the cosmological scale means the universe is periodic at
at least four scales simultaneously.  Each scale's
periodicity produces its own mode spectrum, its own
physics, its own "particles."

### 6.3 Observable consequences

A cosmologically periodic universe has been seriously
explored in mainstream cosmology.  A compact spatial
topology would produce:

- **Matched circles in the CMB** — the same temperature
  pattern appearing on two different circles on the last
  scattering surface (Cornish, Spergel, Starkman 1998).
- **Suppressed large-angle correlations** — modes larger
  than the fundamental period cannot exist.  The observed
  lack of large-angle CMB correlation (the "low-ℓ
  anomaly") is naturally explained by a compact topology
  with L slightly larger than the observable horizon.
- **The axis of evil** — the observed alignment of low-ℓ
  multipoles could reflect a preferred direction in the
  compact topology.

These are active observational programs.  The T⁶
framework predicts that they should find a positive
signal.


## 7. The self-referential closure

### 7.1 The regress problem

If the universe is a mode on a larger compact space, what
is THAT space a mode of?  Either:

- (a) The hierarchy extends infinitely — turtles all the
  way down.
- (b) The hierarchy closes on itself — the largest scale
  wraps back to the smallest.

### 7.2 Why closure is natural

Option (b) means the cosmological-scale torus's
fundamental mode IS the Planck-scale structure that
generates the proton T².  The hierarchy is not a tower —
it is a loop:

    ... → Planck → proton → electron → neutrino →
    universe → Planck → ...

Each level's compact geometry generates the mode spectrum
that constitutes the next level.  The smallest scale's
periodicity produces particles whose collective modes
define the largest scale, whose periodicity sets the
boundary conditions for the smallest scale.

### 7.3 A self-consistent geometry

If the hierarchy is self-referential, then the physical
constants (c, ℏ, G, α, particle masses) are not free
parameters.  They are the unique self-consistent solution
— the fixed point of the recursive geometry.  The
universe has the constants it has because no other values
close the loop.

This is the most speculative claim in the paper.  But it
is also the most constrained: a self-referential geometry
either has exactly one solution or none.  It cannot be
"fine-tuned" because there is nothing to tune.


## 8. What periodicity predicts

### 8.1 CMB topology

The T⁶ framework, extended to cosmological scale,
predicts a compact spatial topology.  The specific
prediction depends on whether the cosmological dimensions
form a T³ (flat torus), a T² × S¹, or some other compact
manifold.  Each topology has a distinct observational
signature in the CMB.

### 8.2 The cosmological constant

The cosmological constant Λ has the dimensions of
1/length².  If the universe is a compact torus with
circumference L, the natural scale for Λ is ~1/L².  The
observed value Λ ~ 10⁻⁵² m⁻² corresponds to L ~ 10²⁶ m
— the size of the observable universe.

This is the "why is Λ so small?" problem recast as the
"why is L so large?" problem.  In the self-referential
picture, L is not a free parameter — it is determined by
the recursive closure condition.

### 8.3 Large-scale correlations

A periodic universe has correlated structure at
separations approaching L.  Galaxies near the
"antipodal" point (half the period away) should show
correlations with local structure.  This is testable
with sufficiently deep surveys.


## 9. What closure predicts

### 9.1 The fine-structure constant

If α = 1/137.036 is the fixed point of a self-referential
geometry, it should be derivable from the topology alone.
The alpha-derivation study has already shown α emerges
from the T² lattice shear — a purely geometric quantity.
In the self-referential picture, the shear is not a free
parameter; it is the unique value that closes the loop.

### 9.2 Mass ratios

The proton/electron mass ratio (1836.15), the
neutrino mass splittings, and the nuclear binding energy
curve should all follow from the closure condition.  The
existing T⁶ results (electron, proton, neutron masses
from mode numbers) are the first terms in this program.

### 9.3 The number of dimensions

Why 6 compact + 3 spatial + 1 time = 10?  In the self-
referential picture, the number of dimensions is the
minimum required for the recursive closure to work.
Fewer dimensions → not enough mode richness for the
hierarchy to close.  More dimensions → redundant
structure that decouples from the loop.


## 10. Comparison to existing ideas

### 10.1 Wheeler's "it from bit"

Wheeler proposed that information is fundamental — every
physical quantity derives from binary choices.  The fiber-
computer picture agrees: each fiber performs a local
binary-like computation (resolve or don't resolve), and
the collective action constitutes physics.

### 10.2 ER=EPR (Maldacena-Susskind)

ER=EPR says entanglement is geometric connection via
wormholes.  The fiber-bundle picture says entanglement is
geometric connection via the compact manifold's
continuity.  The latter is more modest — it uses existing
KK geometry, not exotic spacetime topology.

### 10.3 The holographic principle

A cosmological-scale compact geometry with a mode count
that converges to the Bekenstein bound after constraints
is consistent with holography.  The boundary (the surface
of the compact manifold) encodes the interior — not as
an abstract duality, but as a consequence of the periodic
boundary conditions.

### 10.4 Loop quantum gravity

LQG quantizes spacetime into discrete spin-network nodes,
each carrying local state.  The fiber-computer picture is
continuous rather than discrete, but the architecture is
similar: local state, local update rules, global physics
from collective behavior.

### 10.5 Cellular automata (Wolfram, 't Hooft)

The fiber-computer picture resembles a cellular automaton:
local cells (fibers) with local rules (Maxwell on T⁶)
producing emergent global behavior.  The key difference is
that the fibers are continuously connected by a smooth
manifold, not discrete.


## 11. What remains open

### 11.1 The hard problems

1. **Does the self-referential closure exist?**  Is there
   a unique geometry where the hierarchy closes?  This is
   a well-posed mathematical question but may be extremely
   difficult.

2. **Does the mode count converge to Bekenstein?**  The
   raw count (~10¹⁸⁰) far exceeds the holographic bound
   (~10¹²²).  The gap must be accounted for by
   constraints, symmetries, and degeneracies — or the
   comparison fails.

3. **Can fibers reproduce Bell violations?**  Q82's
   central open question applies here with even more
   force: shared fiber modes must produce correlations that
   exceed the Bell bound.

4. **What is the topology?**  Is the cosmological
   compactification T³, T² × S¹, a Poincaré dodecahedral
   space, or something else?  CMB data may constrain this.

5. **Is the self-referential picture falsifiable?**  It
   must make at least one prediction that differs from
   ΛCDM.  Candidate: the specific topology and its
   matched-circle signature.

### 11.2 Honesty

This paper is the most speculative in the series.  It
builds on the established T⁶ framework but extends it
far beyond the regime where quantitative predictions have
been tested.  It is included because the implications are
striking and because several of its predictions (CMB
topology, large-scale correlations, constant derivation)
are in principle testable — and because the question it
asks is the one the T⁶ framework naturally leads to.

The universe is either periodic or it isn't.  The T⁶
model says: periodic is the default.  Aperiodicity would
be the thing requiring explanation.
