### 1. Premise

- Williamson & van der Mark (1997): electron as a single photon
  confined to a (1,2) torus knot.  Reproduces spin ½ (exact,
  topological) and charge ≈ 0.91e (approximate).
- Natural units reduce all of physics to two independent
  quantities: energy and length.  The model takes this literally —
  particles are energy (photons) confined in geometry (compact
  periodic dimensions).
- Axioms: (1) energy exists, (2) compact dimensions exist,
  (3) Maxwell's equations govern propagation.  Everything else
  — mass, charge, spin, the particle zoo — should follow.

### 2. Confinement and compact space

- The confinement problem: what traps a photon?  Not gravity
  (10²² too weak, R1), not nonlinear EM (no solitons in linear
  Maxwell).  Answer: topology.  A photon on a closed surface
  cannot escape — confinement is exact.
- The compact space is a flat torus T² (two periodic dimensions).
  The photon propagates through flat space internally; its fields
  project into 3D through the toroidal embedding (R12).
- T³ (three periodic dimensions) is required for topological
  linking of multiple photons — needed for hadrons (R14).

### 3. The electron

- The (1,2) mode on sheared T² is the electron.  Properties:
  - Mass: energy of one standing wave, E = m_e c²
  - Spin ½: topological, from the 1:2 winding ratio
  - Charge e: shear breaks toroidal symmetry, making the
    charge integral nonzero for the first time (R19)
  - g ≈ 2: spin-1 photon → spin-½ fermion
- Spin quantization (only integer and half-integer spins are
  physical) combined with charge quantization (only the (1,2)
  mode gives Q = e) makes the electron the lightest charged
  fermion.  No lighter charged spin-½ particle can exist on
  T² — protected by topology, not by assumption (R14 F10).

### 4. The proton and neutron

- The proton = electron's (1,2) fundamental + uncharged (n,2n)
  harmonics.  Harmonics add mass without charge (R20 F1).
  Multiple convergent series reach m_p = 1836 m_e.
- The neutron = two opposite-charge fundamentals (+e and −e)
  + harmonics.  Charge cancels exactly.
- Beta decay: n → p + e⁻ + ν̄.  The electron fundamental
  escapes; excess harmonic energy (0.782 MeV) becomes KE of
  decay products.  Endpoint energy matches experiment (R20 F9).
- Proton stability: one fundamental, nothing to annihilate.
  Neutron instability: charged pair can separate.  Muon/tau:
  same (1,2) fundamental + harmonics, decay by "harmonic
  evaporation" (R20 F10, F17).

### 5. The energy gap and quantization

- The compact cavity has a hard energy floor: no non-trivial
  mode below 245 keV on the electron's T².  Between zero
  (photon) and 245 keV (lightest KK mode), the spectrum is
  empty.  This gap is exact — forced by periodic boundary
  conditions on a finite surface.
- Below the gap: photons propagate classically in 3D.  The
  compact dimensions are invisible.  Maxwell's equations are
  exact, not approximate.
- Above the gap: compact modes can be excited.  Interactions
  become discrete.  Energy quantization, pair creation
  thresholds, and the stability of matter all follow from the
  cavity spectrum — derived from geometry, not postulated.
- The quantum-classical boundary is the KK energy gap.

### 6. The neutrino problem

- Every uncharged mode on the electron's T² has rest mass
  ≥ 245 keV.  The neutrino's rest mass is < 0.8 eV — a gap
  of 10⁵.  The neutrino cannot be a standard eigenmode on this
  T² (R20 F14, F20, F21).
- A separate, larger T² works but explains nothing.  The
  neutrino should be emergent from the same geometry.
- Promising directions: collective excitations of the harmonic
  condensate (phonon-like quasi-particles with sub-eV mass);
  near-degeneracy beats between high modes (natural oscillation
  structure matching neutrino phenomenology).
- The neutrino lives in the gap — not a cavity eigenmode, but
  a qualitatively different excitation of the same geometry.

### 7. Curved eigenmodes and the path forward

- The embedded torus has position-dependent curvature.  Solving
  the wave equation on the curved surface (not flat T²) gives
  modes that localize at the outer equator and become standing
  waves — qualitatively different from flat-space plane waves
  (R21 F1–F3).
- Curvature modifies what shear produces: different modes carry
  different charges from the same geometry.  The n₁ = 0 ground
  state, exactly uncharged on flat T², acquires charge on the
  curved surface (R21 F7).
- The full shear-curvature coupling (not yet computed) could
  provide enough constraints to fix all free parameters —
  deriving α from the requirement that electrons and quarks
  coexist on the same torus.

### 8. What is derived vs. what remains open

**Derived from topology (no measurements needed):**
- Electron winding (1,2) = ground state of fermion sector
- Spin ½, charge e, g ≈ 2
- Lightest charged fermion (spin + charge filters)
- Proton/neutron structure and stability
- Beta decay energetics
- Energy quantization and the quantum-classical boundary

**Open — the α problem:**
- What geometric principle determines the shear s₁₂ (= α)?
- What fixes the aspect ratio r?
- Once these are derived, m_e follows — and the model goes
  from descriptive to predictive.

### 9. Ephemeral particles

- All particles beyond e, p, n are transient configurations of
  harmonic energy on T².  Muon/tau = "hot electrons" that cool
  by shedding harmonics.  Pions, kaons, etc. = unstable harmonic
  excitations.  They decay because lower-energy configurations
  exist; only the electron (lightest charged fermion) and the
  proton (lightest charged baryon — no lighter +e state) are
  stable endpoints.
- The particle zoo is not a zoo of fundamentals — it is a
  spectrum of excitations on a single compact geometry, with
  only one truly stable fermion (the electron) and one truly
  stable baryon (the proton).
