# Sub-Quantum Memory: Information Storage in Compact Dimensions

A speculative paper exploring what follows if the T⁶ compact
geometry model and threshold absorption are both correct.

**Status:** outline stage (2-level)

---

## Abstract

What if the extra dimensions of spacetime can store information?

Two developing theoretical frameworks — each speculative on its
own — combine to imply something neither predicts alone.  The
T⁶ compact geometry model proposes that fundamental particles
are standing waves of light confined on a six-dimensional torus,
where the particle's mass is simply energy trapped in the
geometry.  Reiter's threshold model proposes that energy
absorption is continuous, not quantized: systems accumulate
energy smoothly until a threshold triggers a discrete emission
event.  If both contain elements of truth, their union implies
that the compact dimensions of spacetime could function as an
analog information storage medium — one that is frequency-
addressable, inherently noise-resistant, and invisible to
standard quantum measurements.

This paper develops that idea in two layers.  Part I builds the
physics case in the abstract: how energy could be written into
compact-dimension modes, how it would be retained, and how it
might be read back.  These mechanisms could, in principle, apply
to future synthetic technology.

Part II asks whether nature got there first.  Three numerical
coincidences link the model to biology: the neutrino T²
subplane has circumferences matching the scale of a biological
cell (40–200 μm); its mode energies (6–90 meV) overlap the
voltage range of cell membranes (10–90 mV → 10–90 meV per
charge); and its mode frequencies fall in the terahertz band
where DNA exhibits collective vibrations.  These coincidences
converge on the experimental program of Michael Levin, whose
laboratory has demonstrated that voltage patterns across cell
membranes store morphogenetic information — including memories
that survive decapitation and head regeneration in flatworms.
Levin's deepest unsolved problem is where the target morphology
is stored.  Compact-dimension modes offer a candidate answer.

The paper is a controlled thought experiment — a map of
implications, not a claim of discovery.  It identifies a
specific, falsifiable chain: if T⁶ geometry is real, and
threshold absorption is real, and the neutrino T² scale matches
the cellular scale, then biological morphogenesis should be
measurably sensitive to perturbations at the specific
frequencies of the neutrino T² mode spectrum.

## Outline

The paper is structured in two layers.  **Part I** (sections
1–8) combines T⁶ with threshold theory to develop the physics
of compact-dimension storage in the abstract — a mechanism that
could in principle be engineered into synthetic technology.
**Part II** (sections 9–14) adds the biological evidence,
presenting three numerical coincidences and Levin's experimental
program to suggest that nature may already exploit this
mechanism.  Each layer stands on its own; readers who reject the
biology can engage with the physics, and vice versa.

### 1. Introduction: two speculative frameworks, one concrete question

#### 1.1 The thought experiment
Frame the paper honestly.  We are not claiming discovery.  We
are asking: if two speculative frameworks each contain a grain
of truth, what follows when they are combined?  The answer turns
out to be surprising and testable.

#### 1.2 A brief orientation for the reader
The paper draws on ideas from extra-dimensional geometry,
wave physics, and cell biology.  None requires advanced
training to follow, but some are unfamiliar.  Promise the reader
that every concept will be introduced with a physical analogy
before any mathematics appears.

#### 1.3 What this paper claims and does not claim
Claims: (a) the combination of T⁶ and threshold theory logically
implies compact-dimension information storage; (b) three
quantitative coincidences connect this storage to the biological
cell; (c) specific, falsifiable predictions follow.  Does not
claim: that either framework is proven, that biology uses compact
dimensions, or that the coupling mechanism has been computed.

#### 1.4 Road map
Preview the two-part structure.  Part I: physics of storage in
the abstract.  Part II: the case that nature may already use it.


### 2. Foundation: the T⁶ model — particles as trapped light

#### 2.1 What are "extra" dimensions?
Introduce compact dimensions with the garden-hose analogy: from
far away a hose looks like a 1D line, but an ant on its surface
lives in 2D — one long dimension and one short, curled-up
dimension.  Spacetime may have additional dimensions curled up
too small for us to see directly, but large enough for light to
resonate inside.

#### 2.2 The torus: a surface that wraps around on itself
Explain a torus (T²) as a rectangle whose opposite edges are
glued — like the screen in the classic video game Asteroids,
where flying off the right edge returns you on the left.  A
six-dimensional torus (T⁶) is three such rectangles stacked.
In the T⁶ model, each of the three stable particles (electron,
neutrino, proton) lives on its own T² subplane.

#### 2.3 Standing waves and the mode spectrum
Explain standing waves: a guitar string vibrates at its
fundamental frequency and at integer multiples (harmonics).  A
torus is similar but in two directions — its allowed vibrations
are labeled by two integers (n₁, n₂), one for each direction
around the torus.  Only frequencies that "fit" an exact number
of wavelengths around each loop are permitted.  This discrete
set of allowed frequencies is the mode spectrum.

#### 2.4 Mass is stored energy
The key insight of the T⁶ model: a particle's mass IS energy
stored in one of these standing-wave modes.  The electron, for
example, is light vibrating in the (1,2) mode of the electron
T².  The energy of that vibration, via E = mc², is exactly the
electron's mass.  Compact dimensions are already proven (within
this model) to store energy.  The question is whether they can
store *additional* energy — and whether that energy encodes
information.

#### 2.5 The three tori and their scales
Summarize the three T² subplanes and their characteristic sizes.
The electron T² is subatomic.  The proton T² is smaller still.
The neutrino T² is vastly larger — with circumferences of
roughly 40 μm and 200 μm.  This last number will become
important in Part II.


### 3. Foundation: threshold theory — energy below the quantum floor

#### 3.1 The standard quantum picture
In textbook quantum mechanics, a system absorbs energy in
discrete packets (quanta).  An atom either absorbs a whole
photon or absorbs nothing.  There is no in-between state.
Analogy: a vending machine that only accepts exact change — drop
in less than the price and nothing happens; the coins fall
through.

#### 3.2 Reiter's alternative: continuous filling, sudden release
Eric Reiter proposes that the vending machine actually has a
cup that fills gradually.  Energy flows in continuously, like
water into a bucket.  When the bucket reaches a threshold, it
tips and triggers a detection event — a "click" in a Geiger
counter, or a photon emission.  The click is discrete; the
filling is not.  Reiter calls the discrete click an illusion
of the detector, not a property of the energy itself.

#### 3.3 The unquantum effect
Briefly describe Reiter's experimental claim: when a gamma ray
is split by a beam-splitter, both halves sometimes trigger
detectors simultaneously — a "coincidence" rate exceeding what
quantum mechanics allows.  The claim is controversial and
unreplicated, but the theoretical implication matters for our
thought experiment.

#### 3.4 Why this matters for storage
If absorption is truly continuous, then a system can hold energy
*below* the quantum threshold.  This sub-quantum energy is
invisible to any detector built on threshold physics — which, in
Reiter's framework, is *every* quantum measurement device.  The
energy is physically real but hidden.  A compact-dimension mode
holding sub-threshold energy would be an invisible reservoir: a
place to store information that no standard measurement can
accidentally erase.


### 4. The synthesis: compact dimensions as analog memory

#### 4.1 Combining the pieces
A compact-dimension cavity (T²) has a discrete mode spectrum
(from T⁶).  Energy deposited into the cavity distributes across
these modes.  If absorption is continuous (from threshold
theory), energy need not arrive in full quanta — it can
accumulate smoothly in any mode.

#### 4.2 The mode distribution as a state vector
Analogy: imagine a piano keyboard where each key can be pressed
with any force, and the keys stay down wherever you leave them.
The configuration — which keys are pressed and how hard — is a
multi-dimensional state.  On a T², each allowed mode is a "key."
The amount of energy in each mode is how hard it is pressed.
The full set of mode energies constitutes a high-dimensional
analog state vector: a memory cell.

#### 4.3 Stability: why the state persists
On a flat T² obeying a linear wave equation, each mode's energy
is independently conserved — the "keys" do not drift on their
own.  This is unlike a guitar string, which loses energy to air.
A compact dimension has no "air" — it is a closed geometry with
nowhere for energy to leak.  The state is exactly stable in the
idealized case.

#### 4.4 Information capacity
Estimate the number of independent modes below some energy
cutoff on each T².  Each mode carries a continuous (analog)
amplitude, so in principle the information content per mode is
limited only by noise.  Even with conservative noise limits, a
single T² cavity has vastly more degrees of freedom than a
single bit.


### 5. Writing: how energy enters a compact mode

#### 5.1 Resonant injection
A vibrating system absorbs energy most efficiently at its
natural frequencies.  Analogy: pushing a child on a swing — push
at the swing's natural rhythm and the amplitude grows; push at
the wrong rhythm and nothing happens.  To write energy into a
specific compact mode, supply energy at that mode's frequency.

#### 5.2 Frequency selectivity and amplitude control
The write operation is inherently frequency-selective: energy at
one frequency fills only the matching mode, leaving others
untouched.  The amount of energy injected controls the
amplitude — making the write operation analog, not digital.
Like turning a dial rather than flipping a switch.

#### 5.3 The coupling channel: from 3+1D into compact dimensions
The open question.  Energy in our familiar 3+1 dimensions must
somehow couple into the compact dimensions.  In the T⁶ model,
particles already demonstrate this coupling — the electron's
electric field extends from its compact T² into 3+1D spacetime
as an evanescent tail (the Coulomb field we measure).  The same
coupling, run in reverse, could carry energy *into* the compact
dimensions.  The mechanism has not been computed in detail.

#### 5.4 Continuous injection under the threshold model
In the standard quantum picture, energy must enter in whole
quanta.  Under the threshold model, energy flows continuously —
allowing arbitrarily fine control of the write amplitude.  This
is the difference between a digital write (0 or 1) and a true
analog write (any value on a continuous scale).


### 6. Retention: how long does stored information last?

#### 6.1 The idealized case: perfect conservation
On an isolated flat T² with a linear wave equation, there is no
mechanism for energy to move between modes.  Each mode's energy
is a conserved quantity.  Information stored in the mode
distribution lasts forever.  This is the theoretical ceiling.

#### 6.2 The realistic case: cross-shear leakage
The T⁶ model is not perfectly flat — the three T² subplanes are
connected by shear parameters that slightly tilt one plane
relative to another.  This cross-shear coupling allows slow
energy transfer between modes on different subplanes (this is
exactly the mechanism that produces particle decays in the T⁶
model — the neutron decays because energy slowly leaks from the
cross-sheet mode to the proton and electron planes).  For
storage, cross-shear coupling sets an information lifetime:
the stored state gradually degrades.

#### 6.3 Mode cascade as error correction
Energy in higher harmonics tends to cascade toward the
fundamental — the lowest-frequency mode — through nonlinear
interactions.  Analogy: pluck a guitar string at a random point
and you excite many harmonics, but the sound quickly "settles"
into the fundamental tone.  In compact dimensions, this cascade
acts as a natural error-correction mechanism: small perturbations
to the mode distribution are self-correcting.  The dominant mode
(the "fundamental tone") is the most robust piece of information
in the cell.

#### 6.4 Thermal noise
The 3+1D environment has thermal energy (~25 meV at room
temperature).  Does this noise randomly excite compact modes and
scramble stored information?  The answer depends on the coupling
strength between 3+1D thermal fluctuations and compact-dimension
modes — a quantity that has not been calculated but could in
principle be bounded by comparing neutrino T² mode spacing to
thermal energy.


### 7. Reading: how to extract information from a compact mode

This is the most difficult problem.  Four candidate mechanisms,
in decreasing order of conventional acceptance.

#### 7.1 Destructive readout
Extract all energy from the cavity and measure its frequency
spectrum.  Analogy: shattering a wine glass to identify the
pitch it was resonating at by analyzing the fragments.  This
works but destroys the stored state.  The information is read
once and lost.

#### 7.2 Quantum non-demolition (QND) measurement
In quantum optics, it is possible to measure the number of
photons in a cavity without absorbing any of them — by passing
a probe beam through the cavity and measuring its phase shift.
The compact-dimension analog would couple a probe to the mode
*number* without exchanging energy.  This preserves the stored
state but requires engineering a probe that interacts with
compact modes without disrupting them.

#### 7.3 Sympathetic resonance
Place an array of test resonators near the cavity, each tuned
to a different frequency.  Analogy: a room full of tuning forks.
Strike an unknown fork behind a curtain, and the matching fork
in the room will start humming in sympathy — revealing the
hidden fork's frequency without touching it.  In compact
dimensions, the evanescent field of a cavity mode extends weakly
into neighboring space.  A test resonator tuned to the same
frequency would couple preferentially, absorbing a tiny amount
of energy or exhibiting a detectable phase shift.

#### 7.4 Sub-quantum extraction: the threshold model's unique prediction
If the threshold model is correct, it may be possible to extract
less than one quantum of energy from a cavity, carrying frequency
information without triggering a discrete state transition.
This is forbidden by standard quantum mechanics — making it a
discriminating test between the two frameworks.  If sub-quantum
readout is observed, it simultaneously confirms the threshold
model and demonstrates compact-dimension information retrieval.


### 8. Implications for synthetic technology

#### 8.1 Properties of compact-dimension memory
Summarize the characteristics developed in sections 4–7:
enormous information density (many modes per cavity, continuous
amplitude per mode), inherent noise immunity (mode cascade toward
fundamental), frequency-addressable (write and read by tuning),
and potentially non-destructive readout (sympathetic resonance
or sub-quantum extraction).

#### 8.2 Comparison with existing technologies
Compare qualitatively to magnetic (HDD), electronic (DRAM,
flash), and optical (holographic) storage.  The unique feature
of compact-dimension memory is that it stores information in the
geometry of spacetime itself — not in any material substrate
that can wear out, demagnetize, or degrade.

#### 8.3 What would be needed to engineer it
Be honest about the gap between theory and practice.  The
coupling mechanism between 3+1D fields and compact-dimension
modes has not been computed.  Without that computation, we cannot
estimate write/read speeds, error rates, or energy costs.  This
section describes a *possibility space*, not a design.  Its
purpose is to motivate the computation.

### PART II — Biology: did nature get there first?

### 9. Three numerical coincidences

Introduce Part II by noting that abstract physics becomes
concrete when the numbers match an existing physical system.
Three independent coincidences point to the same system:
the biological cell.

#### 9.1 Spatial scale: the neutrino T² and the eukaryotic cell
The neutrino T² has circumferences L₃ ≈ 200 μm and L₄ ≈ 40 μm.
Eukaryotic cells have diameters of 10–100 μm.  The compact
geometry where the neutrino lives is the same size as the cell
where biology happens.  Illustrate with a scale diagram.

#### 9.2 Energy scale: mode energies and membrane voltages
The neutrino T² mode energies span 6–90 meV.  Cell membrane
resting potentials (Vmem) range from −10 to −90 mV.  The energy
gained by one elementary charge (such as a sodium or potassium
ion) crossing that voltage is eVmem = 10–90 meV — overlapping
the neutrino mode spectrum almost exactly.  This means the
voltage differences that Levin measures across cell membranes
are energetically matched to the standing-wave modes of the
compact dimensions at the cellular scale.

#### 9.3 Frequency scale: DNA vibrations and compact-mode resonances
DNA exhibits coherent, delocalized phonon-like vibrations at
terahertz (THz) frequencies — collective modes involving
hydrogen bonds between base pairs, measured by ultrafast
spectroscopy (Gonzalez-Jimenez et al., Nature Communications
2016).  These vibrations span 0.06–10 THz (0.25–40 meV).  The
neutrino T² mode frequency range overlaps this band.  If compact
dimensions exist at this scale, DNA's collective vibrations are
at the right frequency to couple resonantly to them.

#### 9.4 The compound improbability
Any one coincidence would be unremarkable.  Three independent
coincidences — spatial, energetic, and spectral — all pointing
to the same compact-dimension geometry and the same biological
structure, are collectively harder to dismiss.  They do not
prove coupling.  They motivate looking.


### 10. Levin's bioelectric field: distributed memory without a brain

Introduce Michael Levin's experimental program at Tufts
University as the empirical anchor for Part II.  Levin does
not invoke extra dimensions; his work is entirely within
conventional biology.  But his findings describe phenomena that
our model offers to explain.

#### 10.1 The bioelectric code
All cells — not just neurons — maintain voltage potentials
across their membranes via ion channels and pumps.  These
voltages form spatial patterns across tissues.  Levin's group
has shown that these patterns encode morphogenetic information:
they tell cells what to build, where, and when to stop.

#### 10.2 The code is substrate-independent
A native proton pump in a frog embryo can be replaced by an
unrelated pump from yeast, and as long as the replacement
achieves the same membrane voltage, the same morphological
outcome results (Adams et al. 2007).  The information is in the
voltage value, not in the specific protein.  Analogy: a radio
signal carries the same music regardless of whether the
transmitter is made of vacuum tubes or transistors.

#### 10.3 Memory without a brain
Planarian flatworms trained to recognize a textured environment
retain that memory after decapitation and complete head
regeneration (Shomrat & Levin, J Exp Biol 2013).  The new brain
recovers the old memory.  The information was stored somewhere
in the body — but where?  Conventional candidates (epigenetic
marks, protein gradients) cannot easily account for spatial
memories that survive the turnover of the very cells that held
them.

#### 10.4 Voltage overrides genome
Altering the bioelectric voltage pattern across a planarian's
tissue causes it to grow a head shaped like a different species —
despite having the original species' genome (Emmons-Bell et al.
2015).  The voltage pattern, not the DNA, determines the shape.

#### 10.5 Trophic memory: information that outlasts tissue
Deer shed and fully regrow their antlers each year.  Damage at
one point in the branched structure causes an ectopic branch at
the same point in subsequent years — spatial memory in scalp
cells persisting for months across complete tissue turnover
(Bubenik & Pavlansky 1965).  Whatever stores this memory must
outlast every molecular pathway in the scalp tissue.

#### 10.6 Xenobots: morphogenesis without a blueprint
Frog embryonic skin cells, removed from the embryo and placed
in a dish, self-assemble into novel living organisms — xenobots
— with behaviors never specified by the frog genome (Kriegman
et al. 2020; Blackiston et al., Nature 2025).  They move with
apparent purpose, form communities, and respond to stimuli.  537
genes are uniquely upregulated compared to normal embryos.  The
information for the novel form does not come from DNA.

#### 10.7 The stop condition: Levin's deepest unsolved problem
Regeneration is a closed-loop process: cells build until the
current shape matches a stored target, then they stop.  Levin
calls this target the "morphogenetic setpoint."  Its existence
is demonstrated by the phenomena above, but its physical storage
medium has not been identified.  This is the gap our model
addresses.


### 11. The bioelectric code as a cavity resonance

Draw the structural parallel.  Levin's findings, reinterpreted
through the compact-dimension lens, look like the behavior of
coupled resonant cavities.

#### 11.1 Substrate independence ↔ mode-level information
Levin: "the bioelectric code signals by virtue of its
physiological state, with no 1:1 correspondence to any specific
gene product."  Compact-dimension analog: the mode distribution
is the information carrier.  Which ions produced the membrane
voltage is irrelevant — just as the mode frequency of a drum
does not depend on whether you struck it with a wood or rubber
mallet.

#### 11.2 Isopotential cell fields ↔ coherent cavities
Levin observes that gap junctions divide tissues into
"isopotential cell fields" — regions where all cells share the
same voltage, separated by isolation zones.  In the T⁶ model,
each such region would correspond to a coherent compact-dimension
cavity.  The gap junctions are the coupling channels between
adjacent cavities, and the isolation zones are boundaries where
the mode pattern changes.

#### 11.3 Simple trigger, complex outcome ↔ mode cascade
Levin: a simple bioelectric signal ("build an eye here") triggers
complex, self-limiting morphogenesis — the output contains far
more information than the trigger.  T⁶ analog: the trigger
excites a single compact mode.  The energy cascades to the
fundamental, organizing the full mode distribution.  Analogy: a
single spark does not contain the pattern of a crystal, but it
triggers crystallization from a supersaturated solution — the
pattern is in the medium.


### 12. A geometric answer to the stop condition

The speculative core of the paper.  This section makes the
explicit proposal and shows what it explains.

#### 12.1 The proposal
The morphogenetic setpoint — the target shape that cells build
toward and stop at — is the mode distribution of the neutrino T²
at the cellular scale.  Each cell sits within a compact-dimension
cavity whose mode pattern encodes the target configuration.  The
voltage gradients Levin measures across cell membranes are the
evanescent tails of these standing waves, projecting from the
compact dimensions into 3+1D spacetime — just as the electron's
Coulomb field is the evanescent tail of its (1,2) mode.  The
stop condition is reached when the 3D tissue configuration is
consistent with the compact-dimension template.

#### 12.2 Explanatory power
Show that the proposal accounts for each of Levin's otherwise-
puzzling findings:

- **Memory survives decapitation** because the compact-dimension
  state is not in the tissue — it is in the geometry of
  spacetime at that location.  Destroying the 3D tissue does not
  destroy the compact-dimension mode pattern.
- **Xenobots build novel forms** because cells freed from the
  embryo's bioelectric constraints explore a different region of
  compact-mode configuration space.  The frog genome does not
  limit them; the available compact-dimension modes do.
- **The bioelectric code is substrate-independent** because the
  compact modes respond to the aggregate voltage, not to
  specific molecular species.
- **Trophic memory persists across tissue turnover** because the
  compact-dimension state has a lifetime set by cross-shear
  coupling (section 6.2), which can far exceed any molecular
  pathway's half-life.

#### 12.3 What the proposal does NOT explain
Be explicit.  It does not explain HOW compact modes couple to
cellular voltage.  It does not predict specific head shapes.  It
does not replace Levin's molecular-level findings — it proposes
an underlying layer beneath them.  The gap-junction networks,
ion channels, and transcriptional responses Levin studies are
the *readout hardware*; the compact dimensions are the
*storage medium*.


### 13. Experimental signatures and falsifiable predictions

Each prediction is labeled by which part of the hypothesis it
tests.

#### 13.1 THz perturbation of regeneration (tests T⁶ + biology link)
Expose regenerating planaria to narrow-band THz radiation at
frequencies matching the neutrino T² mode spectrum.  Prediction:
on-resonance frequencies disrupt or alter regeneration
significantly more than off-resonance frequencies of equal
intensity.

#### 13.2 Preferred membrane voltages (tests energy coincidence)
Survey Levin's published voltage data for preferred Vmem values.
Prediction: if compact-dimension modes set the voltage, Vmem
should cluster near values corresponding to neutrino T² mode
energies (6, 12, 29, 30, 58, 90 meV ↔ −6, −12, −29, −30, −58,
−90 mV), rather than being uniformly distributed.

#### 13.3 Neutrino mass sum in biological spectra (tests T⁶ prediction)
The T⁶ model predicts Σm_ν ≈ 117 meV, a specific number not
yet confirmed by cosmology.  Prediction: this energy should
appear as a feature (absorption line, resonance, or break) in
the THz spectrum of biological tissue — if compact modes couple
to molecular vibrations.

#### 13.4 THz shielding of developing organisms (tests compact-mode coupling)
Raise organisms in environments that strongly attenuate THz
radiation (which normally permeates all Earth environments).
Prediction: if THz fields mediate the 3+1D ↔ compact-dimension
coupling, shielded organisms should exhibit morphogenetic
anomalies — errors in pattern formation or regeneration.

#### 13.5 Sub-quantum information extraction (tests threshold model)
Attempt to extract information from a resonant system using
energy transfers below one quantum.  Prediction: if the
threshold model is correct, partial energy extraction carrying
frequency information is possible; if standard QM is correct, it
is strictly forbidden.  This is a direct test of the threshold
model, independent of the biology.


### 14. Limitations, caveats, and open problems

#### 14.1 Status of the component frameworks
The T⁶ model reproduces particle masses and neutrino mass
splittings but has not been derived from a fundamental action
and is not peer-reviewed.  Threshold theory is based on
experiments that have not been independently replicated.  The
biological connection is the most speculative layer, resting on
numerical coincidences that may be exactly that — coincidences.

#### 14.2 The coupling gap
The central missing calculation: how strongly do 3+1D
electromagnetic fields couple to compact-dimension modes?
Without this number, we cannot estimate write/read speeds, noise
floors, or information lifetimes.  The paper identifies this as
the single most important next step.

#### 14.3 Levin's own framework is sufficient for his data
Levin explains his experimental results using the bioelectric
code — voltage patterns in cell membranes acting as a
computational medium.  His framework does not require extra
dimensions.  Our proposal adds a deeper layer ("where does the
code live physically?") but this question may have a
conventional answer we have not considered.

#### 14.4 The paper's value as a "what if"
The paper is a map of implications, not a claim of discovery.
Its value lies in (a) making the logical chain explicit so each
link can be tested independently; (b) generating falsifiable
predictions that none of the component frameworks produce alone;
and (c) connecting three disparate fields — extra-dimensional
geometry, threshold physics, and developmental biology — in a
way that, if confirmed, would be a profound unification.

#### 14.5 Catalog of needed work
List the specific calculations, simulations, and experiments
required to advance or retire each link in the chain.  Organize
by feasibility: what can be done now with existing data (survey
of Vmem distributions), what requires modest new experiments
(THz perturbation of planaria), and what awaits theoretical
breakthroughs (computing the 3+1D ↔ compact coupling strength).
