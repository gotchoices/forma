## Why this paper matters to R62

The paper's central thesis — *charge and spin are the same concept,
distinguished only by whether the symmetry is external (4D
spacetime) or internal (compact directions)* — is exactly the
strategic framework R62 Program 1 ("Electron from light") operates
in.  It is the most directly relevant published precursor to pool
item **f** (spin from the 2×2 internal torus metric), and one of
the few works explicitly attempting to derive spin geometrically
from KK rather than postulating spinors.

It does not preempt R62 — Horoto–Scholtz arrive at spin via a
different mechanism (Killing-vector structure on internal Euclidean
hypersurfaces with Hopf coordinates on S³), not via holonomy on a
sheared 2-torus — but it is the mandatory reference both for
attribution and for cross-checking any derivation R62 produces for
pool item f.

## Core claims (in order of relevance to MaSt / R62)

### 1. Charge and spin are unified as Killing-vector charges

The unifying statement appears explicitly at the end of Section 5
(p. 17 of the preprint):

> *"Thus charge and spin are similar concepts in that they arise
> from the isometries of space.  What differentiates them is that
> one reflects the symmetry of the internal space while the other
> reflects the isometries of ordinary spacetime."*

This is conceptually parallel to MaSt's quantum-number assignment
table in R62-derivations/README.md (ring → mass, tube → charge,
cross-coupling → spin).  Where MaSt distinguishes by *which
compact direction* on a 2-torus, Horoto–Scholtz distinguish by
*spacetime vs. internal space*.  Both push spin into the
"geometric quantum number" category.

### 2. Both Bosons and Fermions are 1-forms (Killing-vector eigenstates)

Their formal definition: an elementary particle is a 1-form Aᵢμ
that is invariant under a group of isometries generated by a set
of Killing vector fields {ξⱼ} that form a representation of the
Lie algebra of the spacetime / internal-space isometries.

The same equation (their Eq. 168 / 191):

ξⱼᵛ ∂ᵥ Aᵢμ + ∂μ ξⱼᵛ Aᵢᵥ = 0

describes both charge conservation (when the Killing field is in
the internal space) and spin conservation (when the Killing field
is in the 4D rotation subgroup of spacetime).  For internal Killing
fields the action splits into a phase part (charge eigenstate)
and a matrix part (the τᵢ representation matrices) — the
matrix part is what we read off as "spin" in the 4D case.

This is a real unification: spin is *not* a separate object
postulated independently; it is the matrix part of the same
equation that gives charge as the phase part.

### 3. Spin requires an extra dimension; spin-½ requires four

In Section 5 they show:

- **Spin-1** falls out of the 3D Euclidean metric using the three
  rotation Killing vectors ξᵢ = σ εᵢⱼₖ xʲ ∂ᵏ.  Solutions of the
  Killing-eigenvector equation (their Eq. 217–220) are
  superpositions of linear and circular polarizations.  When
  applied to a photon, this *is* the EM 4-potential.
- **Spin-½** requires an extra (4th Euclidean) spatial dimension.
  The required Killing vectors are
  ξᵢ = ½(x⁵∂ᵢ − xᵢ∂₅ + εᵢⱼₖ xʲ∂ᵏ), with the τᵢ matrices being
  the spin-½ representation of su(2).  These Killing vectors live
  on the unit 3-sphere S³, and the natural coordinates are the
  **Hopf coordinates** (β, α, γ) on S³.
- **Spin-½ particles "come in pairs"** (their wording, p. 19):
  the SU(2) spinor representation cannot be made real in 4
  dimensions, so the natural eigenstate ϕ̂_D (their Eq. 227) is a
  4-component object that is the eigenstate of the spin operator
  for a *pair* of spin-½ particles.  This is intriguing for any
  matter/antimatter symmetry attempt — though they don't
  themselves connect it to particle-antiparticle pairs.

### 4. Higgs mechanism falls out automatically

In Section 3.4, they show that the Lagrangian for 5D
gravity-with-cylinder-condition produces, after dimensional
reduction, a 4D theory with a non-minimally-coupled scalar field
whose VEV breaks the 5D symmetry — i.e., the Higgs mechanism
emerges geometrically without separate postulation.  The Higgs
field is identified with the conformal factor of the 4D metric.

This is not directly relevant to R62's current scope but is
worth noting as another "MaSt would predict this" item: if MaSt
ever needs a Higgs-like mechanism to provide rest-frame energy
to an otherwise-massless mode, this is the published precedent
showing it falls out of KK geometry.

### 5. Klein quantization survives, mass-hierarchy problem solved

Section 3.3 / 3.4: They argue that the standard "Klein scale
gives Planck-mass particles" problem of traditional KK is *not* a
flaw of compactification but of an incorrect general-relativity
postulate (assuming gμν = ηmn in absence of gravity).  Dropping
this postulate shifts the predicted mass scale from Mpl to
something physical.  This is structurally similar to the move
MaSt makes (compact dimensions are sized by particle masses, not
by Planck/Klein scaling — see R62's "Klein scale issue" framing
section).  Horoto–Scholtz provide a more rigorous mathematical
justification for this move than MaSt currently does.

### 6. Standard Model in 16D as group manifold

Section 4.2 derives non-Abelian gauge theories by requiring the
internal space to be a *group manifold* (not coset).  They
construct U(1)×U(1) (4.2.1), U(1)×SU(2) (4.2.2), and
U(1)×SU(2)×SU(3) (4.2.3) explicitly.  The full Standard Model is
realized in 16D = 4D spacetime + 12D internal group manifold of
SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1).

MaSt currently uses 6 compact dimensions (3 sheets × 2 each), not
12.  If the Horoto–Scholtz framework is taken seriously, MaSt
would need either (a) to extend its compact dimensions to
match the SM gauge structure, or (b) to argue that MaSt's
compactification is a sub-sector that doesn't see the strong /
weak forces.  This is a long-term consideration, not a Track 7
issue.

## What this paper does *not* do (R62's potential novel contribution)

Despite the strong overlap, several specific moves of R62
Program 1 do not appear in Horoto–Scholtz:

1. **Photon (null trajectory) as the underlying object.** Their
   formalism uses massive test particles throughout.  R62's
   "electron from light" central move — that mass arises from
   compact-direction projection of a 6D null trajectory — is not
   the picture they work in.  Their masses come from the
   Higgs mechanism applied to scalar/spinor fields.
2. **Sheared 2-torus.** Their U(1)×U(1) internal space is
   conformally flat (their Eq. 150).  They do not consider the
   off-diagonal metric entry g₄₅ that MaSt makes central.  All
   of MaSt's "shear-mediated" effects (cross-coupling of charges,
   mass-charge mixing, MaSt's specific (n_t/ε)² + (n_r − s n_t)²
   formula) are absent.
3. **Spin-from-cross-coupling.** Their spin-½ derivation requires
   *Killing vectors that close as so(3)*, which forces the
   internal space to admit an SU(2) sub-isometry — i.e., S³ with
   Hopf coordinates.  A 2-torus has only U(1)×U(1) isometries,
   not SU(2).  So in their framework, spin-½ on a pure 2-torus
   would not arise.  R62's pool item f hypothesis (spin from
   shear-induced holonomy on the 2-torus) is *not* contained in
   their derivation, and would constitute a genuinely different
   mechanism for spin emergence.
4. **MaSt's "parity-of-n_tube" empirical rule.** Their derivation
   has no place for a winding-parity rule because they don't have
   compact tube/ring structure.  R62's program of recovering this
   empirical rule from a Berry-phase / holonomy argument on a
   sheared 2-torus is independent of their work.

## Implications for Track 7 (pool item f, when promoted)

If we proceed with pool item f as currently sketched (spin from
cross-coupling on a sheared 2-torus), the relationship to
Horoto–Scholtz will be:

- **Cite as parallel work.** They derive spin in a different
  geometric setting (S³ with Killing-vector eigenstates); we
  derive spin in a different geometric setting (sheared T² with
  Berry-phase holonomy).  Both are "spin from internal-space
  geometry."  Neither subsumes the other.
- **Use as conceptual support.** Their explicit statement that
  charge and spin are unified as Killing-vector charges of
  different spaces *is the framing R62 should adopt verbatim*.
  This is the cleanest published version of the program-1 thesis.
- **Caution.** If we cannot derive spin from holonomy on a
  sheared 2-torus, an alternative path — adopting their Killing-
  vector approach but with the 2-torus as the internal space —
  would not work in their framework either (T² has only U(1)²
  isometry, not SU(2)).  We would then need to either (a)
  enlarge MaSt's internal space to include an S³ factor (likely
  a major change), or (b) argue that the Berry-phase mechanism
  produces something *equivalent* to a representation of so(3)
  even though the underlying isometry group is U(1)².
- **Possibility worth considering.** If pool item f hits a wall,
  the Horoto–Scholtz approach is a viable backup: take MaSt's
  3 sheets, identify each as harboring an S³-like sub-structure
  (perhaps the "ribbon" geometry from R53-style large-ε limits
  is closer to a 3-sphere than to a flat torus?), and run their
  Killing-vector spin derivation on it.

## Reading order recommendation

For working through the paper, focus on:

1. **Abstract + Section 1 (Introduction)** — standalone summary
   of the thesis.
2. **Section 5 (Spin as spacetime symmetry, p. 17–20)** — the
   directly relevant derivation.  Equations 191, 207–211 are the
   master Killing-vector eigenstate equations.  Equations 215–220
   are the spin-1 derivation.  Equations 224–227 are the spin-½
   derivation on Hopf coordinates.
3. **Section 4.2.1 (U(1)×U(1) Theory, p. 12–13)** — direct
   comparison to R62 derivation-2.  Read for cross-checking
   conventions and signs.
4. **Section 6 (Summary)** — their own statement of what was
   accomplished; useful for crafting attribution language.

Sections 2 and 3 (the metric construction and gravity-from-Weyl-
invariance argument) are background; skim unless time permits.
Section 4.2.2 / 4.2.3 (SU(2) and SU(3)) are out of scope for
R62 Program 1 but worth bookmarking for future MaSt work that
might want to incorporate weak / strong interactions.
