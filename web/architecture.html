GRID & MaSt

Two layers: a lattice substrate, and an architecture of compact dimensions on top of it.

Forma is organized in two stacked layers. The lower layer — GRID — is the substrate: a discrete, minimal lattice that reproduces the laws of electromagnetism and gravity from a handful of axioms. The upper layer — MaSt — is the architecture built on that substrate: six compact dimensions where particles live as standing electromagnetic waves.

The two pieces were developed separately and meet in the middle. MaSt takes Maxwell’s equations and α as inputs; GRID produces Maxwell and a derivation of gravity from simpler structure. Together they attempt a single geometric account of fundamental physics — including, uniquely, quantum behaviour as a natural consequence of compact geometry.

GRID — the lattice substrate

GRID (Geometric Relational Interaction Domain) is a minimal causal lattice at the Planck scale. Every cell carries a phase — a clock hand — and edges between cells carry connection variables. Nothing else exists.

From six axioms, GRID yields:

  • Maxwell’s equations, as the continuum limit of local-phase gauge dynamics on the lattice.
  • Einstein’s field equations and Newton’s G, from the lattice’s information density and energy flow across causal boundaries.
  • An impedance of free space (~377 Ω) that is not imported but measured on the lattice.

Beyond the theory, GRID provides a mechanical model of the spacetime fabric. Simulations of the hexagonal lattice confirm that it propagates directional waves without Maxwell put in by hand, superposes them exactly, and accommodates Schwarzschild geometry all the way to the event horizon.

MaSt — Material · Space · time

MaSt is the architecture that sits on GRID. It proposes that spacetime has six additional compact dimensions — three pairs forming material sheets — on which particles are standing waves of confined electromagnetic energy.

The name says what the ingredients are: Material (Ma), the six compact dimensions; Space (S), the three large spatial dimensions we move through; time (t), the single time dimension. The full arena is Ma × S × t: 6 + 3 + 1 = 10. Add the sub-Planck substrate dimension of GRID and the full object is 11-dimensional.

Each material sheet is a two-dimensional compact surface; its size and shape determine which standing-wave modes fit. Different modes on different sheets produce different particle families: the electron and its cousins on the electron sheet, the proton and hadrons on the proton sheet, the neutrinos on the neutrino sheet.

Quantum behaviour in MaSt is not postulated — it emerges from wave mechanics on compact geometry. Quantized energy levels are standing-wave modes. Uncertainty is the Fourier bandwidth limit. Spin is winding topology.

Why two layers?

The two layers answer different questions, and together they avoid having to postulate either:

LayerWhat it explainsTakes as given
GRID Maxwell’s equations; Einstein’s field equations; vacuum impedance; Newton’s G. Six axioms about a discrete causal lattice; one measured constant (α).
MaSt Particle masses, charges, spins; three generations; nuclear scaling; plausible dark matter. Maxwell’s equations (now derived by GRID); α; a handful of geometric scales.

Historically the two programs were separate. MaSt started with Williamson & van der Mark’s 1997 proposal that an electron is a confined photon, and grew — through six model generations, model-A through model-F — into the current 11-dimensional architecture. GRID started as a sanity-check on what kind of substrate could host such a framework, and to everyone’s surprise it produced both Maxwell and gravity from a minimal axiom set.

Guiding principle

Energy and geometry are the only fundamentals.

Mass, charge, spin, and magnetic moment are all emergent. Conservation laws are topological — and therefore exact. Mass conservation is energy conservation. Charge conservation is winding-number conservation (you can’t smoothly unwrap a path on a closed surface). Spin conservation is geodesic topology.

A note on models

MaSt has evolved through six generations (A → F), each superseding the last. The current model — model-F — is an 11-dimensional architecture with geometric α-coupling, Z₃ confinement on the proton sheet (three quark-like constituents, bound by density-fluctuation cancellation), and per-sheet Dirac–Kähler spin that reproduces the Standard Model taxonomy (lepton / meson / baryon) from sheet-count alone.

Nothing here is final. Several predictions beat their model-E predecessors; others (notably the pion masses) remain stubborn. The project tracks both in the open.
